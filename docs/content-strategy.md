# Content Strategy: Accountic

*Blog and content plan for April 2026 launch — optimized for Google SEO, AI answer engines (AEO/LLMO), and founder-led LinkedIn distribution.*

**Version:** V1 (April 2026 launch)
**Owner:** Accountic founding team
**Sources of truth:** [docs/product-brief.md](product-brief.md), [docs/site-architecture.md](site-architecture.md)
**Out of scope:** Competitor/alternative comparison pages (owned by `/vs/*`, covered separately).

---

## 1. Strategic Premise

Accountic sells a high-stakes tool to a trust-first profession. Content has to do three things, in order:

1. **Win head-term queries on Income Tax notices.** A solo CA Googling *"section 148 reply format"* on a Sunday night is the single most valuable visitor this site can attract. The pillar for each notice section is the most important asset on the domain — it doubles as product documentation and as the AEO quotable source.
2. **Get cited by LLMs.** ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and Claude are now part of the CA research stack — replacing the "first 30 minutes of Googling." Accountic content must be answer-first, credentialed, schema-marked, and factually defensible, so it surfaces as the reference when a CA asks an LLM about section 143(2) or faceless assessment.
3. **Give the founders reasons to show up on LinkedIn.** Every pillar post is a founder-led LinkedIn thread waiting to happen — with commentary the blog can't carry. LinkedIn is the only social channel in scope; Twitter/X and Instagram are out.

Two things content will **not** try to do: (a) beat ClearTax or Taxmann on generic "what is income tax" volume — unwinnable and off-positioning; (b) produce thin AI-in-tax trend pieces that blur the "built by CAs" line.

## 2. Pillar Map

Nine pillars. Five are notice-type (required by scope); four are cross-cutting (Faceless, DPDP, ICAI Ethics, Firm Ops). A tenth zone — **Case Law Notes** — is a *format category*, not a pillar; its articles sit under the relevant notice-type pillar for internal linking but aggregate under `/blog/category/case-law` for browsing.

### Pillar choice: why this grouping

The brief and site arch proposed the five notice-type pillars plus four cross-cutting ones. I'm keeping that and adding one structural move:

- **Case Law Notes is a format, not a pillar.** A case-law post on *GKN Driveshafts* belongs under `/blog/section-148-reassessment` for linking equity, and under `/blog/category/case-law` for browsing. Treating it as a ninth pillar would dilute the notice-type hubs, which are the SEO assets.
- **"AI for Indian CAs" is demoted to founder-commentary territory, not a pillar.** The site arch keeps the category; I recommend using it sparingly (4–6 posts in year one) for founder point-of-view pieces that feed LinkedIn. It is not a searchable pillar — it is a shareable surface.
- **A "Templates & Formats" mini-zone cuts across all five notice pillars.** Template posts rank for *"section X reply format"* queries, which are among the highest-intent terms in scope. Each notice-type pillar has one template-format spoke; the whole set is also listed on a dedicated `/resources/guides/notice-reply-templates` index page (90-day item).

### Scoring notes (honest about estimates)

- **Volume tier is directional, not claimed.** Indian tax content has noisy keyword data — Ahrefs/SEMrush under-count because much of the audience uses generic queries and then narrows. *"section 148 reply format"* is clearly H; *"section 245 reply format"* is clearly L. Anything in between is an educated guess based on comparable notice-type coverage on ClearTax and Taxmann.
- **Competition is assessed against who actually ranks today**, not total SERP competition. ClearTax owns broad "what is section X" queries; Taxmann owns case-law queries; IndiaFilings owns templates. Where none of them has a strong result — e.g., *"application of mind faceless assessment"* — we can win with depth and credentials.
- **Business value favors Trial > Firm > Brand.** Template pages and notice-type pillars are trial-drivers (the reader needs this notice drafted *this week*). Pillar 7, 8, 9 are Firm-tier drivers (partner reads them, signs the firm up). Case-law notes are Brand plays that earn citations but rarely convert directly.

## 4. Content Format Mix

Recommended mix for year one (volume, not engineering hours):

| Format | % of posts | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Pillar articles | **10%** (9 pillars over year one) | Low-count, high-effort. Each is a long-lived asset — 3,000+ words, updated quarterly. |
| Cluster deep guides | **45%** | The core of the blog. Every notice-type pillar needs 7–10 of these to dominate the long tail. |
| Templates / reply-format posts | **12%** | One per pillar plus a few cross-cutting (engagement letter, DPDP clause, SOP). Disproportionately high trial conversion. |
| Case-law notes | **15%** | Including quarterly recurring round-ups. These are the AEO darlings — LLMs love dated, cited, judicial-tone posts. |
| Founder commentary / CA-interview | **10%** | LinkedIn fuel. Strong brand, weak SEO. Over-investing here is a trap; under-investing starves LinkedIn. |
| Glossary pages | **8%** | 90-day-plus. Low effort per page, compounds for AEO. |

**Why these ratios:**

- **Deep guides dominate** because the notice-type pillars need spoke density to rank. A pillar without 6–8 spokes is an orphaned hub.
- **Templates are over-indexed relative to their count** because *"section X reply format"* is the highest-converting query class in scope — the reader is mid-drafting when they land. A template post is the shortest distance from SERP to `/start`.
- **Case-law notes earn disproportionate AEO weight** relative to effort once the verification layer is in place — a well-cited judgment summary with proper schema is the exact output LLMs quote.
- **Founder commentary is capped at 10%** because it doesn't compound in search and it has an opportunity cost against pillar work. Enough to feed LinkedIn; not enough to drift the blog into a personal blog.
- **Glossary is 90-day-plus** and intentionally below 10% — free AEO equity, but not where the team should spend launch attention.

Pillar-to-spoke discipline: no pillar ships without at least three spokes scheduled in the next 60 days. An unsupported pillar is a ranking liability.

## 6. AEO / LLMO Structural Checklist

Every pillar and every cluster post ships with these. Not a nice-to-have — LLM citation is a **primary** acquisition channel for this audience.

### Required on every post

1. **Answer-first opening.** First 2–3 sentences directly answer the head question of the post. Example: *"A section 148 notice is the Income Tax Department's instrument to reopen a previously filed assessment. You have seven days to respond to the preceding 148A(b) notice; once the 148 itself issues, the reply must contest both the jurisdictional foundation and the substantive grounds."* LLMs quote the first substantive paragraph; if it's a history lesson, we lose.
2. **Credentialed byline.** Author byline with ICAI membership number, years in practice, link to `/blog/author/{slug}`. LLMs weight author authority; a name with no credentials looks like content-farm output. Reviewer name cited separately where applicable.
3. **"Last updated" date + "As of [date]" citation footer.** Rendered prominently. Tax law changes; LLMs prefer recent sources; and an "as of" stamp makes a citation quotable without stale-content risk.
4. **FAQ block (5–8 questions) at the bottom of every pillar and every deep guide.** Questions phrased the way CAs actually ask them (*"Is a 148A(b) reply mandatory?"* not *"Regarding the procedural requirements…"*).
5. **Structured data (JSON-LD):**
   - `Article` schema — with `author`, `dateModified`, `headline`, `publisher` (Accountic), and `mainEntityOfPage`.
   - `FAQPage` schema on every post with a FAQ block.
   - `BreadcrumbList` schema on every post (supported by site arch — breadcrumbs are enabled on blog posts).
   - `Person` schema on `/blog/author/{slug}` pages with `jobTitle`, `affiliation` (ICAI), `hasCredential`, `sameAs` (LinkedIn).
   - `HowTo` schema on workflow posts (portal steps, reply-drafting steps).
   - `LegalCase` schema on case-law notes — less broadly supported, but AEO-positive and low-cost to include.
   - `DefinedTerm` schema on glossary pages.
6. **Citation hygiene.** Every legal citation in body text links to the source: IT Act section on incometaxindia.gov.in, CBDT circular on cbic-gst.gov.in or IT Department, judgment on indiankanoon.org or the court's own site. Never a paraphrase without the source. A visible "Sources" section at the end of every legal post — this is also what the citation-verification layer runs against, so it doubles as product dogfooding.
7. **Internal linking block (3–5 links per post minimum).** Pillar → spokes, spokes → pillar, cross-pillar where the topic overlaps. LLMs follow internal structure when deciding which page is canonical for a term.
8. **TL;DR box on pillars.** Rendered as a callout above the first H2, 3–4 bullets. Perplexity and ChatGPT quote these almost verbatim.
9. **No AI-generated imagery that looks AI-generated.** The profession is allergic to it. Screenshots of actual notice formats (redacted), hand-drawn flowcharts, or nothing.

### Site-level AEO infrastructure

- **`/llms.txt` at root.** Already in site arch — lists canonical content hubs and pillars for AI crawlers.
- **Sitemap.xml regenerated on every publish**, with `lastmod` accurate.
- **Canonical URLs stable.** No post moves from `/blog/{slug}` to `/blog/category/{slug}` later. Already a site-arch rule; content editorial must not override it.
- **`robots.txt` permissive to AI crawlers** (GPTBot, ClaudeBot, PerplexityBot, Google-Extended). Default to allow; this is the whole point.
- **Author pages indexable** and crosslinked from every post.

### What *not* to do for AEO

- Don't stuff FAQ blocks with questions no one asks. LLMs learn to distrust them.
- Don't publish the same article under two slugs for "A/B" SEO — splits equity and makes LLM citation unreliable.
- Don't write opaque headers. *"Understanding Section 148"* is worse than *"What section 148 is, who issues it, and when you must respond."* LLMs lift headers as answer anchors.

## 7. LinkedIn Adaptation Rules

Founder-led LinkedIn is the second-largest distribution channel after Google. No duplicate-content penalty exists for blog-to-LinkedIn repurposing *as long as* the LinkedIn post is not a verbatim copy and does not link to an external canonical on every post.

### Which posts become LinkedIn

| Blog format | LinkedIn treatment | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Pillar article | Thread of 6–10 posts across 2–3 weeks — one hook per sub-point | Every pillar |
| Template / reply-format | Single post with a carousel (PDF doc) of the template structure | Every template |
| Case-law note | Single post with the citation + a founder take on what it means for everyday filing | Every case-law note |
| Listicle ("top 6 reasons faceless drafts fail") | Single post, reformatted as a numbered LinkedIn post | Every listicle |
| Deep guide | Picked selectively (maybe 1 in 3). A good guide becomes a LinkedIn post; a procedural one doesn't | Selective |
| Founder commentary | Native LinkedIn post first, optional blog post later | Always |
| CA-interview | Single post quoting the interviewee, with a photo — high shareability | Every interview |

### Reformatting rules (no duplicate-content risk)

1. **Rewrite, don't copy.** Same ideas, different sentences. LinkedIn's algorithm suppresses copy-paste content; Google has no penalty for unique rewrites.
2. **Native-first.** The LinkedIn post is self-contained — reader does not need to click through. The CTA at the bottom is *"Full version on the blog"*, not *"click to read"*.
3. **No link in the post body.** LinkedIn's algorithm suppresses reach on posts with an outbound link. Put the link in the first comment, or omit.
4. **Hook in the first 1–2 lines.** LinkedIn truncates at ~200 characters. The hook is the whole post for 60% of viewers.
5. **Author is the founder (named), not "Accountic."** The profession buys from people. A brand post gets a third of the reach.
6. **End with a concrete question.** CAs love to correct each other in comments — engagement spikes.
7. **Carousels for templates.** A PDF carousel of a reply template gets 3–5× the reach of a text post.
8. **Mid-month: 1 commentary post with no blog equivalent.** A raw take from a sales call or a client situation. Keeps the feed human.

### Cadence

- **Launch month:** 2–3 posts/week across founders (not one founder). Distributed authorship signals a real team.
- **Month 2–3:** 3–4 posts/week. Pillar threads deliberately staggered so the feed isn't thread-heavy.
- **Month 6+:** **5 posts/week** sustained, assuming freelance content support by then. One of them is a weekly case-law quick-take — a recurring format the audience will start to expect.

### What to avoid on LinkedIn

- Thought-leadership on AI generally. Stay in the CA lane.
- Pitching the product in more than ~1 in 5 posts. The feed should read as *"a CA who happens to run a tool,"* not *"a tool masquerading as a CA."*
- Engagement bait ("Agree? 👇"). The audience is senior and cringes.
- Reposting a blog-post snippet verbatim. Both algorithms punish it; CAs notice it.

## 8. What NOT to Write

Deliberate negative space. Every item here has a reason — saying no to content is how a small team ships depth.

| Don't write | Why |
|---|---|
| "What is Income Tax" / "How to file ITR" / "ITR-1 vs ITR-2" guides | Dominated by ClearTax, Taxmann, IndiaFilings, and the IT Department's own portal. Unwinnable, and off-positioning — Accountic is not a consumer tax brand. |
| Generic "AI in taxation" trend pieces | Empty calories. Blurs the "built by CAs" line. Every founder post about AI should anchor to a concrete section/notice/workflow, not to the meta-trend. |
| GST notice content | Out of V1 scope. Publishing GST content pre-ships the product's breadth claim past what V1 actually delivers and sets the wrong expectation with the first cohort. Pick back up post-launch, only once GST is in the product. |
| MCA / company law / customs content | Same reason. V1 is Income Tax only. |
| Compliance calendar / deadline tracker content | Calendar features aren't in the product. A calendar blog post creates a feature gap in the reader's mind that V1 can't close. |
| Tally / bookkeeping integration content | Roadmap item. Not V1. Don't set the expectation. |
| "Best AI tools for CAs" listicles | Either (a) we put ourselves in it, which looks desperate, or (b) we don't, which hands the keyword to competitors. Skip the category. Let someone else rank; we rank for notice-specific queries. |
| "Top 10 accounting software" / "Top 10 CA software" | Same reason. Off-positioning; we're not a CA suite. |
| Comparison blog posts (vs Quick Litigate / NABS / etc.) | These are `/vs/*` landing pages, not blog posts. Don't split equity. |
| Generic thought-leadership on "the future of accounting" | Founders are practicing CAs, not pundits. Speak from cases, not from predictions. |
| "How to use ChatGPT for tax work" guides | Directly contradicts positioning. Instead: *"How LLMs hallucinate IT Act sections — and how to verify"* (topic #63 in the table) — which is the anti-frame. |
| Consumer-facing finance (personal tax saving, LIC vs ULIP) | Wrong audience. We sell to CAs, not their clients. |
| Vendor interviews / "sponsored" roundups | No. Trust-first profession. |
| Thin "5 things to know about Section 148" listicles | A 500-word listicle on a head-term topic cannibalizes the pillar and looks like every other SEO farm. If we publish a listicle on section 148, it's *"Common errors in 148 replies"* — a specific, experienced take, not a generic overview. |
| Newsjack pieces on Budget day every year | One per year max. Budget content ages out in three months and doesn't compound. |
| "A day in the life of a CA" lifestyle pieces | No product connection. Burns the LinkedIn budget without returning SEO equity. |
| Humor / meme content about tax season | Wrong register for a trust-first buyer. A senior partner doesn't route notice work to a brand that made a meme about faceless assessment. |

### Two cases where the "don't write" list has exceptions

1. **Budget-day coverage** — one focused post on "what changed for assessment/reassessment/faceless in this Budget" is allowed, once a year, because it's a timely AEO play and CAs search for it. Must be in by 10am on Budget day or it's worthless.
2. **Major Supreme Court judgment** on reassessment, faceless, or any in-scope notice type — a same-day case note goes up, full stop. Accountic wants to be the site an LLM cites within 48 hours of a landmark judgment.

## 9. Success Signals for the Content Program

Six months in, the content strategy is working if:

- Three of the five notice-type pillars are on **page 1 of Google** for their head terms in India.
- At least two Accountic posts are **cited in ChatGPT / Perplexity / Gemini / Claude answers** for notice-type queries. (Test monthly; track via controlled prompts — *"what is the 148A(b) reply format"*, *"how should a CA respond to a section 143(2) notice"*.)
- **`/trust/dpdp`** and one DPDP blog post are referenced on a firm customer's public website or engagement letter (signals authority).
- Content drives **≥20% of free-trial signups** by month 6, **≥30%** by month 12.
- **Founder LinkedIn** consistently generates 2–3 inbound demo requests per month by month 6.
- **No post deleted or moved** (redirect discipline). Canonical URLs stable means LLM-cached citations hold.

## 10. Open Decisions

Not content-strategy decisions exactly, but they shape execution:

1. **Editor/managing editor hire or freelance.** A part-time managing editor by month 3 is likely necessary to sustain 10 posts/month at the stated quality bar. Founder time is finite.
2. **Gating.** Default is no gating. Guides and templates are free. Decide by month 6 whether a high-value asset (e.g., "DPDP engagement-letter template pack") is email-gated. Recommendation: keep everything ungated in year one — AEO is the acquisition play, and gated content doesn't get cited.
3. **Newsletter.** Content program assumes a lightweight founder-voice newsletter starts at month 3 — weekly, recaps new posts plus a commentary piece. Decide by month 2.
4. **CA Interview permissions.** Interview subjects are likely named customers. Sequence with the 90-day pilot-customer plan in the brief — interviews start month 4, not month 1.
5. **Author-page format.** Site arch specifies ICAI number on author pages. Confirm with founding partners that this is shared publicly.
