## Executive Summary

Responding to an Income Tax notice is a Chartered Accountant's highest-stakes recurring task — and often the most time-consuming. Routine notices burn 3–4 hours; scrutiny and reassessment notices can consume days. Across ~1.6 lakh practicing CAs in India, this is the single largest pool of skilled, repetitive work in the profession.

**Accountic V1** is a focused AI workbench for Income Tax notice response: upload a notice, get a structured analysis and a draft reply grounded in verified IT Act references, relevant case law, and the CA's own prior replies. Priced at **₹200 per notice** with quota-based monthly plans, it compresses days of research and drafting into a focused expert review — with a citation-verification layer backed by a credit-refund policy, the only one of its kind among Indian notice tools. Built by a founding team of practicing CAs and IT professionals, on infrastructure provided by SOC 2 Type II–compliant technology partner Thalia, with India-resident data handling. Launching April 2026.

## The Problem

An Income Tax notice is a time-bound legal instrument with financial exposure that can run into crores. Responding to one requires:

- **Reading and classifying** — u/s 143(2), 148, 142(1), 139(9), 245, each with distinct deadlines and drafting conventions.
- **Research** — current IT Act sections, CBDT circulars, and case law (ITAT, HC, SC) relevant to the specific grounds raised.
- **Drafting** — a defensible reply that demonstrates "application of mind" under faceless assessment and aligns with the CA's prior positions for the same client.
- **Formatting and filing** — portal-ready output under an unforgiving clock.

Most CAs do this on weekends and after-hours. Routine notices take 3–4 hours. Scrutiny and reassessment notices frequently consume **multiple days** of concentrated work. A solo practitioner handling 20–40 notices a month is doing a full week (or more) of pure drafting before any other client engagement. Mid-size firms push the work to juniors — but senior review is still the bottleneck.

Current coping mechanisms are all compromised:

- **Legacy CA suites** (Winman, Genius, CompuTax, KDK) handle filing but not notice drafting.
- **Generic AI tools** (ChatGPT, Claude via browser) draft fast but hallucinate sections, cite repealed provisions, leak PAN/GSTIN to foreign LLMs with no data-processing agreement, and produce boilerplate output that has drawn increasing judicial commentary on "application of mind."
- **First-wave AI notice tools** (Quick Litigate, NABS NoticeAI, TaxNoticeAI, TaxCorp AI) plus **Taxmann.AI × EY India** (announced Draft Bot for notice drafting, built on Taxmann's 60-year citation library and EY's tax technology stack) are the real competitive set. Most first-wave tools span GST, IT, MCA, customs, and company law simultaneously, with citation accuracy, privacy posture, and professional tone as second-order concerns; Taxmann.AI brings unmatched research-side credibility but enters drafting from a research-first posture and will optimize for mid/large firms and enterprise distribution.

## The Solution

A focused four-step workflow:

1. **Upload** — CA uploads a notice (PDF or scan). Manual upload in V1; see roadmap.
2. **Analyse** — Accountic classifies the notice and extracts key parameters (section, AY, demand amount, deadline, grounds raised), surfacing applicable IT Act provisions and relevant case law.
3. **Draft** — a reply grounded in the specific notice, applicable law, and the CA's **own prior replies** (uploaded as reference, in bulk or over time). Every legal citation is verified against a live IT Act and case-law repository before it reaches the draft.
4. **Export** — clean, portal-ready output for the CA to review, refine, and file.

**Design principle: Accountic assists the CA; it does not replace the CA.** Every draft shows its reasoning and citations transparently and expects human review — a compliance posture (ICAI Code of Ethics, judicial expectations under faceless assessment) and a trust posture (CAs will abandon any tool that embarrasses them before an assessing officer).

Competitors that offer institutional memory build it per-dispute, starting empty; Accountic's firm memory is grounded in your historical archive from the first notice you process. **Your corpus and outputs are never used to train models serving any other firm or customer.** Under ICAI confidentiality and DPDP, this is the only posture a senior partner can sign off on. Switching cost compounds with every notice you process.

## Who This Serves

**Primary: Solo and small-firm practicing CAs** handling their own or their clients' Income Tax notices. India has ~1.6 lakh practicing CAs and ~1 lakh registered firms, ~70% solo. They are detail-oriented and risk-averse, value control and auditability over "AI magic," and are already squeezed by faceless-assessment volumes. Success for them: a notice that used to consume half a day (or more) is handled in under 30 minutes, with output they will confidently put their name on.

**Primary: Mid-size CA firms (5–30 professionals)** running notice-response as a workflow. Buyer is the senior / managing partner; user is the junior or mid-level CA. Success: faster turnaround, consistent quality across juniors, less senior review time, more notice volume without headcount growth.

**Secondary (post-V1): Finance teams and founders** who want to understand a notice before engaging their CA — a natural adjacent segment once the core workflow is proven.

## Pricing

Pricing is per-notice, packaged in monthly notice-quota tiers. Billing unit is the notice (not the seat), which decouples revenue from the ~₹15k/yr per-seat ceiling of Indian CA software and frames the purchase as "expert output at ₹200 vs. multiple hours or days of my time."

| Tier | Monthly price | Notice quota | Effective price/notice |
|---|---|---|---|
| Free trial | ₹0 | 3 notices (one-time) | — |
| Basic | ₹2,000 | 10 / month | ₹200 |
| Pro | ₹20,000 | 100 / month | ₹200 |
| Firm / Enterprise | Custom | Higher quotas + seat controls | ₹200 or below |

**Soft quota with overage billing, not hard mid-month cutoffs.** A 153A deadline is not a good moment to discover a subscription limit. Quota overruns bill at the same effective per-notice rate until the next cycle.

**Indian-native billing.** GST-compliant invoice with the firm's GSTIN, payment via Razorpay (NEFT, UPI, cards), TDS-deductible where applicable. No USD billing, no foreign processors.

**Why ₹200/notice holds vs. cheaper competitors** (e.g. Quick Litigate at an effective ~₹75/notice on a 40-credit annual plan): the premium buys (a) citation verification with a credit-refund policy, (b) firm-memory/voice matching with explicit confidentiality commitments, (c) SOC 2 Type II–compliant infrastructure via Thalia with India residency and DPA availability, and (d) IT-notice depth over multi-domain breadth. If a single filed draft citing a repealed section can damage a CA's standing with an AO, the delta is trivial.

Pricing will evolve with usage data; V1 targets are to validate willingness-to-pay across Basic and Pro and to establish firm-tier custom pricing conversations.

## Compliance & Regulatory Context

- **DPDP Act, 2023** — CAs are Data Fiduciaries; engagement letters must specify data processing, retention, and sharing. Accountic offers Data Processing Agreements to firm customers. India-resident data handling, consent-per-document uploads, and explicit "no training on firm data" commitments are designed to make the CA's own DPDP compliance easier, not harder.
- **ICAI Code of Ethics (effective 1 April 2026)** — AI is formally recognised as a management consultancy service. Accountic is positioned as an assistant to the CA's professional judgment, with auditable workflow records suitable for peer review.
- **Faceless assessment / judicial guidance** — draft replies must demonstrate "application of mind." Accountic's workflow requires human review, shows reasoning, and exposes verified citations — aligning with judicial expectations.

## Key Risks & Open Questions

**Launch-blocking or near-launch decisions** the founding team should close before or immediately after launch:

1. **GTM playbook.** The existing CA network is a strong first channel, but a repeatable motion — pilot commitment, pricing anchor used in the intro, onboarding flow, first-week activation checklist — does not yet exist.
2. **Citation-verification internals.** The verification layer is only as strong as the underlying repository. Internally define coverage breadth, refresh cadence, and failure-mode handling; publish the parts that matter to buyers (e.g. refund policy) without over-committing to scope externally.
3. **Competitive response on two fronts.** (a) Feature parity from first-wave tools: Quick Litigate has an auto-fetch + advanced-RAG "Pro plan" advertised as in-pipeline, and NABS NoticeAI already ships multi-portal auto-fetch and PII redaction. Accountic's 90-day portal-fetch commitment closes the visible feature gap; a bookmarklet / browser-extension stopgap at launch closes the demo-optics gap immediately. (b) Legitimacy pressure from Taxmann.AI × EY India, whose announced Draft Bot enters the category with Taxmann's 60-year citation library as its RAG corpus and EY's enterprise tax distribution. Counter-positioning: solo and small-firm CAs (below Taxmann.AI's likely ARPA floor) and drafting-workflow depth (Taxmann is research-first). Until launch, the defensible wedge remains citation verification with credit-refund, corpus-wide firm memory from day one, and the Thalia / India-residency / DPA stack.
4. **Named pilot customers.** A trust-first profession buys on peer validation. Secure 5–10 named reference firms in the first 60 days — peers Rajesh Mehta can call before he signs.
5. **Success metrics.** The 90-day targets above are proposed and need founder sign-off; the product must instrument itself to report against them from day one.
6. **Pricing validation.** Slab ratios (Basic vs. Pro) and effective ARPA will only be clear after the first cohort. Expect to revisit the structure within 90 days of launch.
7. **Citation-incident protocol.** Even with the verification layer, any incident of a bad draft reaching an AO would damage trust materially. Incident response, refund execution, and customer-communication plans should be defined and rehearsed pre-launch.
