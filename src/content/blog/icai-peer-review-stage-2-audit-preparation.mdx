## ICAI Peer Review: Prepare Your CA Firm for Stage 2

If your firm conducts attest functions and you have not yet been called for a peer review, do not assume you have more time than you think. ICAI is steadily pulling smaller and mid-size firms into the mandatory net, and Stage 2 is where most firms get caught unprepared. This article gives you a practical, file-level checklist to fix gaps before the reviewer walks in.

---

## What Is ICAI Peer Review and Who Must Comply Now

The ICAI Peer Review mandate operates under the Statement on Peer Review (Revised 2022). The mandate is phased, moving from larger firms with more attest clients down to smaller practices, and the current phase covers firms that sign more than a threshold number of attest function engagements per year, including statutory audits, tax audits, limited reviews, and compilation reports.

The phased rollout means firms with as few as 15–20 attest clients in a year are now squarely in scope. If you received a peer review notice or are within the current threshold band published by ICAI's Peer Review Board, you need to act now rather than waiting for a formal call letter.

Stage 1 is a review of your firm's quality control policies — essentially, does your Quality Control Manual (QCM) reflect the requirements of SQC 1? Stage 2 goes one level deeper: the reviewer selects individual engagement files and tests whether your firm's actual work matches the policies you documented in Stage 1. Passing Stage 1 does not protect you from adverse Stage 2 findings.

---

## Stage 1 vs Stage 2: Scope, Evidence, and Failure Risk

Most partners underestimate Stage 2 because they conflate it with Stage 1. The table below shows exactly where the reviewer's focus shifts and what evidence you need ready at each level.

| Dimension | Stage 1 — Firm-Level Review | Stage 2 — Engagement-Level Review |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Primary focus | SQC 1 quality policies and firm procedures | Individual audit/attest engagement files |
| Evidence examined | Quality Control Manual, HR policies, CPE records | Working papers, engagement letters, sign-off logs, independence declarations |
| Independence documentation | Firm-level annual independence policy | Per-engagement signed independence confirmation from every team member |
| Materiality | Policy for setting materiality in QCM | Documented materiality calculation with rationale in each file |
| Risk assessment | Policy statement in QCM | Evidence in working papers that risk assessment was actually performed |
| Reviewer sign-off | Policy for partner review | Dated partner review note on each file, post-fieldwork but pre-report |
| Common failure mode | Outdated or generic QCM language | Missing dates, unsigned forms, no evidence of actual review |

The critical insight here is that a well-drafted QCM earns you a Stage 1 pass but gives no protection in Stage 2. Reviewers are trained to look for the gap between policy and practice, and that gap shows up in the files.

## Top Stage 2 Findings ICAI Reviewers Flag Most Often

After speaking with partners who have been through Stage 2 and reviewing ICAI's own published guidance, a clear pattern of recurring findings emerges. Fix these first and you eliminate the majority of your adverse observation risk.

**Missing or undated engagement partner review evidence.** The most common finding. A reviewer picks up a file, looks for the partner's review note, and finds either nothing or a review checklist with no date. Under SA 220 and SQC 1, the engagement partner review must be evidenced and must be datable to a point after fieldwork completion and before the audit report is signed. A blank signature or a retroactively added note with no date trail does not satisfy this requirement.

**Unsigned independence and conflict-of-interest confirmations.** Firms typically have an annual independence declaration signed once. But ICAI's peer review framework requires per-engagement independence confirmation, particularly for team members beyond the partner. If your Article assistants, audit seniors, or even fellow partners on a specific engagement have not signed an engagement-specific independence form, the file has a gap that reviewers will note.

**Inadequate risk assessment and materiality documentation.** Many firms set materiality in their head and never write it down. Others use a standard template that is never tailored to the specific client. Reviewers will look for a documented basis — percentage of revenue, net assets, or profit before tax, with a rationale for why that benchmark was chosen for that client. Similarly, the risk assessment under SA 315 must show evidence of actual inquiry and judgment, not just a ticked checkbox.

**Absence of a formal engagement acceptance or continuance checklist.** Before a new audit engagement is accepted or an existing one is continued, SQC 1 requires a documented assessment of independence, competence, and ethical requirements. Most small and mid-size firms do this informally, if at all. The peer review will expose it.

**No assembly completion date or incomplete file assembly.** SA 230 requires that the final audit file be assembled within 60 days of the audit report date. Reviewers check for an assembly completion date on the file cover and look for evidence that the file was not altered after that date.

## Step-by-Step Workflow to Achieve Stage 2 Readiness

Use this workflow if your peer review is scheduled within the next 90 days. Each step is sequenced to build on the previous one, and the entire process can be managed by a designated in-house coordinator with partner oversight.

1. Update your Quality Control Manual to reflect SQC 1 requirements and the latest ICAI peer review guidance, including per-engagement independence confirmation procedures, engagement acceptance checklists, and the 60-day assembly completion rule under SA 230.
2. Appoint one partner as the internal Peer Review Coordinator, give them a list of all attest engagements completed in the last two financial years, and task them with pulling the physical and digital files for review.
3. Prepare a sample of at least 10 engagement files — covering statutory audits, tax audits, and any limited reviews or compilations — spanning the period the reviewer is likely to test, prioritising files where the report was issued in the last 18 months.
4. Run a file-by-file gap analysis against the Stage 2 checklist: verify each file has a signed engagement letter, per-engagement independence declaration, documented materiality calculation, risk assessment working paper, and a dated partner review note.
5. Standardise your working paper indexing across all files using a uniform index sheet, so that the reviewer can navigate directly to each required document without the team needing to explain the filing structure.
6. Back-fill genuinely missing process documentation where permissible — for example, if independence was confirmed verbally but never recorded, have team members sign backdated confirmations with a disclosure note explaining the circumstance; do not fabricate work that was not done.
7. Conduct an internal mock review by asking a partner not involved in the sampled engagements to review three or four files cold, as a peer reviewer would, and note every gap they find before the actual visit.
8. Prepare a draft representation letter that your firm will provide to the reviewer, addressing your firm's compliance with SQC 1, the scope of attest functions conducted, and your CPE compliance status for all partners.
9. Organise both physical file access and digital/cloud file access before the reviewer arrives — confirm that passwords, permissions, and folder structures are in order, and designate one staff member as the point of contact for file retrieval requests during the review.
10. Set up a post-review remediation tracker: if the reviewer issues observations, you need a documented response and closure plan with dates, which ICAI's Peer Review Board expects to see before a clean certificate is issued.

## Documentation Standards Every Engagement File Must Meet

Before the reviewer arrives, every engagement file must independently satisfy a minimum documentation standard. Think of it as a file that can speak for itself without any verbal explanation from your team.

Under SA 230, the file must contain a final signed engagement letter, the signed management representation letter, and an assembly completion date recorded on the file cover or index. The assembly completion date must fall within 60 days of the audit report date — anything beyond that is a finding in itself.

Partner review notes must comply with SQC 1: they must be documented, dated, and clearly attributable to the engagement partner. The date of the partner review note must postdate the completion of fieldwork but precede the signing of the audit report. A review note with no date, or one that appears to have been added after the report date, will immediately draw an adverse observation.

Your firm's file retention policy must be documented and applied consistently. The minimum retention period is seven years from the date of the audit report. For digital files, you need evidence of secure storage — access logs, version control, or at minimum a cloud storage policy that shows files cannot be altered after assembly completion. For physical files, locked storage with a documented access register satisfies this requirement.

**Case Scenario:**

A five-partner firm in Mumbai with approximately 80 statutory audit clients underwent its first Stage 2 peer review and received three major observations. The first was that CPE logs for two partners were incomplete — attendance certificates existed but were not compiled into a firm-level CPE register linked to individual partners. The second was that materiality had been documented in fewer than half the sampled files; in the others, the working paper file had no materiality calculation at all. The third was the absence of any formal engagement acceptance checklist — the firm had accepted all engagements based on partner judgment, which is acceptable in practice but was not documented anywhere.

The firm took 60 days to remediate. They adopted a standardised working paper template built around SA 230 and SA 315 requirements, with mandatory fields for materiality, risk assessment, and independence that could not be left blank. They ran a half-day partner training session specifically on the documentation standards expected in Stage 2. They also built a CPE register in a shared spreadsheet, mapping attendance certificates to individual partners and calculating annual hours against ICAI requirements.

On their second attempt, the firm received a clean peer review certificate with no adverse observations. The engagement partner who led the remediation noted that the most valuable outcome was not the certificate itself, but the fact that every new file since then has been built to the same standard from day one — which means the next peer review cycle requires no scramble at all. This pattern is directly replicable for any firm with 2 to 10 partners targeting first-time compliance.
