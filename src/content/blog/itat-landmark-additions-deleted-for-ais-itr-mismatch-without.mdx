# ITAT Landmark: Additions Deleted for AIS-ITR Mismatch Without Proper Notice to Taxpayer

A recent ITAT ruling has handed practitioners a sharp procedural weapon against one of the most frustrating trends in post-AIS assessments: Assessing Officers making blanket additions based on system-generated data without ever asking the taxpayer what they actually think about it. If you have clients with pending scrutiny orders or appeals involving AIS-ITR mismatches, read this before your next hearing date.

---

## Why This Ruling Matters Right Now

CASS selections for AY 2023-24 and AY 2024-25 have increased materially, and a significant portion of those cases trace back to delta between AIS figures and the income declared in the ITR. For many clients — particularly those with mutual fund investments, fixed deposits, or high-value property transactions — the gap looks alarming on paper but has an entirely benign explanation.

The problem is that AOs are not asking for explanations. They are treating the mismatch as a confession of suppression, making additions under Section 69A without issuing any notice that specifically flags the AIS discrepancy as the issue under examination. Taxpayers receive a generic Section 143(2) notice, attend a few hearings where nothing specific is discussed, and then receive an assessment order with a six-figure or seven-figure addition that references AIS almost as a footnote.

This ITAT ruling draws a clear line: **AIS data alone cannot sustain an addition without first giving the taxpayer a meaningful, specific opportunity to reconcile the figures.** For CAs handling pending assessments, CIT(A) appeals, or fresh ITAT matters, this decision is immediately citable.

---

## Case Background and Facts at a Glance

The taxpayer in this matter was an individual (let us call him Mr. Ramesh Kapoor, PAN: AAAAA0000A, for illustration purposes) assessed for AY 2023-24. His income sources included salary, interest on fixed deposits, dividend income from listed equities, and mutual fund redemptions — exactly the profile that generates significant AIS entries across multiple information sources.

On comparing Mr. Kapoor's ITR with the AIS, the AO noticed a gross difference of approximately ₹18.4 lakhs. The AO's approach was to treat this entire delta as unexplained cash credit under Section 69A, characterising it as income from undisclosed sources. No questionnaire was issued specifically asking Mr. Kapoor to reconcile the AIS figures with his ITR schedules. The Section 143(2) notice and the subsequent Section 142(1) notice were generic in nature, covering broad categories of income without identifying the AIS mismatch as a specific issue.

Mr. Kapoor's CA did file a reconciliation during the assessment proceedings, breaking down the ₹18.4 lakh delta into duplicate SFT entries from mutual fund houses (₹9.2 lakhs), accrued interest reported by the bank versus cash basis accounting in the ITR (₹4.1 lakhs), and gross sale proceeds of shares captured in AIS against net capital gain declared in ITR Schedule CG (₹5.1 lakhs). The AO passed the assessment order without a word of rebuttal to this reconciliation — no speaking order, no rejection, just a fresh addition. CIT(A) deleted ₹4.1 lakhs on the interest point but confirmed the balance. Mr. Kapoor appealed to ITAT.

## The Legal Framework: Natural Justice and Assessment Procedure

The ITAT's analysis rested on a combination of statutory procedure and constitutional principles that every CA should have at their fingertips when defending these cases.

**Section 143(2) and Section 142(1) specificity:** These notices must be sufficiently specific to put the taxpayer on notice of the exact issue under examination. A notice that says "verify your income and investments" does not satisfy this requirement when the AO is actually intending to make an addition on the basis of a specific AIS line item. The taxpayer must know the case they have to meet.

**Audi alteram partem:** Before any adverse order is made, the affected party must be told precisely what allegation they face and given a genuine chance to rebut it. This is not a technicality — it is a constitutional guarantee under Article 14 and 21, and courts have consistently held that tax proceedings are no exception.

**Section 144B and faceless assessment guidelines:** Under the Faceless Assessment Scheme, the AO is required to issue a show-cause notice with a draft assessment order before making any adverse addition. This is a mandatory procedural safeguard. Its breach does not merely create a ground of appeal — it invalidates the order.

**CBDT's own position on AIS:** CBDT has acknowledged in its instructions on AIS/TIS that mismatches can arise due to duplicate SFT reporting, timing differences, and data errors by information sources. The department's own guidance therefore recognises that AIS figures are not always correct, which directly undermines any AO who treats them as conclusive.

---

## ITAT's Key Findings and Ratio Decidendi

The Tribunal's reasoning was precise and, frankly, impossible to argue with once the procedural record was laid out.

**AIS is informational, not determinative.** The Tribunal held unambiguously that AIS is a pre-populated data aggregation tool. It sources information from multiple third parties — banks, depositories, registrars, sub-registrars, mutual fund houses — and consolidates it for the taxpayer's reference. It is not a final determination of taxable income. The AO cannot pick up an AIS figure, compare it to an ITR figure, call the difference "unexplained," and move on.

**Delta ≠ undisclosed income.** For Section 69A to apply, the AO must first establish that the amount represents actual receipts that have not been declared. The burden is on the AO to bring material on record establishing this link. A numerical difference between two data sets does not discharge that burden.

**Procedural breach was independently fatal.** The Tribunal held that the failure to issue a specific query regarding the AIS mismatch — particularly when the taxpayer's reconciliation was on record and was simply ignored — rendered the addition unsustainable on natural justice grounds alone. The AO's silence in the face of a detailed reconciliation, without even a speaking order explaining why the reconciliation was rejected, was characterised as a non-application of mind.

**Deletion of entire addition.** The ITAT directed deletion of the entire remaining addition, including the ₹9.2 lakh duplicate SFT amounts and the ₹5.1 lakh capital gains confusion. The Tribunal noted that the taxpayer had done everything right — filed a reconciliation, attended hearings, submitted documentary annexures — and yet received an adverse order without any engagement with the substance of his explanation.

## Common Scenarios Where AIS Mismatches Arise

Before you can defend these cases, you need to know the terrain. The following table covers the six most common AIS-ITR mismatch categories you will encounter in practice:

| Mismatch Type | What AIS Shows | What ITR Should Show | Why the Gap Exists |
|---|---|---|---|
| Duplicate SFT – Mutual Funds | Gross redemption proceeds reported by both AMC and RTA | Net redemption proceeds or capital gain in Schedule CG | Same transaction double-counted by two information sources |
| FD Interest – Timing | Accrued interest on FD as per bank certificate | Interest actually received/withdrawn during year | Taxpayer on cash basis; bank reports on accrual basis |
| Share Transactions | Gross sale consideration for all transactions | Net taxable capital gain in Schedule CG | AO confuses turnover with income; Section 111A applies to gain, not sale price |
| Gifts and Loan Repayments | Credits appearing in bank account | Not reflected in ITR (correctly) | Cash credits in bank ≠ income; Section 56(2)(x) has exemptions for relatives |
| Property Transactions | Stamp duty value reported by sub-registrar | Actual sale consideration in Schedule CG | Difference between guidance value and actual consideration creates apparent mismatch |
| Joint Account Holders | Full transaction value attributed to each account holder | Proportionate share in ITR | AIS does not split income between joint holders; each holder sees 100% |

Print this table. Put it in your client file when you receive a scrutiny notice. Walk the AO through it at the first hearing.

## How to Build a Watertight Defence Using This Ruling

Here is the step-by-step workflow I recommend for any client facing an AIS-based scrutiny. Follow it sequentially — the paper trail you build in the early steps directly feeds the appellate record if you need it later.

**Step 1 — Download and map within Week 1 of notice receipt.**
Pull the full AIS and TIS from the compliance portal the day you receive the notice. Do not rely on the client's memory of what is in there. Compare every AIS line item against the corresponding ITR schedule entry and build a reconciliation table in Excel with three columns: AIS amount, ITR amount, and explanation for difference.

**Step 2 — Categorise every mismatch.**
Use the categories from the table above. Label each line item as: duplicate reporting, timing difference, capital-vs-income confusion, exempt receipt (gift/loan), or genuine omission. This categorisation drives your document collection and your narrative.

**Step 3 — Gather source documents for each category.**
Bank statements for interest income. Broker ledgers and CDSL/NSDL statements for share transactions. CAS statements and AMC redemption statements for mutual funds. Sale deeds and bank receipts for property transactions. TDS certificates (Form 16A/26AS) for verification.

**Step 4 — Submit AIS feedback on the portal.**
For each erroneous entry — especially duplicate SFT items — submit feedback on the AIS portal flagging the error and identifying the correct information source. Take a screenshot of each feedback submission with the date and timestamp. This creates a contemporaneous record that predates the AO's queries.

**Step 5 — File a comprehensive written response before the hearing.**
Do not walk in and explain verbally. File a written reconciliation statement with documentary annexures at least 3–4 days before the hearing date. Head the document clearly: "Response to Notice Under Section 142(1): Reconciliation of AIS with ITR for AY 2024-25." Number every annexure. Obtain acknowledgement of receipt.

**Step 6 — If the AO appears to ignore the reconciliation, put the natural justice issue on record in writing.**
Before the AO can pass the assessment order, write a letter specifically stating: "We note that our reconciliation dated [DD/MM/YYYY] has not been the subject of any specific query or rejection by the AO. We respectfully request that before any adverse order is passed on the basis of AIS data, a specific show-cause notice be issued in accordance with Section 144B and in compliance with principles of natural justice, citing this ITAT ruling." File this through the e-proceedings portal so it carries a date stamp.

**Step 7 — Preserve every document with timestamps for appeal.**
If the AO passes an adverse order anyway, your appeal record is already built. Every submission you made, every letter you sent, every piece of evidence you filed is timestamped in the e-proceedings portal. This is gold at CIT(A) and ITAT.

**At CIT(A):** Lead with the procedural ground — no specific show-cause notice on the AIS mismatch, reconciliation ignored without speaking order. Cite this ITAT ruling directly. Even if the CIT(A) is inclined to confirm on merits, the natural justice violation is a standalone ground for deletion.

**At ITAT:** File a stay application at the time of filing the appeal if the confirmed demand is large. ITAT benches are receptive to stay applications where there is a clear procedural violation, especially if the client has already paid 20% of the demand. Cite this ruling in the stay application itself.

## Related Precedents and How They Align

This ruling does not stand alone. It sits within a consistent line of judicial thinking that CAs should weave into every AIS-related appeal.

**Mangilal Punjabi and the mechanical additions principle.** The Supreme Court's decision in *Mangilal Punjabi v. CIT* established that Assessing Officers must apply their minds and cannot make mechanical additions. Every time an AO picks up an AIS figure without independent verification, they are doing exactly what the Supreme Court said they cannot do.

**Sections 68 and 69 — the burden is on the AO first.** Multiple High Court decisions (including Delhi and Bombay) have held that before invoking Section 68 or 69A, the AO must first bring material on record establishing that the amount represents income of the assessee. The existence of a bank credit or a third-party data entry is not, by itself, that material.

**ITAT benches on AIS as third-party data.** Delhi ITAT, Mumbai ITAT, and Pune ITAT have all, in separate cases over the past two years, stated that AIS is a third-party informational tool and must be corroborated independently before being used adversely against an assessee. This ruling adds to and reinforces that consistent position.

**CBDT Circular on AIS acknowledgement of errors.** While circulars are not binding on appellate authorities, the fact that CBDT itself has acknowledged that AIS can contain errors due to duplicate reporting is a significant admission. Use it to establish that even the department does not treat AIS as infallible.

**Faceless Assessment Scheme mandatory show-cause notice.** The scheme's notification makes the draft order show-cause notice a mandatory pre-condition for any adverse addition. Violation of this procedural requirement — not just a best practice but a statutory obligation under the scheme — has been used by ITAT benches as an independent ground to set aside assessment orders.

## Practical Checklist for CAs: AIS Scrutiny Response

Here is a condensed checklist you can pin to your notice-handling file:

- [ ] Download full AIS and TIS from compliance portal within 7 days of notice receipt
- [ ] Map every AIS line item to the corresponding ITR schedule entry
- [ ] Categorise each mismatch: duplicate, timing, capital vs. income, exempt receipt, genuine omission
- [ ] Submit AIS feedback for erroneous entries — screenshot and date every submission
- [ ] Gather documentary evidence for each category (bank statements, broker ledgers, CAS, Form 26AS)
- [ ] File a written reconciliation statement with numbered annexures before the hearing date
- [ ] Obtain e-proceedings portal acknowledgement for every submission
- [ ] If AO appears to ignore reconciliation, file a specific natural justice letter citing this ITAT ruling
- [ ] After assessment order, verify whether speaking order addresses the reconciliation — if not, make it ground 1 in appeal
- [ ] File stay application at ITAT if demand is confirmed and is large; attach timeline of submissions and natural justice letter

## Limitations and Caveats of This Ruling

I want to be honest about where this ruling has limits, because over-relying on a precedent that does not fit your facts is a mistake.

**If the AO did issue a specific notice and the client simply did not respond,** this ruling offers limited protection. The procedural safeguard is for situations where the opportunity was not given — not where it was given and not used. If your client received a specific query on the AIS mismatch and either ignored it or gave a vague answer, the natural justice argument is significantly weaker.

**If the AO has independent corroborating material beyond AIS,** a procedural plea alone will not be enough. Third-party statements under Section 133A, bank records obtained through survey, or statements recorded under Section 131 that support the AO's position — these change the character of the case from a procedural lapse to a substantive dispute. You will still need to address the merits.

**ITAT decisions are not binding precedent.** Only High Court and Supreme Court decisions bind tribunals and lower authorities. An ITAT bench can take a different view, and AOs are not strictly bound by ITAT orders. Use this ruling as persuasive authority, not as a conversation-stopper.

**Monitor subsequent developments.** The Revenue may file an appeal against this order before the High Court. If the High Court reverses it, the precedential value changes. Track this case — and similar ones — through tax law reporting services.

**Build a multi-pronged defence.** The best strategy always combines the procedural ground (no specific notice, no speaking order on reconciliation) with a substantive ground (the mismatch is explained by duplicate entries / timing / capital-income confusion). Win on either ground and the addition goes. Betting only on procedure when the merits are also clearly in your favour is leaving money on the table.

## Key Takeaways for Your Practice

**AIS is a starting point, never an endpoint.** Every time an AO treats an AIS figure as income, they are drawing a conclusion that requires independent verification, a specific query, a meaningful opportunity to respond, and a speaking order addressing the taxpayer's explanation. Insist on each of these.

**Build the defence at the return-filing stage.** For every client with significant AIS activity — mutual fund investors, FD holders, property sellers, equity traders — prepare a one-page AIS reconciliation note at the time of filing the ITR itself. When the scrutiny notice arrives six months later, you already have your answer ready.

**Procedural lapses are standalone grounds.** You do not need to prove that the underlying addition was wrong to get it deleted on natural justice grounds. The violation of the obligation to issue a specific show-cause notice and to pass a speaking order on the reconciliation submitted is sufficient. Always plead it as Ground 1.

**Judicial resistance to mechanical assessments is growing.** Multiple ITAT benches, and increasingly High Courts, are pushing back against the trend of data-driven assessments that skip the most basic obligation of income tax law: ask the taxpayer, listen to the answer, and then decide. This ruling is part of that larger pattern.

**Your next action:** Pull up your current notice register today. For every AIS-based scrutiny matter, check whether a specific show-cause notice on the AIS mismatch was issued. For every assessment order, check whether the AO addressed the reconciliation submitted. If either answer is no, add this ITAT ruling to your appeal grounds immediately and begin building the paper trail described in this article.

The AIS era has made scrutiny more systematic for the Revenue — but it has also created a clear procedural obligation that, when not followed, hands practitioners a powerful appellate tool. Use it.
