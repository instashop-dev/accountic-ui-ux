# Section 245 Notice: Respond to Income Tax Refund Setoff

A Section 245 notice is one of those items that lands in a client's inbox and immediately escalates to your phone. The client sees "refund adjustment" and panics. Your job is to move fast, verify the demand, and respond correctly — because silence on this notice is legally treated as consent.

---

## What Is a Section 245 Notice and Why Did You Receive One?

Section 245 of the Income Tax Act gives the department the authority to adjust a pending refund against any outstanding tax demand from a prior assessment year, before releasing the refund to the taxpayer. It is not a new provision, but what changed is the scale of automated enforcement.

CPC began running aggressive automated setoffs from AY 2023-24 processing onwards. Clients who had forgotten about decade-old demands suddenly received notices as the system matched refunds to outstanding entries in the demand register — even demands that were disputed, stayed, or already paid but not correctly reflected in the portal.

The critical point to communicate to every client immediately: this notice is an intimation, not a final order. The taxpayer has a statutory right to agree or object, and the department must act on that response. The standard response window is 30 days from the date of the notice — not from the date your client forwards it to you.

---

## Anatomy of a Section 245 Notice: Key Fields to Check

Before you draft a response, spend ten minutes verifying the notice details against your own records. Errors at this stage cascade into incorrect objections and wasted time before the AO.

**Client Scenario:** Your client, a mid-sized proprietorship, receives a Section 245 notice in March 2025 during processing of the AY 2025-26 return. The notice states an outstanding demand of ₹3.8 lakhs for AY 2014-15 is being set off against a refund of ₹2.1 lakhs for AY 2024-25. You pull up your demand register and immediately spot two problems: (a) the AY 2014-15 demand was disputed before CIT(A) and a stay was granted in 2016, and (b) the refund year cited is AY 2024-25, not the current year — meaning this setoff relates to a prior year's refund that the client never received.

Three fields in the notice demand your immediate attention every time:

| Field | What to Verify | Where to Cross-Check |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Demand AY and demand amount | Match against your internal demand register and Form 26AS | Outstanding Demand tab on IT portal |
| Refund AY and refund amount | Confirm which year's refund is being withheld | ITR acknowledgement and Form 26AS |
| Response deadline | Count calendar days from the notice date, not the receipt date | Communication date shown on portal |
| Communication reference number | Required to file the online response — note it before proceeding | Top right of the notice PDF |

Do not rely solely on the notice document. Log in to incometax.gov.in, navigate to Pending Actions, and read the demand details directly from the portal. Discrepancies between the notice PDF and the portal entry must be flagged in your response.

## Step-by-Step Workflow to Respond to a Section 245 Notice Online

This is the sequence that works. Do not skip the verification steps — submitting a response based on incorrect portal data is the most common error we see in practice.

1. Log in to incometax.gov.in using the taxpayer's credentials (or your CA login if authorised). Navigate to **Pending Actions → Response to Outstanding Demand** and locate the specific Section 245 notice using the communication reference number.
2. Click on the demand entry and review the demand details shown — Assessment Year, demand amount, and the refund proposed for adjustment. Note any mismatch between what the portal shows and what the notice PDF states.
3. Cross-verify the demand amount against Form 26AS, AIS, and your internal records. If the demand was previously paid, pull the challan counterfoil or the Form 26AS extract that shows the tax credit. If the demand is under appeal, retrieve the CIT(A) or ITAT order and the stay order if applicable.
4. Select the appropriate response option from the portal: **"Demand is correct"**, **"Demand is partially correct"**, or **"Demand is incorrect / not payable"**. Each option opens a different sub-form — read the sub-options carefully before selecting, because the system branches based on your reason code.
5. Attach supporting documents in PDF format — challan copies, appeal orders, stay orders, or rectification request references. The portal has a file size limit; compress documents if necessary but ensure all pages are legible.
6. Submit the response and immediately download the acknowledgement PDF. This acknowledgement is your proof of timely response and must be saved in the client file.
7. Set a reminder to track the status under the same Pending Actions tab after 30 days. If the status has not updated to "Processed" or the refund has not been released, raise a formal grievance on the portal under the Grievance module citing the acknowledgement reference.

## How to Dispute a Stale or Already-Paid Demand

Three scenarios come up repeatedly in practice, and each requires a specific document set. Mixing them up wastes a response opportunity.

**Demand already paid.** If the client paid this demand and the credit simply was not absorbed in the system, select "Demand already paid" as the sub-reason and upload the challan counterfoil along with a Form 26AS extract showing the matching BSR code, date, and amount. If the credit shows in Form 26AS but not in the demand register, simultaneously file a rectification request under Section 154 to correct the discrepancy — the Section 245 response alone does not automatically trigger demand closure.

**Demand under appeal or stay.** Upload the appeal order from CIT(A) or ITAT confirming the matter is pending, and attach the stay order with clear reference to the demand AY. The portal allows you to select "Stay granted" as a sub-reason — use it. A demand that is stayed cannot legally be used for setoff, but the automated system does not distinguish; your response is what puts the record straight before the Assessing Officer.

**Old demands (pre-AY 2009-10).** CBDT has issued instructions acknowledging that legacy demands in the system may be stale, time-barred, or incorrectly migrated. If the demand appearing in the notice relates to a year older than AY 2009-10, cite the relevant CBDT circulars in your written response and simultaneously raise a rectification request under Section 154 asking for the demand to be reviewed or deleted. If necessary, follow up with a manual grievance to the jurisdictional AO with documentary support.

## Agree vs. Disagree: Consequences and Next Steps Compared

Choosing the wrong response option has real consequences for your client's refund timeline and the demand's legal status. Use this reference before selecting your option on the portal.

| Response Option | Legal Consequence | Refund Release Timeline | CA Action Required |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Demand is correct (full agreement) | Demand closed, setoff confirmed | Balance refund released in 7–10 working days after processing | Verify refund receipt; close the demand in your register |
| Demand is partially correct | Undisputed portion set off; disputed portion stays open for adjudication | Refund for undisputed portion released; balance held pending AO review | Monitor AO proceedings; track grievance if no hearing notice within 30 days |
| Demand is incorrect / not payable | Setoff stays on hold; Assessing Officer reviews response and may call for documents or pass a fresh order | Refund remains withheld until AO decides | Follow up proactively; do not wait for AO to act — file grievance after 30 days if no movement |
| No response (silence) | Treated as consent; setoff confirmed automatically after deadline | Refund adjusted against demand permanently | No recourse without rectification or fresh legal proceedings |

The partial agreement route is often the most commercially sensible — it gets the undisputed refund portion released quickly while preserving the client's legal position on the contested demand.

## Common Mistakes CAs Make When Handling Section 245 Notices

The biggest mistake is treating this notice as a low-priority item. We have seen firms where junior staff file the Section 245 notice in the "to review" pile, the deadline passes, and the setoff is confirmed on a demand the client had already paid years ago. That conversation with the client is avoidable.

The second common error is responding without reconciling the demand year. The notice may cite AY 2015-16, but your client's records may show payment against AY 2014-15 — close, but not the same demand. Filing an objection that says "paid in 2015" when the portal references a different year's demand results in rejection and a fresh inquiry from the AO. Always match the exact AY before selecting your reason code.

The third error is assuming the response closes the matter. Submitting the response is the beginning, not the end. Track the portal status every two weeks. If the status remains at "Submitted" without progressing to "Processed" for more than 30 days, escalate immediately via the Grievance module — attach the acknowledgement reference number and a brief note of the facts. Refunds stuck in post-response limbo are more common than the department acknowledges.

## CA Practice Checklist: Section 245 Response Readiness

During the July–March period when CPC processing and automated setoffs peak, a Section 245 notice can arrive for any client, any morning. Firms that have a system handle these in hours; firms that do not handle them in days and miss deadlines.

1. Build and maintain a **demand register** mapping every active client's PAN to all outstanding demands by Assessment Year, along with the current status: paid, under appeal, stayed, or disputed. Update this register at the close of every filing season.
2. At the start of each filing season, **pre-collect key documents** for every client with open demands: paid challans with BSR codes and dates, CIT(A) and ITAT orders, stay orders, and Form 26AS for all open demand years. These must be available immediately when a notice arrives — not gathered after receipt.
3. Assign one team member to run a **weekly Pending Actions check** on the IT portal for all active clients from July through March. This is the only reliable way to catch notices before clients forward them to you with five days left on the clock.
4. Create a **standard response template** for each scenario — demand paid, demand under appeal, demand stayed — so that when a notice arrives, the response is assembled from verified documents in under two hours rather than drafted from scratch.
5. After every submission, **log the acknowledgement reference number** in your demand register against the client and the notice. If the matter does not resolve within 30 days, the grievance must reference this number — do not lose it.
6. Review all Section 245 responses at the **partner level** before submission for any demand above ₹5 lakhs. The consequences of an incorrect response — particularly selecting "Demand is correct" when it is not — cannot be easily undone without rectification proceedings or fresh litigation.
