## Section 139(9) Defective Return: Fix & Refile Checklist for CAs

Every August, inboxes light up with Section 139(9) notices. CPC Bengaluru runs its automated validation engine on freshly filed returns and flags anything that doesn't pass muster. If you have more than a handful of individual and business clients, you'll be dealing with at least a few of these every post-filing season — and the 15-day clock starts ticking the moment the notice lands.

This guide cuts straight to what you need to do, in sequence, so no client's return gets treated as never filed.

---

### What Is a Section 139(9) Notice and When Does It Trigger?

Section 139(9) of the Income Tax Act gives CPC the power to declare a return "defective" if it is incomplete, unsigned, or filed without required schedules, supporting tax payment proofs, or audited accounts where an audit is mandatory. The notice is system-generated — no Assessing Officer manually reviews these before they go out — so you will occasionally receive notices that are plainly erroneous.

Once issued, the assessee has 15 days from the notice date to either rectify and refile a corrected return or submit a valid disagreement. If neither action is taken within that window, the original return is deemed to have never been filed — a consequence with serious downstream implications under Section 234F and Section 144.

The 15-day window is not absolute. A written application for extension can be made to the Assessing Officer before the deadline lapses, citing specific reasons. In practice, AOs are generally willing to grant short extensions if the request is made promptly and in good faith.

---

### Complete List of Common Defect Codes and Their Root Causes

The ITD issues specific defect codes, and mapping each code directly to the filing error saves you significant time. You do not need to re-examine the entire return — just look at the code, identify the root cause, and fix it.

| Defect Code / Description | Root Cause in Original Filing | Action Required |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Wrong ITR form selected | Client had capital gains but filed ITR-1; business income reported in individual return instead of ITR-3/4 | Refile using correct ITR form with all applicable schedules |
| TDS schedule incomplete or missing | Schedule TDS1/TDS2 left blank; TDS entries not reconciled with Form 26AS or AIS | Populate TDS schedules from Form 26AS; reconcile with AIS before refiling |
| Tax payable without challan details | Return shows self-assessment tax liability but BSR code, date, or challan amount is missing | Pay outstanding tax, enter challan details (BSR code, date, serial no., amount) correctly |
| P&L / Balance Sheet not furnished | Business or professional income return filed without attaching financial statements in the ITR | Complete Schedule P&L and Balance Sheet; match figures to books |
| Audit report not submitted or not linked | Turnover crosses threshold but Form 3CA/3CB/3CD not filed or not linked to the return on portal | Upload and link the audit report before or during return rectification |
| Advance tax schedule mismatch | Advance tax paid but not entered in the return, or amounts differ from Form 26AS | Correct advance tax schedule entries to match challan and Form 26AS records |
| Exempt income schedule missing | Client has agricultural income, LTCG below threshold, or other exempt income that must be disclosed | Fill the applicable exempt income schedule even if the amount does not affect tax |

Certain defect codes — such as a P&L omission — require a fresh computation of income. Others, like a TDS schedule gap, need only a schedule correction with no change to the final tax figure. Knowing this distinction helps you prioritise effort and communicate realistic timelines to clients.

### 15-Day Rectification Window: Step-by-Step Portal Workflow

Work through this sequence without deviation. Missing any step means the notice remains open, and the system will treat non-response as the return being invalid.

1. Log in to the Income Tax e-filing portal (incometax.gov.in) using the client's PAN credentials or your CA login with the client's authorisation active.
2. Navigate to **e-File → e-Proceedings → View Notices / Orders**, then locate the Section 139(9) notice — filter by AY 2024-25 or AY 2025-26 as applicable — and click **Submit Response**.
3. Review the defect code displayed on screen. Select **"Agree"** if the defect is genuine and you intend to refile a corrected return, or select **"Disagree"** if the notice is erroneous and you have documentary grounds to contest it.
4. If you selected "Agree", prepare and upload the corrected ITR (original return type — revised returns filed under Section 139(5) are different; this is a rectification under the 139(9) response workflow) and submit it through the e-Proceedings interface, not through the standard filing screen.
5. Note the acknowledgement number generated for the corrected return and enter it in the response submission field to formally link the corrected filing to the defect notice.
6. Download and save the final response acknowledgement from the portal — this is your proof that the notice has been addressed and the return has been corrected within the deadline.

Do not file the corrected return independently through the regular filing path and assume the notice is closed. The return must be submitted specifically through the e-Proceedings response workflow, or the defect notice will remain unresolved in CPC's system.

### Pre-Submission Checklist Before Filing the Corrected Return

Before you hit submit on the corrected return, run through these verification steps without shortcuts. A secondary defect on the corrected return restarts the problem and further compresses your client's timeline.

**Form selection** is the first checkpoint. Confirm the ITR form matches the client's income heads, residential status, and audit applicability for the relevant AY. For AY 2025-26, double-check that any new schedule requirements introduced in the updated forms are addressed — ITD has a habit of adding disclosure requirements at the start of each season.

**Schedule completeness** is where most secondary defects originate. Verify that schedules for TDS (TDS1 and TDS2), TCS, advance tax, SFT transactions, and foreign assets are fully populated. Cross-check every TDS entry against Form 26AS and AIS — any mismatch at this stage will generate a fresh processing query even if the 139(9) notice is resolved.

**Tax payment reconciliation** must be done before uploading. Confirm self-assessment tax has been paid in full, and that the challan details — BSR code, date of deposit, challan serial number, and amount — are correctly entered in the return. An unpaid liability or a challan entry error is one of the most common reasons a corrected return itself gets flagged.

Finally, if the original defect involved a missing audit report, confirm the report is uploaded to the portal and the acknowledgement number is linked to the return before submission. A linked audit report that is not properly attached to the return still triggers the defect.

---

### Disagree Path: When and How to Contest a 139(9) Notice

Not every 139(9) notice is valid. CPC's automated system makes errors, and a blanket "agree and refile" approach on every notice wastes client resources and can inadvertently introduce changes to an otherwise correct return.

**Case Scenario:** A client files ITR-3 for AY 2024-25 with complete P&L and Balance Sheet schedules. CPC issues a 139(9) notice citing "P&L not furnished." On examining the original filing, the return is complete and the financial schedules are fully populated — the defect appears to be a CPC processing error, possibly due to a portal glitch during submission. Here, the correct path is to select "Disagree" and submit a written explanation.

The disagreement response must be specific. Cite the defect code shown on the notice, state clearly why the defect does not apply to this return, and reference the original filing acknowledgement number and date. Vague responses like "the return was correctly filed" will not hold up if the matter escalates to the AO.

Keep the following documents ready when submitting a disagreement — the original ITR-V acknowledgement, challan receipts for all tax payments, the audit report with its filing acknowledgement if applicable, and screenshots of the filed schedules. If CPC does not accept the disagreement and the AO takes up the matter, this documentation is your first line of defence.

### Consequences of Missing the 15-Day Deadline and Recovery Options

If the 15-day window lapses without any response, Section 139(9) is unambiguous: the return is treated as if it was never filed. The client is then exposed to late-filing penalties under Section 234F (up to ₹5,000 for returns filed after the due date, or ₹1,000 for total income below ₹5 lakh), and the AO can proceed with a best-judgment assessment under Section 144.

The primary recovery route is a condonation of delay application under Section 119(2)(b), addressed to the Principal Commissioner of Income Tax. The application must demonstrate genuine hardship — illness, bereavement, natural calamity, or a demonstrable portal failure. CBDT circulars set outer time limits for these applications, so file the condonation request as soon as you identify the missed deadline, not after the situation has deteriorated further.

For assessments where the deadline has lapsed but the AO has not yet completed assessment proceedings, a belated return under Section 139(4) may be available depending on the AY. For AY 2024-25, the belated return deadline was 31 December 2024. If that window is also closed, the condonation route under 119(2)(b) is effectively the only path left. Counsel the client early on realistic outcomes — a best-judgment assessment and 234F penalty may still be better resolved proactively than left to fester.

### CA Practice Tips: Preventing 139(9) Notices at Source

The most efficient way to manage 139(9) notices is to generate fewer of them. Every defect notice is a filing error that passed through your pre-submission process undetected — so the fix is systematic, not reactive.

Build an internal pre-filing checklist that mirrors ITD's known defect triggers. Cover form selection logic, schedule completeness, tax reconciliation, audit linkage, and challan entry verification as mandatory steps before every return submission. Run this checklist as a team-level quality gate, not an individual CA's personal preference.

Set up automated AIS and Form 26AS reconciliation in your practice management tool so TDS mismatches are flagged before filing, not after. Most modern CA practice software supports bulk AIS downloads — use that capability to run reconciliation across your entire filing queue at least once a week during the July–September peak season.

Put a calendar reminder 20 days after each return submission to log in and check the e-Proceedings tab for any freshly issued 139(9) notices. CPC typically issues notices within two to four weeks of filing. Catching the notice on day 20 gives you a comfortable buffer to assess, correct, and respond within the 15-day window rather than scrambling on day 13.
