# GST SCN Response: AI Workflow for Show Cause Notices

If your firm handled GST show cause notices manually through FY2023-24, you already know the pain. Notices piled up faster than your team could open the PDFs. The situation is not improving in FY2024-25, and firms still relying on copy-paste responses from older matters are missing deadlines and conceding demands they should be contesting.

This article gives you a practical, repeatable AI-assisted workflow to respond to GST SCNs efficiently, without cutting corners on professional judgement.

---

## Why GST Show Cause Notices Surged After 2023

CBIC's 2023 scrutiny drive was targeted and data-driven. The department ran system-level matching of GSTR-2A against GSTR-3B, flagged ITC mismatches above threshold amounts, and issued bulk SCNs to taxpayers where the divergence exceeded acceptable limits. Fake invoicing networks identified during the special enforcement drive added another wave of Section 74 notices on top of the routine Section 73 demand notices.

SCN volumes rose over 40% in FY2023-24 according to CBIC data. Mid-size CA firms with five to fifteen partners were hit hardest. Large firms had dedicated GST litigation teams; small firms had fewer notices. Mid-size firms faced the volume without the dedicated headcount.

The recurring grounds are predictable: ITC mismatches under GSTR-2A/3B divergence, Rule 86A ITC portal blocks, Section 73 demands for short payment, and Section 74 allegations where the department claims fraud or suppression. Recognising these categories in advance is the first step to building a scalable response system.

## Anatomy of a GST Show Cause Notice

Before you can respond strategically, you need to read the notice correctly. Many junior team members conflate a scrutiny notice under Section 61 with a demand notice under Section 73 or 74. These are fundamentally different instruments requiring completely different response strategies.

The table below maps the common notice types to their key fields, so your team can triage an incoming SCN within minutes of receipt.

| Notice Type | Form | Triggering Section | Response Strategy | Deadline |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Scrutiny of returns | ASMT-10 | Section 61 CGST Act | Furnish clarification with reconciliation data | 30 days from notice |
| Demand notice (non-fraud) | DRC-01 | Section 73 CGST Act | Contest ground or pay with DRC-03; no penalty if paid before order | 30 days under Section 73(9) |
| Demand notice (fraud/suppression) | DRC-01 | Section 74 CGST Act | Require department to establish mens rea; contest vigorously | 30 days; penalty mandatory if fraud upheld |
| ITC mismatch communication | DRC-01C | Rule 88D | Respond with supplier-level reconciliation or pay the difference | 7 days (short window — track carefully) |
| ITC portal block | Rule 86A order | Rule 86A | Challenge vires and seek stay; commissioner-level order required | No fixed statutory form; immediate legal action advisable |

Check the DIN on every notice. An SCN without a valid DIN is non est in law — this is a preliminary objection your AI tool should flag automatically before any substantive response is drafted.

Deadline calculation matters enormously. The 30-day clock under Section 73(9) runs from the date of service, not the date your client forwarded the email to you. Build that service-date tracking into your intake process from day one.

## Step-by-Step AI Workflow to Draft Your SCN Response

This seven-step workflow is designed to fit comfortably within the 30-day window, with a target completion of 12 working days for standard ITC mismatch notices and up to 20 working days for complex Section 74 fraud allegations.

1. Ingest the PDF notice into your AI tool and run structured extraction to pull the DIN, notice form type, demand quantum, assessment period, specific section invoked, and all evidentiary grounds cited by the department. Flag any missing mandatory fields immediately.
2. Classify the notice ground into one of your firm's predefined categories — ITC mismatch, short payment, exempt supply mis-classification, reverse charge non-compliance, or fraud/suppression. This classification drives the entire downstream legal strategy.
3. Map each classified ground to the applicable statutory provision, relevant CBIC circular, and the top three binding precedents from tribunals or High Courts in your jurisdiction. AI performs this mapping in seconds; a junior associate would take two days.
4. Send the AI-generated ground map to the client with a targeted data request. Ask only for the documents that directly address the mapped grounds — supplier GSTR-1 amendments, payment proofs, e-way bills, or contract copies as applicable.
5. Once client data arrives, run a reconciliation comparison between the department's demand figures and the client's actual transaction data. Quantify the genuinely contested amount, the amount explainable by supplier amendments, and any amount where partial liability acceptance may be pragmatic.
6. Generate the structured draft reply using your firm's approved template, populating each paragraph with the mapped case law, circular references, and client-specific reconciliation data from Step 5. The AI produces a complete first draft with annexure index.
7. If partial acceptance is the optimal outcome, generate a DRC-03 payment advisory showing the exact amount, the applicable tax head, and the procedural steps to ensure the voluntary payment extinguishes the penalty exposure under the Section 73 proviso.

This workflow does not eliminate partner review — it compresses the research and drafting time so your partner's attention lands on the judgement calls that actually require experience.

## Mapping Notice Grounds to Case Law and Statutory Provisions

The quality of an SCN response depends almost entirely on how well you match the specific ground in the notice to the strongest available legal defence. Generic responses citing broad principles are dismissed quickly by adjudicating officers. Precise, ground-specific replies backed by recent High Court decisions get dropped at the adjudication stage.

| Notice Ground | Key Statutory Provision | Primary CBIC Circular | Leading Case / Precedent |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| ITC reversal for common inputs (exempt + taxable supply) | Rule 42 / Rule 43 CGST Rules | Circular No. 183/15/2022-GST | Bharat Hari Singhania — proportionate credit on common inputs |
| GSTR-2A vs GSTR-3B mismatch (supplier non-filing) | Section 16(2)(c) CGST Act | Circular No. 183/15/2022-GST | Deivanai Ammal High Court line — recipient cannot be denied ITC for supplier default |
| Section 74 fraud allegation — suppression | Section 74 CGST Act | No specific circular; refer to CBIC enforcement guidelines | Absence of mens rea evidence in SCN itself — challenge at threshold stage |
| Time-barred demand | Section 73(10) / Section 74(10) limitation | Circular No. 31/05/2018-GST | Multiple High Courts on limitation being a jurisdictional bar, not a procedural defence |
| Rule 86A ITC portal block | Rule 86A CGST Rules | No circular; judicial remedy primary | Several High Court stays — block must be preceded by recorded reasons and commissioner sanction |

On Section 74 notices specifically, train your AI to flag one critical issue upfront: does the notice actually contain evidence of fraud, wilful misstatement, or suppression, or does it simply assert these words to invoke the extended five-year limitation period? If the SCN merely uses the language of Section 74 without supporting evidence, your reply should challenge the invocation of Section 74 at the threshold and argue that Section 73 is the applicable provision. This single argument, if successful, reduces the penalty exposure dramatically.

For time-bar defences, have your AI calculate the limitation date automatically from the relevant financial year and compare it against the SCN date. A notice issued even one day after the Section 73(10) deadline is void — this is a jurisdictional objection that takes precedence over all substantive arguments.

## Practical Example: Responding to an ITC Mismatch SCN

**Client Scenario:**

A manufacturing client received a DRC-01 in September 2024 citing an Rs. 18 lakh ITC mismatch across six suppliers for FY2021-22. The demand included tax, interest under Section 50, and a proposed 100% penalty under Section 73. The CA firm had 30 days to respond.

On Day 1, the AI workflow ingested the notice and extracted all six supplier GSTINs and the mismatch amount per supplier. It immediately flagged that the limitation period for FY2021-22 non-fraud demands under Section 73(10) was approaching — a critical contextual alert.

On Day 2, the AI mapped each supplier to their GSTR-1 filing history on the GST portal. Three of the six suppliers had filed amended GSTR-1s after the original mismatch was computed but before the SCN was issued. The AI pulled these amendments and recalculated the actual mismatch at Rs. 3.2 lakh — not Rs. 18 lakh.

By Day 4, the client had confirmed the reconciliation figures and provided bank-confirmed payment records for the three remaining suppliers. The AI draft reply was generated in approximately 4 hours, structured across five sections: preliminary objections, supplier-wise reconciliation annexure, legal grounds for ITC entitlement, computation of the genuinely disputed amount, and a DRC-03 advisory for the Rs. 3.2 lakh where the documentation was thin.

The partner reviewed, revised two paragraphs on the Section 50 interest calculation, and signed off. The reply was filed on Day 12 — 18 days before the deadline. The firm's previous manual benchmark for a comparable notice was three full working days of associate time just to produce a first draft, typically reaching the partner on Day 20 or later.

## Compliance Guardrails: What AI Cannot Replace

AI compresses research and drafting time — it does not replace professional judgement. There are specific checkpoints where CA sign-off is not optional.

Client-specific contractual arrangements and industry-specific exemption notifications are frequently absent from the public datasets on which general AI tools are trained. A pharmaceutical company claiming ITC on promotional samples, or a real estate developer calculating ITC reversal under Rule 42, will have fact-specific positions that require the CA to manually review the client's agreements and the applicable sectoral notification.

The most dangerous misstep in SCN responses is an inadvertent admission of partial liability that estops your client from taking a contrary position in appeal. If a reply acknowledges that Rs. 5 lakh of ITC was "incorrectly claimed" to secure a partial demand drop, that admission will be cited against you if the client appeals the residual demand. This risk calculus requires a senior partner's explicit sign-off — no AI tool should be authorised to include admissions without human review.

All case citations generated by AI must be verified against the official GST Council portal, Manupatra, or SCC Online before the reply is filed. Citing an overruled decision, or worse, a hallucinated citation, is a professional hazard that could damage your firm's credibility before an adjudicating officer you will appear before again.

## Setting Up AI-Assisted SCN Response in Your CA Practice

Implementation does not require a technology transformation programme. These steps will get your firm operational within a few weeks.

1. Connect your document management system to a notice ingestion workflow so every inbound SCN — whether received by email, from the GST portal, or uploaded by a client — automatically triggers a deadline tracker entry and an alert to the responsible partner. Manual deadline tracking in spreadsheets is the single biggest compliance risk in SCN work.
2. Build a firm-specific prompt library covering your top 10 recurring notice grounds. Write these prompts collaboratively with your senior litigation associates, since the best prompts encode your firm's preferred argumentative structure and the jurisdictional preferences of your local GSTAT and High Court benches.
3. Create a standardised review checklist for partners — covering DIN validity, limitation period check, mens rea assessment for Section 74 notices, and admission risk review — so that every AI-drafted response passes through identical quality gates regardless of which team member prepared the underlying draft.
4. After each SCN matter closes, log the outcome: demand dropped entirely, demand reduced, demand upheld at adjudication, or matter gone to appeal. Tag the notice ground and the primary argument that succeeded.
5. Use this outcome data to rank your case law mapping. Arguments that consistently secure dropped demands should be prioritised in your prompt library; arguments that are routinely rejected should be flagged for review or retired.
6. Review your prompt library and case law database quarterly. CBIC issues new circulars regularly, and High Court decisions on ITC entitlement and limitation are flowing in rapidly in FY2024-25. A prompt library that was accurate six months ago may be citing superseded positions today.

## Your Next Step

Pull the three oldest unresolved GST SCNs currently sitting in your firm's inbox. For each one, identify the notice form type, the specific section invoked, and the deadline date. If any deadline falls within the next 15 days, that is your immediate priority.

Run those three notices through the seven-step workflow above — even if you do it manually the first time — and compare the time taken against your previous benchmark. Once you have that data, you have the business case to invest in dedicated AI tooling and a structured prompt library.

The firms that build this capability now will handle three times the SCN volume in FY2025-26 without adding headcount. The firms that do not will keep losing the race against the 30-day clock.
