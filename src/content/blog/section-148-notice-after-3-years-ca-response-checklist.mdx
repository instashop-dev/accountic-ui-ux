If your client walked in this morning with a Section 148 notice dated more than three years after the end of the relevant assessment year, do not treat it as routine. These notices carry a significantly higher legal burden for the Assessing Officer, and a large proportion of them are jurisdictionally vulnerable from day one. Your job in the first 48 hours is not to start drafting a return — it is to build a challenge framework.

This checklist is designed for practising CAs handling reassessment matters post-Finance Act 2021. Every step assumes you are working under time pressure with a client who is already anxious.

---

## Why Section 148 Notices Beyond 3 Years Are Legally Vulnerable

The Finance Act 2021 fundamentally restructured the reassessment regime, and many Assessing Officers are still issuing notices as if the old Section 147/148 framework is intact. That gap between how the law reads and how the department operates is where your client's defence lives.

### The Old vs. New Framework

Under the pre-2021 regime, the AO needed "reason to believe" that income had escaped assessment, and the limitation period extended up to six years in certain cases. The Finance Act 2021 replaced this with a two-tier structure: Section 148A mandates a mandatory pre-notice inquiry and show-cause procedure, and Section 148 can only be issued after a Section 148A(d) order is passed. The limitation period was simultaneously compressed — three years became the standard ceiling, with a hard outer limit of ten years for specific high-value cases.

### The Twin Conditions for Notices Beyond 3 Years

For a Section 148 notice to be legally sustainable beyond three years from the end of the relevant assessment year, **both** of the following must be satisfied simultaneously:

1. The escaped income must be **₹50 lakhs or more** — not suspected, not estimated, but supported by material on record.
2. There must be **tangible material** in the AO's possession — not inference, not data mining, not a vague tip from an informant.

If either condition fails, the notice is beyond jurisdiction. The burden of establishing both conditions rests entirely with the AO, not with your client.

### Supreme Court and High Court Precedents That Matter

The Supreme Court's ruling in **Union of India v. Ashish Agarwal (2022)** was a watershed moment. The Court upheld the validity of reassessment notices issued under the old regime but directed that they be treated as notices under Section 148A(b), effectively giving the new procedure retrospective application. Several High Courts — including Delhi, Bombay, and Gujarat — have since used this ruling as the foundation for striking down notices where the AO failed to satisfy the twin conditions for extended-period assessments.

Keep a citation file. New High Court judgments on this issue are emerging almost monthly, and an authority that was not available when you filed the reply may be exactly what you need at the writ stage.

### Common AO Errors That Create Immediate Jurisdictional Grounds

- Issuing Section 148 directly without completing the Section 148A process
- Relying on Annual Information Statement (AIS) data mismatches without independent verification
- Stating escaped income figures without specifying the underlying transaction or source
- Mechanical 148A(d) orders that do not engage with the client's reply
- Notices issued to the wrong jurisdictional AO or under a wrong PAN mapping

Any one of these is a live jurisdictional ground. Document each one from the moment you receive the notice.

## Immediate Triage: First 48 Hours After Receiving the Notice

The 30-day reply window sounds comfortable until you realise that the first two days are the most consequential. Every legal option you want to preserve downstream — writ petition, stay application, procedural challenge — depends on what you document in this window.

### Step 1: Verify Date of Issue vs. Date of Service

The limitation period runs from the **date of issue** of the notice, not the date your client received it. Pull the notice date from the header, cross-check it against the ITBA portal record, and immediately calculate the exact assessment year implicated. A notice dated 31 March 2024 for AY 2020-21 is exactly at the three-year boundary — any date after that is beyond the standard limit and triggers the extended-period conditions.

### Step 2: Confirm the 148A Compliance Trail

Log into the Income Tax e-filing portal under the client's credentials and check the notices section. You should find:

- A Section 148A(b) show-cause notice predating the Section 148 notice
- The client's reply (or a record that no reply was filed)
- The Section 148A(d) order from the AO

If any of these are missing for a non-search case, you have a procedural invalidity argument that is strong enough to take to the High Court on its own.

### Step 3: DIN Verification on ITBA Portal

Every faceless notice must carry a valid **Document Identification Number (DIN)**. The CBDT circular requiring DIN on all communications is unambiguous — a notice without a valid DIN or issued with a manually generated number (outside the system) is void. Check the DIN on the portal before doing anything else. Courts have consistently held invalid DIN notices to be non-est in law.

### Step 4: Identify Who Issued the Notice

Determine whether the notice came from the **National Faceless Assessment Centre (NFAC)** or a jurisdictional Assessing Officer. Post-2021, most reassessments are supposed to run through the faceless mechanism. A notice issued by a jurisdictional AO in cases that should have been faceless is another ground — note the issuing authority on your triage sheet.

### Step 5: Preserve the Evidentiary Record Immediately

Instruct your client to do nothing — absolutely nothing — with original documents until you have secured copies of:

- The original ITR for the relevant assessment year with acknowledgment
- Form 26AS and the AIS/TIS for that year
- Bank statements corresponding to the transactions flagged (if identifiable from the notice)
- Any prior assessment orders, scrutiny notices, or correspondence for that year

If the AO later claims fresh tangible material, your preserved record of what was originally filed is your first line of counter-evidence.

## Section 148A Compliance Check: Was Due Process Followed?

Section 148A is not a formality. It is a jurisdictional prerequisite. If the AO did not complete the four-step process correctly, the Section 148 notice that follows is void ab initio — not merely voidable.

### The Four-Step Process You Must Verify

| Step | Section | What Should Have Happened | What to Check |
|------|---------|--------------------------|---------------|
| 1 | 148A(a) | AO conducts inquiry with prior approval of specified authority | Was inquiry approval obtained? Is it on record? |
| 2 | 148A(b) | Show-cause notice issued to assessee with reasons | Was notice received? Does it specify the exact escaped income? |
| 3 | — | Assessee given minimum 7 days to reply | Was the reply window actually 7 days or more? |
| 4 | 148A(d) | AO passes a speaking order considering the reply | Does the order address your client's reply specifically? |

A "speaking order" under 148A(d) is not a template printout with the client's name inserted. It must engage with the specific contentions raised in the reply. If your client replied to the 148A(b) notice and the 148A(d) order reads like a form letter, that order is legally insufficient and the Section 148 notice built on it is challengeable.

### The 7-Day Minimum Reply Window

If the 148A(b) notice gave your client fewer than 7 days to respond, this is a statutory violation. Document the dates precisely. Courts have found this ground sufficient to invalidate notices even when the AO had substantive material for reassessment.

### Search Case Exceptions — and How AOs Misuse Them

Section 148A does **not** apply in cases covered under Section 153A or 153C (search and seizure cases). Some AOs have attempted to invoke this exception even where no valid search was conducted on the assessee or their related persons, effectively bypassing the 148A process. If your client was not the subject of a search, or if the search was more than four years old when the notice was issued, scrutinise this exception claim carefully. The exception must be explicitly and correctly invoked in the notice itself.

## Limitation Period Verification: Computing the 3-Year and 10-Year Clocks

Limitation calculation under the post-2021 framework sounds straightforward but has several landmines, particularly around COVID-era extension orders. Get this calculation right before you do anything else.

### Base Rule: 3-Year Window

For escaped income below ₹50 lakhs, the AO can only issue a Section 148 notice within **3 years from the end of the relevant assessment year**. Assessment year ends on 31 March of the relevant year. The three-year clock runs from that date.

**Example:** For AY 2021-22 (which ended 31 March 2022), a notice must be issued by 31 March 2025. A notice dated 1 April 2025 is one day out of time and void.

### Extended Limit: The 10-Year Window

For notices beyond 3 years, the AO must affirmatively establish that the escaped income is ₹50 lakhs or more. This is not a soft condition. The escaped income figure must be based on identifiable material — not computed by inference or extrapolation — and must clear the ₹50 lakh threshold before the extended window even opens. If the AO's stated escaped income is ₹48 lakhs, the extended window does not apply, full stop.

### Assessment Year Mapping Table (Post-Ashish Agarwal)

| Assessment Year | End of AY | 3-Year Limit | 10-Year Limit |
|----------------|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| 2013-14 | 31 Mar 2014 | 31 Mar 2017 | 31 Mar 2024 |
| 2014-15 | 31 Mar 2015 | 31 Mar 2018 | 31 Mar 2025 |
| 2015-16 | 31 Mar 2016 | 31 Mar 2019 | 31 Mar 2026 |
| 2016-17 | 31 Mar 2017 | 31 Mar 2020 | 31 Mar 2027 |
| 2017-18 | 31 Mar 2018 | 31 Mar 2021 | 31 Mar 2028 |
| 2018-19 | 31 Mar 2019 | 31 Mar 2022 | 31 Mar 2029 |
| 2019-20 | 31 Mar 2020 | 31 Mar 2023 | 31 Mar 2030 |
| 2020-21 | 31 Mar 2021 | 31 Mar 2024 | 31 Mar 2031 |
| 2021-22 | 31 Mar 2022 | 31 Mar 2025 | 31 Mar 2032 |

*Note: These are base calculations without COVID extension overlays. Verify extensions separately for each AY.*

### COVID-Era Extension Orders: What Survived the Supreme Court

The CBDT issued multiple extension notifications during 2020-2021 citing COVID. The Supreme Court in **Ashish Agarwal** and related proceedings validated some of these extensions but imposed conditions. In particular, time-barred notices that were issued between 1 April 2021 and 30 June 2021 under the old regime were given a fresh start under the new 148A framework — but only if the assessee was given an opportunity to respond. Any notice issued outside these specific windows, or for AYs where even the extended limitation had expired, remains challengeable. Do not assume a COVID extension automatically saves the notice. Map the specific AY against the specific extension order that the AO is relying on.

## Analyzing the 'Reason to Believe' and Tangible Material Requirement

Even if the notice is within the limitation period and the 148A process was correctly followed, you can still challenge jurisdiction if the AO's "reason to believe" is legally insufficient. For extended-period notices, this is your most powerful substantive ground.

### How to Obtain the Reasons Recorded

Your client has a statutory right to inspect the file and obtain the reasons recorded by the AO. Submit a written application on letter-head addressed to the Assessing Officer immediately after receiving the Section 148 notice. Request:

1. A copy of the reasons recorded for issuing the notice
2. The material on record that constitutes "tangible material" for reassessment
3. The prior approval order from the specified authority (PCCIT/CCIT as applicable)

If the AO refuses to provide these, document the refusal. Courts have treated non-disclosure of reasons as itself vitiating the reassessment proceedings.

### What "Tangible Material" Actually Means

The Supreme Court in **CIT v. Kelvinator of India** (and reaffirmed in multiple post-2021 judgments) held that "reason to believe" requires tangible material — something that can be objectively verified, not merely a change of opinion or a re-read of material already on record during original assessment.

**Legally insufficient as standalone material:**
- A third-party statement that has not been put to the assessee
- Unverified tip from an informant
- Raw AIR/AIS data showing a transaction without verification of whether it was returned
- A difference between ITR income and Form 26AS that the assessee had already explained during filing

**Potentially sufficient (subject to scrutiny):**
- A specific bank transaction not appearing anywhere in the filed return, with documentary proof
- A statement recorded under Section 131 that directly implicates the assessee
- Survey findings under Section 133A with corroborating material

### Change of Opinion vs. Fresh Material: The Critical Distinction

If the item now being flagged was already part of the original assessment — whether or not the AO examined it — reopening on the same material is a change of opinion, which is not permissible. This is particularly relevant in scrutiny cases. If your client's AY 2018-19 return was picked up for scrutiny and the AO passed an assessment order, and the current notice relates to a transaction that was already on the assessment record, document this overlap meticulously. The section 148 notice in that scenario is on very thin jurisdictional ground.

### Red-Flag Reasons: Categories Most Likely to Be Legally Weak

| Reason Category | Legal Vulnerability | What to Check |
|----------------|--------------------|--------------  |
| Low ITR-to-AIR mismatch | High — AIR data alone is not tangible material | Was the transaction returned? Under which head? |
| Cash deposits below ₹10 lakh | High — below SFT reporting threshold, inference-based | Was it returned as income or explained in ITR? |
| Unverified SFT data | High — requires independent corroboration | Has the AO verified with the reporting entity? |
| Third-party statement | Medium-High — must be put to assessee first | Was your client given a copy and opportunity to respond? |
| Survey findings on related party | Medium — depends on nexus established | Is there a direct link to your client's income? |

### The Jurisdictional Analysis Memo

Prepare a written memo for your own client file analyzing each of the above dimensions. This memo serves two purposes: it disciplines your own thinking, and it becomes the foundation for the writ petition if proceedings need to be escalated. Date and sign the memo. Record what material you reviewed, what conclusions you reached, and what advice you gave. If the client later takes a different decision, this memo protects you professionally.

## Drafting the Reply to Section 148 Notice: Structure and Strategy

This is where many CAs make the single most damaging mistake: filing the return first and raising objections later. Do not do this. Filing the return first is an implicit concession that the notice is valid.

### The Golden Rule: Jurisdiction Before Substance

Your reply must lead with jurisdictional objections. If you mix jurisdictional objections with substantive disclosures in the same document, courts have sometimes treated the substantive disclosures as evidence of participation in the proceedings — which weakens the jurisdictional challenge. Structure the reply so that Part I is entirely about why the notice should not have been issued, and Part II (only if needed) deals with substantive responses.

### Reply Structure Framework

**Part I — Preliminary Objections (Jurisdictional)**

1. *Limitation:* "The notice dated [date] pertains to AY [year]. The relevant assessment year ended on 31 March [year]. The notice has been issued beyond the three-year limitation period prescribed under Section 149(1)(b) of the Income Tax Act, 1961 as amended by the Finance Act 2021. The escaped income alleged does not satisfy the ₹50 lakh threshold required to invoke the extended limitation period."

2. *Procedural Invalidity:* "The notice is vitiated by non-compliance with the mandatory procedure prescribed under Section 148A. Specifically, [the 148A(d) order does not address the contentions raised in our reply / the 7-day minimum reply window was not provided / the 148A(a) inquiry approval is not on record]."

3. *Absence of Tangible Material:* "The reasons recorded by the Assessing Officer, as obtained by us vide our letter dated [date], do not constitute tangible material as required by the law. The material relied upon is [AIS data / third-party statement / AIR mismatch], which does not independently establish escapement of income."

4. *Legal Citations:* Cite Ashish Agarwal, Kelvinator of India, and any recent High Court judgment in your jurisdiction directly on the section 148 limitation point.

**Part II — Without Prejudice Submissions (if required)**

Only include this section if you have made a strategic decision to file a return or disclose facts without prejudice to the jurisdictional objections. Open this section with: *"Without prejudice to the foregoing preliminary objections, which are specifically preserved, the following submissions are made purely in the alternative."*

### When to File the Return "Without Prejudice"

This is a strategic call, not a rule. File without prejudice if:
- The client has a completely clean return with no exposure on the flagged items
- You want to demonstrate cooperation to prevent coercive action before a writ hearing
- The jurisdictional objection is strong but not airtight, and you want to reduce the risk of ex-parte assessment

Do not file without prejudice if:
- Filing would require disclosing transactions that are genuinely problematic
- The jurisdictional objection is so strong that concession of any kind is unnecessary

### What to Avoid in the Reply

Never use language that can be read as accepting the AO's jurisdiction. Avoid phrases like "we submit the following details as required" or "in compliance with your notice." Every sentence should be framed as a response made under protest and without prejudice to your jurisdictional challenge.

## Post-Reply Workflow: From AO Response to Appellate Strategy

Filing the reply is not the end of your work — it is the beginning of the next phase. What the AO does next will determine which appellate track you use.

### Workflow: After Filing the Reply

```
Reply Filed
    │
    ├─► AO Accepts Objection → Proceedings dropped → Document closure
    │
    └─► AO Rejects Objection (passes order u/s 148A(d) or ignores objection)
              │
              ├─► Writ Petition (Article 226) — High Court
              │         │
              │         └─► Apply for interim stay on assessment proceedings
              │
              └─► Allow assessment to proceed
                        │
                        └─► Assessment order passed
                                  │
                                  └─► Appeal to CIT(A) → ITAT → HC
```

### High Court Writ vs. Waiting for Assessment

The writ petition route is generally faster and more effective when:
- The jurisdictional ground is clean (clear limitation breach, clear 148A non-compliance)
- There is a risk of coercive recovery action
- High Court has recent favourable precedent directly on your point

Wait for assessment and appeal to CIT(A) when:
- The jurisdictional objection is arguable but not clear-cut
- There are substantive income disclosures that need to be examined anyway
- The escaped income figure means a favourable assessment is achievable

In our experience, clean limitation breaches — especially where the notice is even one day beyond the statutory period — should go directly to the High Court. Do not give the department a second opportunity to cure a limitation defect through an assessment order.

### Interim Stay Strategy

When filing the writ petition, simultaneously file an application for ad-interim stay of the assessment proceedings. Your stay application must establish:
1. **Prima facie case:** The jurisdictional ground is clearly arguable
2. **Balance of convenience:** Assessment proceedings would cause irreversible prejudice
3. **Irreparable harm:** If assessment proceeds, demands, penalties, and recovery action follow automatically

Attach the notice, the reply, and the rejection order as exhibits. Courts have been granting stays in well-pleaded Section 148 writ petitions with considerable regularity post-2021.

### CIT(A) Appeal: What to Preserve

If you are going through the assessment route, ensure the following are specifically preserved as grounds in the CIT(A) appeal:
- All jurisdictional objections raised in the Section 148 reply
- Objection to the AO's rejection of preliminary objections
- Objection to reliance on each specific piece of tangible material
- Any procedural violations during the assessment proceedings

Fee calculation for CIT(A) is based on assessed income — confirm the correct fee slab before filing. Use the e-filing portal under the "Pending Actions" tab for CIT(A) submission. Attach all documents contemporaneously rather than relying on supplementary filings later.

### Build Your Citation Bank

Maintain a running document — I call it a Section 148 Precedent Tracker — with the following columns: Court, Case Name, Date, Key Holding, AY Involved, Link/Citation. Update it every two weeks. Judgments from Delhi HC, Bombay HC, and Gujarat HC have been the most active post-2021, and a judgment from March 2024 may be exactly what you need for a hearing in June 2024.

## Client Communication and Documentation Protocol

Section 148 matters test the CA-client relationship more than most engagements. Clients panic. They want to "just file something and settle it." Your job is to hold the line professionally while keeping the client informed and confident.

### Engagement Letter Addendum

For every Section 148 matter, issue a separate engagement addendum that covers:
- **Scope:** Specifically list what is included (notice reply, 148A analysis, writ support if needed) and what is not (criminal proceedings, PMLA matters)
- **Fees:** Specify the stage-wise fee structure — initial triage fee, reply drafting fee, separate fee if writ petition escalation is needed
- **Limitation of liability:** State clearly that your advice is based on documents provided by the client, and that undisclosed transactions create risks that are outside the scope of your engagement
- **Client obligations:** The client must provide complete information and must not take any unilateral action (filing a return, responding directly to the AO) without your approval

### Explaining the "Do Not File Return" Strategy

Most clients will instinctively want to file the return immediately to "show compliance." Explain it this way: *"Filing the return right now is like accepting a traffic challan before contesting whether the police officer had jurisdiction to stop you in the first place. We have strong legal grounds here. Filing first weakens those grounds unnecessarily."*

Use an analogy, not legal jargon. Then follow up with a written note to the client confirming the advice given and the client's acknowledged understanding. This protects you if the client later second-guesses the strategy.

### Document Retention Checklist

Given the 10-year reassessment window, your clients need to retain the following for at least 10 years from the end of the relevant AY:

- [ ] Original ITR filing with acknowledgment
- [ ] All supporting computation sheets
- [ ] Form 26AS and AIS/TIS for the year
- [ ] Bank statements and investment records
- [ ] Any prior assessment correspondence for that year
- [ ] All Section 148 / 148A notices received
- [ ] Your firm's replies and accompanying evidence
- [ ] 148A(d) order and assessment order if passed

Advise digital preservation in at least two locations. Hard drives fail; cloud backups with version history are the minimum standard.
