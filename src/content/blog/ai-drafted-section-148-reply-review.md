An AI-drafted section 148 reply needs five specific checks before it leaves the reviewer's desk: jurisdiction and sanction, citation integrity, the match between the reply and the AO's recorded reasons, the 148A(b) procedural thread, and the prayer. Each check takes under five minutes. None is optional. Together they take 25–35 minutes — irreducible, and the floor below which an AI-drafted s. 148 reply should not be filed.

This post is the section-specific spoke under our cross-cutting note on the [five failure modes of AI-drafted notice replies](/blog/ai-drafted-notice-replies-failure-modes). Use the cluster index for the wider failure-mode survey. Use this post when the notice in front of you is a section 148, the AI draft is on screen, and the reply has to ship by Friday.

## The five checks

> 1. **Jurisdiction and sanction.** Was the s. 151 sanction obtained from the correct rank, and are the recorded reasons attached?
> 2. **Citation integrity.** Every case, section, and circular cited is real, current, and on the proposition stated.
> 3. **Reasons-recorded match.** The reply answers the AO's specific recorded reasons — not similar-sounding ones.
> 4. **The 148A(b) thread.** The procedural steps prior to the s. 148 issuance are reconciled in the reply.
> 5. **The prayer.** Specific relief, mapped paragraph-by-paragraph to the grounds raised.

Each check below assumes the AI draft is already substantively reasonable. The checks are about what the draft does *not* know — the jurisdictional record, the procedural file, the AO's specific reasons, and the prayer-to-grounds mapping that only a human reviewer can produce.

## Check 1 — Jurisdiction and sanction

The post-Finance Act 2021 framework requires that any s. 148 notice be preceded by a sanction under s. 151, obtained from the prescribed authority at the correct rank — Joint Commissioner / Additional Commissioner for some cases, Commissioner-rank or higher for others, depending on the time elapsed since the end of the relevant assessment year and the nature of the information.

An AI draft will almost always *mention* sanction. What it will frequently miss is whether the sanction in the specific notice in front of you is from the correct rank under the framework that applied at the time the notice was issued. The framework has been amended several times since 2021; older drafts cite older versions.

The check, in practice:

1. Open the notice and the recorded reasons.
2. Identify the rank of the sanctioning authority.
3. Identify the date of the sanction and the date of the s. 148 issuance.
4. Match against the s. 151 framework that was in force on the sanction date.

If the rank is wrong for the framework, that is a paragraph-1 jurisdictional objection — and one that, in our experience, succeeds on the merits more often than the substantive grounds do. The AI draft will not flag it. The reviewer must.

For the broader argument structure where this objection sits, see [replying to a section 148 notice — the template that holds up](/blog/section-148-reply-template).

## Check 2 — Citation integrity

This check is structural, not editorial. Every citation in the draft — case, section, circular, instruction — must be verified against an authoritative source before the draft moves forward.

Patterns to look for in s. 148 drafts specifically:

- **Fabricated High Court judgments on reassessment.** Common shape: a credible-sounding name, an ITR or SCC citation in correct format, a proposition that is exactly what the draft needs. Cross-check on Indian Kanoon. If the case does not return a judgment, it does not exist.
- **Citations to *CIT v. Kelvinator of India* for propositions Kelvinator did not stand for.** *Kelvinator* is the change-of-opinion bar. It is *not* the rational-nexus standard for *"reason to believe"* — that line of authority traces to *ITO v. Lakhmani Mewal Das*. An AI draft that cites *Kelvinator* for the rational-nexus proposition is using the wrong case.
- **Section 147A and other inventions.** There is no s. 147A. The reassessment framework is sections 147, 148, 148A, and 149. Drafts that cite *"147A"* need correction, every time.
- **Pre-2021 CBDT reassessment circulars cited as if they still applied.** The Finance Act 2021 superseded much of the prior procedural guidance. Older circulars need to be checked for current applicability before they are relied upon.

A reply that survives the citation check has, at minimum, a verified factual foundation. A reply that doesn't is a reply that gets attacked by the AO at first reading and on appeal. For the underlying case authority on the rational-nexus standard — and how to deploy it without it reading boilerplate — see our note on [why ITO v. Lakhmani Mewal Das still decides your s. 148 reply](/blog/lakhmani-mewal-das-s148).

## Check 3 — Reasons-recorded match

Section 148 proceedings have a peculiar discipline: the *reasons recorded* by the AO are the document the reply must answer. Not the section. Not the assessment year. The specific reasons.

An AI draft has read the notice. It has not always read the reasons recorded — sometimes the reasons are not part of the notice PDF the draft was generated from, and sometimes the draft generalises from the notice text alone. The result is a reply that addresses similar-sounding objections instead of the AO's specific recorded reasons.

The check: open the recorded reasons and the AI draft side-by-side. For every reason the AO recorded, identify the paragraph in the reply that addresses it. If a reason has no paragraph, the reply has a gap. If a paragraph addresses a reason that wasn't recorded, the paragraph is wasted ammunition that telegraphs the appeal strategy and gives the AO an unprovoked head-start.

This check is also where the application-of-mind doctrine bites hardest on the assessee's side. A reply that answers the wrong objections is a reply that signals to the AO — and, if it goes there, the ITAT — that no one read the reasons. For more on the doctrine, see [application of mind in faceless assessment](/blog/faceless-assessment-application-of-mind).

## Check 4 — The 148A(b) thread

Under the post-Finance Act 2021 framework, a s. 148 notice is preceded by a 148A(b) show-cause and the assessee's response, then a 148A(d) order, then the s. 148 itself. Each step generates a document. Each document is part of the procedural thread the reply must reconcile.

An AI draft will, often, treat the s. 148 in isolation — answering the notice without acknowledging the 148A(b) reply already on record, or contradicting positions taken in that earlier reply. This is a credibility failure: the AO has the earlier reply, and the inconsistency is visible in the case file.

The check is mechanical:

1. List the procedural steps prior to the s. 148: 148A(b) show-cause, your reply, the 148A(d) order, the s. 148 issuance.
2. Identify any positions taken in the earlier reply that the AI draft now contradicts or omits.
3. Reconcile — either by acknowledging the earlier position and explaining the change, or by removing the contradiction.

A reply that contradicts the assessee's own 148A(b) response is a reply that hands the AO a clean paragraph of the assessment order.

## Check 5 — The prayer

The prayer is the paragraph the AO reads first. It is also the paragraph AI drafts treat with the least specificity — generic *"the proceedings be dropped"* or *"the addition not be made"* without mapping to the grounds.

A useful prayer specifies, ground by ground, what is being sought:

- *On Ground 1 (jurisdictional): the proceedings be dropped on the basis that the s. 151 sanction was obtained from a rank below the prescribed authority for the relevant time-window under s. 149.*
- *On Ground 2 (substantive): no addition be made under s. 68 in respect of the cash credits aggregating ₹14,70,000, on the basis of the bank reconciliations and confirmations enclosed at Annexure 4.*
- *In the alternative: any addition be limited to the unreconciled balance of ₹X, with credit for the reconciled portion.*

The check, then: does the prayer paragraph specify the relief, mapped to the grounds, with the alternatives clearly stated? If not, the AO will infer the assessee's position; an inference is more likely to go against the assessee than a specification.

A practical discipline: re-write the prayer last, after the other four checks are clean. The grounds change during review; the prayer must change with them. AI drafts often generate the prayer in parallel with the body and don't re-sync after edits. Re-writing the prayer last is the cheapest way to keep the two aligned.

## How the review compresses

At DPS &amp; Co., the five checks together take 25–35 minutes on a routine s. 148 reply. The breakdown:

| Check | Typical time |
|---|---|
| 1 — Jurisdiction and sanction | 5 minutes |
| 2 — Citation integrity | 10 minutes |
| 3 — Reasons-recorded match | 5 minutes |
| 4 — The 148A(b) thread | 5 minutes |
| 5 — The prayer | 3 minutes |

The total is irreducible. It is also small compared to the cost of an adverse order. Adverse orders on s. 148 carry through to penalty proceedings, recovery, and frequently to the ITAT — the downstream cost of skipping a 30-minute review is several orders of magnitude larger than the review itself.

## FAQ

**Can these five checks be automated?**
Citation integrity (Check 2) can and should be — it is the most mechanical, the most error-prone if done manually, and the most consequential when missed. The other four require CA judgment on a specific procedural file and don't generalise to a deterministic check. Accountic automates Check 2 with a verifiable audit log; the other four remain a CA's responsibility, by design.

**What's the difference between Check 3 and Check 4?**
Check 3 — *reasons-recorded match* — is about *substance*: does the reply answer the AO's specific objections? Check 4 — *the 148A(b) thread* — is about *consistency*: does the reply align with positions the assessee has already taken in the earlier procedural steps? Both are pass / fail; both have to clear.

**Should I disclose the use of AI tooling in the reply itself?**
The reply is filed under the CA's signature; the operational unit of trust is the CA's professional judgment, not the drafting workflow. Disclosure to the AO inside the reply is unnecessary. Disclosure to the *client*, in the engagement letter, is a separate matter — and one we treat as standard practice for any AI-assisted work under the [DPDP framework](/blog/dpdp-2023-ca-firms-checklist).

**Does the prayer need to be drafted last?**
Yes. The prayer maps to the grounds; if the grounds change during review, the prayer changes with them. AI drafts often generate the prayer in parallel with the body and don't re-sync after edits. Re-write the prayer last, after the other four checks are clean.

**What if Check 2 (citation integrity) flags a fabricated case?**
Remove the case and the proposition it was supporting. Do not search for a *"similar"* case to substitute — the substitution often introduces a second error in the same paragraph. If the proposition matters, find a real case for it from a verified source. If the proposition was decorative, drop it entirely.

**Are these five checks specific to s. 148, or do they generalise?**
The five-check structure generalises — every notice section needs jurisdiction, citation integrity, reasons-match, procedural-thread, and prayer review. The *content* of each check is section-specific. We are publishing parallel checklists for s. 143(2), s. 142(1), s. 139(9), and s. 245 in the same cluster.
