Section 143(2) notices land in your client's inbox without warning — and the clock starts ticking immediately. As the CA on record, you have a narrow window to verify the notice, gather documents, draft a legally sound response, and protect your client's position without overexposing them.

This is not a checklist you build on the fly. This is the checklist you should already have, ready to deploy the moment you receive that email or portal notification.

---

## What Triggers a Section 143(2) Notice

The Assessing Officer issues a Section 143(2) notice when the department decides a return warrants closer examination before it is processed under Section 143(1). The statutory constraint is clear: the AO **must serve this notice within 6 months from the end of the financial year in which the return was filed**. A notice served even one day late is time-barred and can be challenged.

Common triggers fall into predictable categories. High-value cash deposits (especially post-demonetisation patterns that are still being scrutinised), mismatches between the return and Form 26AS / AIS / TIS, foreign asset or income disclosures under Schedule FA or Schedule FSI, and AIR (Annual Information Return) flags from registrars, mutual funds, or banks — all of these push a return into the scrutiny queue.

**Know whether it's limited or complete scrutiny — this determines your entire response strategy.**

| Scrutiny Type | Scope | What AO Can Examine |
|---|---|---|
| Limited Scrutiny (CASS) | Restricted to specific issues mentioned in notice | Only the flagged issue(s) — nothing else |
| Complete Scrutiny | Full examination | All heads of income, deductions, and assets |
| Manual Scrutiny | Specific high-risk cases | As specified by CBDT guidelines for that year |

Under the Faceless Assessment Scheme 2019 (amended in 2021), most Section 143(2) notices are now issued and processed through the National Faceless Assessment Centre (NaFAC). There is no physical AO interaction. All submissions go through the e-Filing portal, and responses must be submitted digitally. If you are still treating this like a conventional assessment where you "drop by the ward" — stop.

## Decoding the Notice: What to Check Within 24 Hours

The first 24 hours are not for drafting responses. They are for verification. A defective notice is a gift — but only if you catch it in time.

**Step 1: Verify the DIN.**
Every notice issued on or after 1 October 2019 must carry a Document Identification Number (DIN) as per CBDT Circular 19/2019. If the DIN is absent, or if it does not appear in the DIN directory on the e-Filing portal, the notice has no legal standing. Raise this ground immediately — in writing, via the portal — before doing anything else.

**Step 2: Confirm PAN and Assessment Year.**
This sounds elementary but errors happen. Verify that the notice is addressed to your client's correct PAN and the correct Assessment Year. A notice for AY 2023-24 cannot be used to reopen issues from AY 2022-23. Also confirm that your client actually filed a return for the relevant AY.

**Step 3: Identify limited vs. complete scrutiny scope.**
Read the notice text carefully. If it says "the case has been selected for limited scrutiny on the issue of [specific transaction]" — that boundary is legally significant. The AO cannot expand the scope during proceedings without converting to complete scrutiny through a separate process. Document this restriction in your engagement file.

**Step 4: Authenticate the notice on the portal.**
Log in to the Income Tax e-Filing portal. Navigate to **Pending Actions > Response to Outstanding Demand** or **e-Proceedings > View Notices**. Confirm the notice visible on the portal matches the one received. If there is a discrepancy — for example, a notice received by email that does not appear on the portal — treat it with caution and seek written clarification before responding.

**Step 5: Map your internal milestones backward from the compliance date.**
Note the compliance date specified in the notice. Work backward: set your internal document collection deadline at least 10 days prior, your draft response review at 5 days prior, and your final submission at 2 days prior. Build this into your practice tracker the same day.

## Critical Deadlines CA Must Track

Deadline management in scrutiny proceedings is non-negotiable. One missed date can convert a manageable case into an ex-parte assessment under Section 144.

| Milestone | Statutory / Practical Deadline | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Initial response to Section 143(2) notice | 15–30 days from date of service (confirm exact date on notice) | Varies — always read the notice, never assume |
| Assessment completion deadline | 12 months from end of relevant AY (standard) | Extended to 18 months in specific cases under Finance Act 2021 — e.g., search cases |
| Adjournment request filing | At least 3 working days before compliance deadline | Not on the due date — AO will reject or ignore |
| Jurisdiction / procedural objection | Before first substantive compliance date | Raise DIN defects, limitation, or jurisdiction issues at the very first opportunity |
| Section 154 rectification (post-order) | 4 years from the end of AY in which order was passed | Use for TDS credit errors, arithmetic mistakes before filing CIT(A) appeal |
| CIT(A) appeal under Section 246A | 30 days from date of service of assessment order | File Form 35 on e-Filing portal; condonation of delay possible but avoid relying on it |
| ITAT appeal | 60 days from date of CIT(A) order | Brief client on prospects before proceeding — costs escalate significantly |
| Stay of demand during appeal | File application immediately after filing appeal | Demand stays automatically at 20% of disputed tax under CBDT guidelines if appeal is pending |

**Practical example:** Client Rajesh Nair (PAN: ABCPN1234K, AY 2023-24) receives a CASS-selected limited scrutiny notice on 15 January 2025 with a compliance date of 14 February 2025. Your internal document collection deadline is 4 February 2025. First draft review: 9 February 2025. Final portal submission: 12 February 2025. This two-day buffer absorbs last-minute portal glitches — which happen more often than CBDT would like to admit.

## The Complete CA Response Checklist: Documents to Gather

Send this list to your client within 48 hours of receiving the notice. Do not wait for the first hearing or communication from NaFAC.

**Core Return Documents**
- Return of Income (ITR filed) and ITR-V acknowledgment for the relevant AY
- Computation of total income with all working notes
- Copy of previous year's return if there are carried-forward losses being claimed

**Bank and Financial Records**
- All bank account statements (savings, current, FD, overdraft) for the full financial year — every account, not just the primary one
- Cash deposit summaries with dates, amounts, and branch details for any deposit above ₹50,000

**Books of Accounts**
- Profit & Loss Account, Balance Sheet, and Trial Balance
- Ledger extracts for specific heads flagged in the notice (purchases, sundry creditors, capital account)
- Depreciation schedule if capital expenditure is under examination

**TDS and Tax Credit Reconciliation**
- Form 16 (salary) and Form 16A (other TDS) for all deductors
- Form 26AS, AIS, and TIS downloaded from the portal for the relevant AY
- **Reconcile all three before submission** — unexplained differences between AIS and the return are the most common trigger for Section 69 additions

**Investment and Capital Gains**
- Capital gains statements from broker (equity, mutual funds) — ISIN-level detail if needed
- Property purchase/sale deeds, stamp duty valuations, and cost of improvement records
- Share transaction reports showing holding period for LTCG vs. STCG classification

**Foreign Income and Assets (where applicable)**
- Form 67 (foreign tax credit claim) and supporting documents
- DTAA claim documentation with country-specific treaty article references
- Overseas bank statements and foreign income computation

**Cash and Loan Documentation**
- Source-of-funds trail for cash deposits or cash payments above ₹2 lakh (gift deeds, agricultural income declarations, prior savings evidence)
- Loan agreements, repayment schedules, and confirmation letters from lenders
- If loans are from related parties: copies of lender's ITR and bank statements confirming the source of funds lent

## Drafting the Response: Structure & Best Practices

A response to a Section 143(2) notice is a legal document. It will be read by an Assessment Unit officer who may never interact with you directly. Clarity and precision are more valuable than length.

**Structure every response the same way:**

1. **Reference line:** Notice DIN, PAN, AY, date of notice
2. **Query-by-query response:** Use the AO's own numbering — Query 1, Query 2, and so on
3. **For each query:** One-paragraph factual summary → supporting document reference → legal basis (section, circular, or case law)
4. **Document index:** Numbered list of every document enclosed, cross-referenced to the query it supports

In limited scrutiny cases, **do not volunteer information beyond the scope of the query**. This is not about concealment — it is about scope management. If the notice asks about cash deposits, your response should address cash deposits and nothing else. Submitting unsolicited capital gains details does not help your client; it hands the AO a new line of inquiry.

Where income is exempt or a deduction is legally valid, cite the authority explicitly. For example: *"Interest income of ₹45,000 earned on PPF account is exempt under Section 10(11) of the Income Tax Act, 1961 — copy of PPF passbook enclosed at Exhibit C."* This is how you build a record that survives an appeal.

**Every submission must go through the Faceless Assessment portal with a timestamped acknowledgment receipt.** Save these receipts in your client file. If the AO later claims non-compliance, the acknowledgment is your first line of defence.

## Common Mistakes CAs Make in 143(2) Proceedings

After fifteen years of handling scrutiny matters, the mistakes I see repeatedly are not technical — they are procedural. And procedural mistakes are the ones that hurt most because they are entirely avoidable.

**Missing the deadline without a prior adjournment request.** If you miss the compliance date without having filed an adjournment request, the AO can proceed ex-parte under Section 144 and make a best-judgment assessment. This is almost always worse than any response you could have filed. File an adjournment request — even if it is not granted, the filing protects you procedurally.

**Submitting contradictory documents across queries.** If Query 1 concerns capital introduced in the business and Query 3 concerns a loan from a family member — and the documents you submit for each tell inconsistent stories about the same transaction — the AO will flag the contradiction. Review all documents together before submitting, not query by query in isolation.

**Treating AIS data as gospel without reconciliation.** AIS often captures gross transaction values — for example, proceeds from mutual fund redemptions without offsetting the cost of acquisition. If you submit a response that matches AIS numbers without adjustment, you may be acknowledging income that is not actually taxable. Always reconcile AIS against the return before framing your response.

**Relying on verbal assurances from the Assessment Unit.** In a faceless regime, verbal communication barely exists — but even in transitional cases or older proceedings, do not act on anything that is not in writing. If you are told informally that a query has been dropped, seek a written communication. It will not come, but asking creates a record.

**Failing to maintain a contemporaneous file note.** ICAI's SA 230 principles — even though designed for audit — apply by analogy to tax representation. For every material development: note the date, what was discussed or submitted, and what the next step is. This file note is your primary defence if a client later disputes your advice or if ICAI receives a complaint.

**Not advising clients on perjury risk under Section 277.** When you help a client draft an explanation — particularly for cash deposits or unexplained credits — make clear that a false explanation filed with the tax authorities carries criminal exposure under Section 277. Document this advice in writing.

## Managing Multiple Clients During Peak Assessment Season

If you are running a CA firm of any meaningful size, scrutiny season means managing twenty, thirty, or fifty Section 143(2) matters simultaneously. The only way to do this without errors is with systems — not with heroics.

**Build a centralised tracker** with these minimum fields: client name, PAN, AY, notice date, notice type (limited/complete), compliance deadline, internal document collection deadline, assigned team member, current status, and next action. A shared spreadsheet works if you do not have practice management software, but dedicated tools like IRIS, ClearTax for CA, or even a configured Notion workspace are significantly better.

**Triage by risk level, not by client relationship.** Cases involving international transactions, high-value unexplained credits, repeated scrutiny for the same client, or potential Section 68/69 additions need your personal attention and the earliest slots in your review calendar. A straightforward TDS reconciliation matter can be handled by a well-briefed senior.

**Standardise your document request templates.** A pre-formatted PDF or Word document listing every category of documents required — customisable by adding the specific queries from the notice — reduces the back-and-forth with clients by 40% in our experience. Clients know exactly what is needed; they are not guessing from a phone conversation.

**Set internal deadlines 10 days ahead of statutory deadlines.** This buffer absorbs client delays, bank statement turnaround times, and your own review cycle. If the portal compliance date is 20 March, your internal submission date is 10 March, and your document collection deadline is 1 March. Build this automatically into your tracker when the notice is entered.

**Delegate, but verify.** Document retrieval and initial organisation can be done by juniors. The reconciliation of AIS vs. return vs. books needs a senior. The final response draft needs a partner-level review. Sign off only after you have read the draft independently — not after being told it is ready.

## ICAI Guidelines & Malpractice Exposure for CAs

Tax representation in scrutiny proceedings is a professional engagement with real professional risk. ICAI's framework is clear on what is expected — and what happens when those expectations are not met.

**Clause 11 of the ICAI Code of Ethics** prohibits a CA from knowingly making false or misleading statements before tax authorities. This applies not just to direct falsehoods but to representations that are technically accurate but contextually misleading. If you know a client's explanation is weak or implausible, you have an obligation to advise them — not to package a bad explanation in polished language.

**Obtain a formal engagement letter for every scrutiny matter** — separately from your annual retainer, if applicable. The engagement letter should specify the scope (responding to the specific notice), the AY covered, the fees, your reliance on client-provided documents, and a clear statement that you are not responsible for the accuracy of information provided by the client. This letter is your primary contractual protection.

**If a client refuses to provide complete or accurate documentation**, you face a professional choice. Under Clause 21 of Part I, Second Schedule of the Chartered Accountants Act, 1949, a CA can withdraw from an engagement where continuing would compromise professional standards. Document the refusal, issue a written notice to the client, and retain copies. Withdrawal is uncomfortable but far less uncomfortable than being party to a false submission.

**Maintain an authority letter or power of attorney on file** before filing any response or attending any hearing on behalf of a client. For faceless assessments, the system generates submission records against the CA's login — the authority documentation must match the representation being made. ICAI has received complaints from AOs themselves where CAs appeared to act without client authorisation.

**ICAI's Disciplinary Mechanism** is triggered by complaints from clients, tax authorities, third parties, or suo motu action. The primary defence in every case is documentation: engagement letters, file notes, client communications, and submission acknowledgments. A well-maintained file has resolved more disciplinary inquiries than any eloquent written defence.

## Adjournment Requests: When and How to File

An adjournment request is not an admission of unpreparedness — it is a professional tool. Use it when you need it, but use it correctly.

**File at least 3 working days before the compliance deadline.** A request filed on the due date is almost always rejected or ignored. Three working days gives the Assessment Unit time to process the request and update the compliance schedule.

**State a specific, verifiable reason.** Vague requests — "documents are being collected" — are routinely rejected. Strong grounds include: bank statement requests pending (attach the bank's written acknowledgment), client hospitalisation (attach medical certificate), broker statement delay for international transactions (attach broker correspondence), or a CA firm partner's conflicting ITAT hearing date. The more specific and documented the reason, the higher the probability of approval.

**Submit via the Faceless portal and follow up.** File through the e-Proceedings section and download the acknowledgment immediately. If you receive no response within 48 hours — neither approval nor rejection — send a follow-up submission referencing the original request and its DIN/submission reference. Create a paper trail.

**Understand the appellate value of a denied adjournment.** If the AO denies a reasonable adjournment request and then passes an adverse order, the denial is a ground of appeal — provided you have the written record of your request and the written denial. An AO who refuses a reasonable adjournment without recorded reasons is adding grounds for the assessee to challenge the final order.

**Use adjournments strategically, not habitually.** Two adjournment requests in a single proceeding is a pattern the Assessment Unit notices. It signals case management issues and can subtly prejudice the tone of the proceedings. Request once when genuinely needed; thereafter, deliver on your committed dates.

## Post-Assessment Actions: From Order to Appeal

The assessment order under Section 143(3) is not the end of the matter — it is the beginning of the next phase. How you respond in the 30 days following the order determines the client's exposure.

**Read the order against the notice.** Map every addition made in the order against the specific queries raised in the original notice. If the AO has made additions on issues that were not part of the limited scrutiny scope — a common overreach — this is your strongest ground of appeal. Document the discrepancy clearly.

**Address the demand notice under Section 156 within 30 days.** Even if you are planning to appeal, do not ignore the demand. Pay at least 20% of the disputed tax demand (the automatic stay threshold under CBDT guidelines) to prevent coercive recovery action while the appeal is pending. Advise your client clearly on the consequences of non-payment: interest under Section 220(2) at 1% per month begins accruing after 30 days.

**File Section 154 rectification before CIT(A) if there are obvious errors.** If TDS credit has not been given despite correct Form 26AS entries, or if there is an arithmetic error in the order, a Section 154 rectification application is faster and cheaper than an appeal. File it first. If rejected, include the rejection order as part of the CIT(A) appeal grounds.

**File Form 35 on the e-Filing portal for CIT(A) appeal within 30 days.** The 30-day clock runs from the date of service of the order — not from the date on the order. Attach the assessment order, grounds of appeal, statement of facts, and all relevant documents. Pay the appeal fees (nominal but mandatory). Request a stay of demand simultaneously.

**Brief your client in writing before filing the appeal.** Provide a written note covering: the additions made, the legal merits of each ground, a realistic assessment of success probability, the estimated cost of the appeal process (professional fees, appeal fees, interest), and the timeline. Document that you gave this advice. If the appeal does not go as expected, this written briefing is what demonstrates you acted professionally and in the client's informed interest.

## Your Next Step as the CA on Record

If you currently have Section 143(2) notices pending in your practice, do three things today:

1. **Open your client tracker** and verify that every pending notice has a compliance deadline entered, an internal collection deadline set 10 days prior, and an assigned team member. If any of these fields are blank, fill them before you close this article.

2. **Check whether each pending notice has a DIN** and whether that DIN has been validated on the e-Filing portal. Invalid notices should be challenged in writing this week — not at the hearing stage.

3. **Send the document checklist** from this article to every client with a pending notice. Do not wait for them to ask what they need to provide.

Scrutiny proceedings are won or lost in the preparation phase. The CA who responds three days before the deadline with a complete, well-indexed, legally reasoned response wins almost every time. The CA who scrambles on the due date with a partial file loses — even when the underlying facts support the assessee.

Build the system once. Run it on every case.
