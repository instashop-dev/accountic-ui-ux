**Citation:** ITO v. Lakhmani Mewal Das, (1976) 103 ITR 437 (SC). Three-judge bench. Justice H.R. Khanna, writing for the court.

A half-century-old judgment is not the obvious place to start drafting a notice reply in 2026. But every practicing CA who touches s. 148 ends up here eventually, because the core test Lakhmani lays down has survived every legislative overhaul the section has been through — including the 2021 introduction of s. 148A.

## The question before the court

The ITO reopened an assessment on the basis of information received from the CIT that certain loans shown in the books appeared to be "hawala" transactions. The assessee argued the reopening was bad because the AO had not applied his own mind — he was simply acting on borrowed satisfaction.

The Supreme Court agreed.

## The test, in one sentence

> The reasons for the belief that income has escaped assessment must have **a rational connexion or a relevant bearing** on the formation of the belief, and must not be extraneous or irrelevant.

That is the entire Lakhmani test. The AO's reasons must connect rationally to the conclusion that income has escaped. Not suspicion. Not borrowed satisfaction. Not extraneous information. A **rational nexus** between what the AO knew and what the AO concluded.

## Why it still matters in 2026

Three reasons Lakhmani is still cited in orders today, five decades later:

1. **s. 148A preserved the underlying standard.** The Finance Act 2021 changed the procedure — pre-show-cause, hearing, sanction — but kept the substantive standard: reason to believe, supported by information. Lakhmani's rational-nexus test is the interpretive backbone of "reason to believe."
2. **Every ITAT bench knows it.** It is the most-cited Supreme Court authority on s. 148 jurisdictional challenges. A reply that invokes Lakhmani with a specific, well-framed objection on the AO's reasons lands immediately.
3. **It forces specificity.** "No rational nexus" is an empty phrase. Lakhmani-anchored drafting *requires* you to specify which piece of information the AO relied on, and explain — with reference to the facts of the case — why that information did not rationally support the conclusion drawn.

## How to cite it without it looking boilerplate

The quick test: a reply that just name-drops *Lakhmani Mewal Das* and moves on is a reply that was written on autopilot. A reply that uses Lakhmani well does three things:

- Quotes the rational-nexus sentence *once*, in the paragraph where it does the most work.
- Maps it to the specific information the AO cited in the recorded reasons.
- Explains, in two or three sentences, why *on these specific facts*, the connection the AO drew is not rational.

Anything beyond that is padding.

## The related cases worth knowing

- **Phool Chand Bajrang Lal v. ITO, (1993) 203 ITR 456 (SC)** — clarified that new, specific, reliable information can support reopening even without fresh material coming to light after the original assessment.
- **CIT v. Kelvinator of India Ltd., (2010) 320 ITR 561 (SC)** — the "change of opinion" bar. Not a Lakhmani successor; a companion case addressing a different failure mode.
- **Commissioner of Customs v. Sayed Ali, (2011) 3 SCC 537** — even in Customs, the "rational nexus" language is the doctrinal descendant.
