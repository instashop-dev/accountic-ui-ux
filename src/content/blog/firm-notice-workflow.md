Most solo CAs handle 10–15 notices a month. A small firm with five to fifteen professionals can see 30–50. The operational question at that volume isn't "how do I draft well" — you already know that. It's *how do I route, review, and file without dropping a deadline or embarrassing the firm.*

Here's the playbook we've built at DPS &amp; Co. over the last decade of volume growth.

## Stage 1 — Intake (Monday AM)

Every Monday, one junior goes through the inboxes: individual CA email, firm e-filing portal logins, client forwards. Every new notice gets logged into a single spreadsheet with:

- Client name
- Notice section (139(9), 142(1), 143(2), 148, 245, or "other")
- AY
- Deadline
- Demand or ground category
- Assigned CA

**Rule:** a notice that isn't logged by end-of-day Monday doesn't exist on the firm's radar. Missed deadlines almost always trace back to an intake failure.

## Stage 2 — Triage (Monday PM)

The senior partner spends an hour on Monday afternoon reviewing the log. Each notice gets categorised:

- **Routine** — s. 139(9) defects, intimation corrections, straightforward 143(1) mismatches. Goes to an article or junior. Turnaround: same week.
- **Standard** — 143(2) CASS selections, routine 142(1). Goes to a mid-level CA. Turnaround: 10 days before deadline.
- **High-stakes** — 148, 148A, second-round 143(3), s. 263 revisionary. Goes to a senior CA with partner oversight. Turnaround: half the available time before deadline.

## Stage 3 — Drafting

Routine and standard drafts are written by the assigned CA, using the firm's template library. Templates are updated annually; the current library is 23 templates indexed by section and scenario.

High-stakes drafts follow a different process: a one-page briefing note from the CA to the senior, then a drafting session, then the draft. The briefing note alone catches about a quarter of issues that would have landed in the draft otherwise.

Since late 2025, the first draft on routine and standard matters is produced in Accountic. The CA reviews, adjusts to firm voice, and files. On high-stakes matters, Accountic is used for research and citation verification but the draft is still written by the senior.

## Stage 4 — Partner Review (Before Every Filing)

Non-negotiable. Every notice reply, without exception, is reviewed by a partner before filing. Review checklist:

1. Does every ground in the notice have a direct response?
2. Are all citations verified against the current repository?
3. Is the tone appropriate to the notice section?
4. Is the enclosure list complete and indexed?
5. Is the prayer specific?

Review is fast — average 8 minutes per routine notice, 20 minutes per scrutiny, 45 minutes per 148. It compounds. A firm that doesn't partner-review every reply is running on luck.

## Stage 5 — Filing + Closure

Filing is routed through a single login, tracked in the same spreadsheet from stage 1. The row gets closed with the DIN of the filed reply and the exchange of emails, if any, with the AO. Deadline cleared. Next.

## The one KPI that matters

We track a dozen things. Only one matters: **notices filed before the midpoint of the available window.**

A reply filed on the last day signals a firm that's running hot. A reply filed before midpoint signals a firm in control. Clients can tell, even when they can't articulate it.

Our current number: 78% of routine notices filed before midpoint, 62% of scrutiny, 54% of 148s. It's the number I look at first, every Monday morning.

## What's changed with AI tooling

The honest answer: fewer reviewer-hours per notice. Our routine-notice cycle time is down from ~3 hours end-to-end to ~90 minutes. Scrutiny is down from ~14 hours to ~6. The partner-review time hasn't changed — that's the bit that's irreducible, and it's the bit clients are paying for.

Volume, not speed, is where the operational leverage shows up. A firm that used to handle 30 notices a month can handle 45 without adding headcount, if it's willing to put the template discipline in place first.
