# Section 148A Notice Defects That Void Reassessment: HC Cases

Reassessment litigation has fundamentally changed since Finance Act 2021. If your client receives a Section 148 notice today, the first question is almost never about the merits of the escaped income allegation. It is almost always about procedure. High Courts across India have quashed hundreds of reassessment proceedings purely on procedural grounds — and that trend is accelerating into AY 2024-25 and AY 2025-26 matters as the post-Ashish Agarwal fallout continues to work through the system.

This article maps the procedural defects that courts have treated as fatal, with specific rulings, so you can build a complete challenge from day one.

---

## Why Section 148A Procedural Compliance Is Non-Negotiable Post-2022

Finance Act 2021 inserted Section 148A as a mandatory pre-reassessment inquiry step, effective 1 April 2021. Before an Assessing Officer can issue a Section 148 notice, the law now requires a structured sequence: conduct an inquiry, issue a show-cause notice (SCN), allow the assessee to respond, and then pass a reasoned written order. This sequence is not administrative courtesy — it is a statutory precondition to jurisdiction.

The Supreme Court's landmark ruling in *Union of India v. Ashish Agarwal* (2022) dramatically reset the litigation landscape. The Court validated the new regime and directed that all notices issued under the old Section 148 framework before 1 April 2021 be treated as SCNs under the new Section 148A(b). This created a mass re-do exercise, and the CBDT issued directions for Assessing Officers to re-follow the Section 148A procedure for those pending cases.

What emerged from that exercise was predictable: thousands of notices issued hurriedly, with missed deadlines, absent DINs, and pro-forma orders that ignored assessee replies. Courts have since held that procedural non-compliance provides an independent and often decisive ground to quash a notice — entirely without touching the question of whether income actually escaped assessment. For a practising CA, this means your first line of defence is procedural, and it should be explored before you even brief a counsel on the substantive merits.

---

## The Four Core Procedural Requirements Under Section 148A

Every Assessing Officer must satisfy four distinct statutory conditions in the correct sequence before a Section 148 notice can validly issue. Understanding these as a workflow — not just a list — is essential because each step is a potential point of failure.

1. Conduct a prior inquiry under Section 148A(a) and obtain prior approval from the specified authority before issuing any show-cause notice. The inquiry must be genuine and documented; a notional or post-facto inquiry does not satisfy this requirement.

2. Issue the show-cause notice under Section 148A(b) with sufficient particularity about the alleged escaped income. The SCN must identify the specific information or material the AO is relying upon — a vague allegation that "income has escaped assessment" without identifying the source or quantum is not compliant.

3. Allow the assessee a minimum of seven days to respond, extendable up to thirty days on request. This timeline is mandatory, not directory — courts have consistently held that giving fewer than seven days is a jurisdictional defect that voids the entire proceedings.

4. Pass a reasoned written order under Section 148A(d), which specifically addresses the assessee's reply point by point and records why the AO is satisfied that the case warrants reassessment. A rubber-stamp order that does not engage with the objections raised is independently fatal to the notice.

Only after all four steps are completed in this sequence can the AO lawfully issue the Section 148 notice. Missing, reversing, or compressing any step gives rise to a challenge.

## High Court Rulings: Missed Timelines and Time-Barred Notices

Timeline defects are among the most reliably successful grounds for quashing reassessment proceedings because they go to the court's jurisdiction rather than to the AO's discretion.

**Case Scenario: Bagaria Properties — Section 148 Issued Before Reply Period Expired**

In *Bagaria Properties v. ITO* (Calcutta High Court, 2023), the Assessing Officer issued the Section 148 notice before the seven-day reply window under Section 148A(b) had even expired. The assessee had not yet submitted a reply when the notice landed in their portal. The Calcutta HC held the Section 148 notice void ab initio — there was no valid Section 148A(d) order in existence, because the mandatory opportunity to reply had been foreclosed. The court refused to treat this as a curable irregularity and declined to remand.

The Delhi High Court's decision in *Suman Jeet Agarwal v. ITO* (2022) addressed the post-Ashish Agarwal notices issued pursuant to CBDT directions. The CBDT had set a deadline for AOs to complete the Section 148A procedure and issue fresh Section 148 notices. AOs who issued their notices after that extended deadline found their proceedings quashed as time-barred. The Delhi HC held that the CBDT-prescribed calendar formed part of the jurisdictional framework and missing it was fatal.

The Allahabad High Court has consistently taken the position that limitation periods under Section 149 are jurisdictional in nature. Courts cannot condone or extend them; the defect is incurable. In multiple 2023 rulings, the Allahabad HC quashed notices where the AO attempted to argue that the limitation period had been extended by CBDT circulars in circumstances where no valid extension power existed.

**Practical takeaway for your practice:** The very first thing you should do when a client brings you a Section 148 notice — for any AY — is map the dates. Record the date of the Section 148A(b) SCN, the date the reply window closed, the date of the Section 148A(d) order, and the date of the Section 148 notice. Any sequence violation or deadline breach is potentially fatal.

## Quashing on DIN Absence: Document Identification Number Defects

CBDT Circular No. 19/2019 requires that all communications issued by income tax authorities must carry a computer-generated Document Identification Number (DIN). The circular explicitly states that any communication issued without a DIN — unless it falls within narrowly prescribed manual exceptions — shall be treated as invalid and as if it was never issued.

The Bombay and Gujarat High Courts have been particularly rigorous on this point. Both courts have held that a Section 148 notice, a Section 148A(b) SCN, or a Section 148A(d) order issued without a valid DIN has no legal existence. The document is non-est — it does not merely carry a defect; it simply does not exist in the eyes of the law. The Bombay HC, in particular, has declined to read down this requirement as procedural, treating it instead as a condition that goes to the very validity of the communication.

Courts have carved out a narrow exception: if the AO falls within one of the manual exception categories prescribed by CBDT and has recorded that fact in writing before issuing the document, a DIN-less communication may survive. The exception is not self-operating — the AO must have documented the exception category contemporaneously, not retrospectively. In practice, courts have been very reluctant to accept post-facto explanations for DIN absence.

**DIN verification checklist for every Section 148A matter:**

| Document | Where to Check DIN | Risk if Absent |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Section 148A(b) show-cause notice | Top right corner of the notice PDF on the portal | Entire proceedings void ab initio |
| Section 148A(a) inquiry approval order | Separate order document from specified authority | Jurisdictional defect — no valid inquiry conducted |
| Section 148A(d) written order | Order document uploaded to assessee's e-filing portal | No valid Section 148A(d) order exists; Section 148 notice has no foundation |
| Section 148 reassessment notice | Notice PDF on the assessee's portal | Notice non-est; no valid reassessment jurisdiction |

Verify all four documents separately. A DIN on the Section 148 notice does not cure a DIN defect on the Section 148A(d) order — these are independent documents, and each must independently comply.

## Inadequate Opportunity: Rulings on Truncated Reply Windows and Non-Application of Mind

Even where an AO issues documents with proper DINs and within limitation, inadequate opportunity at the Section 148A(b) stage provides a third, entirely independent ground to challenge proceedings.

**Case Scenario: Maruti Ispat — Three-Day Reply Window Is Fatal**

In *Maruti Ispat v. ITO* (Rajasthan High Court, 2023), the AO issued an SCN giving the assessee only three days to reply, citing urgency because the limitation period was approaching. The Rajasthan HC was unimpressed. The court held that the minimum seven-day window under Section 148A(b) is a mandatory statutory requirement, not a directory guideline. The AO's administrative inconvenience — even impending limitation — cannot override the assessee's statutory right to a minimum opportunity. The SCN was quashed, and because it was quashed, the downstream Section 148A(d) order and Section 148 notice fell with it.

The Delhi High Court has developed a clear rule on the quality of the Section 148A(d) order itself: the order must specifically address each objection raised by the assessee in the reply. Where an assessee raises three distinct objections — say, a limitation argument, a claim that the information is stale, and a merits argument that the income was disclosed — the Section 148A(d) order must engage with all three. An order that simply says "having considered the reply, I am satisfied that income has escaped assessment" without addressing individual objections has been held to be non-speaking and void.

**Case Scenario: Same-Day Order — Non-Application of Mind**

The Gujarat High Court has addressed a pattern of Assessing Officers passing the Section 148A(d) order on the very same day the assessee files the reply. The court's logic is clean: if the reply is filed at 3 PM and the order is uploaded at 4 PM on the same working day, the AO has not meaningfully read the reply, let alone considered it. The Gujarat HC has held this pattern to be indicative of non-application of mind, vitiating the entire proceeding. In an era where AOs are under pressure to beat limitation deadlines, same-day orders are common — and they are being quashed with some regularity.

The Madras High Court has added another dimension: the AO cannot, in the Section 148A(d) order, rely on information or material that was not disclosed to the assessee in the SCN. If the SCN mentioned an AIR transaction and the final order additionally relied on a cash deposit pattern from banking data, the assessee was never given an opportunity to address that additional material. The Madras HC has quashed such orders as a violation of the principles of natural justice — an argument that is distinct from, and additional to, the statutory timeline arguments.

## Defect-by-Defect Comparison: Which Lapses Courts Treat as Fatal vs. Curable

Not every procedural defect carries the same consequence. Courts have developed a rough taxonomy of defects, and understanding which category your client's case falls into determines whether you go straight to a writ or attempt other remedies first.

| Procedural Defect | Judicial Treatment | Leading Court(s) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Section 148 notice issued before Section 148A(d) order | Fatal — void ab initio; no downstream proceedings can survive | Calcutta HC, Delhi HC |
| Notice time-barred under Section 149 | Fatal — jurisdictional; courts cannot condone; entire reassessment falls | Allahabad HC, Delhi HC |
| Missing DIN on SCN, 148A(d) order, or Section 148 notice | Fatal — document treated as non-est; no valid legal communication | Bombay HC, Gujarat HC |
| No prior approval from specified authority under 148A(a) | Fatal — jurisdictional precondition not met; AO had no authority to issue SCN | Delhi HC, Gujarat HC |
| Section 148 notice issued before reply period under 148A(b) expired | Fatal — mandatory opportunity foreclosed; void ab initio | Calcutta HC |
| Reply window shorter than seven days | Fatal — mandatory minimum not met; SCN and downstream proceedings void | Rajasthan HC |
| Section 148A(d) order passed same day as reply filed | Generally fatal — non-application of mind inferred; proceedings vitiated | Gujarat HC |
| Section 148A(d) order fails to address individual objections | Fatal — non-speaking order; quashed with liberty to pass fresh order | Delhi HC, Madras HC |
| Minor technical error in approval chain later rectified | Curable — courts have remanded for fresh consideration in limited cases | Some Allahabad HC benches |
| Reasons in SCN inadequate but information otherwise available to assessee | Sometimes curable — courts have remanded rather than quashed outright | Mixed — Allahabad HC |

The jurisdictional split between High Courts is real and strategically important. The Bombay HC tends toward strict void treatment — if the DIN is missing, the document does not exist, full stop. Certain benches of the Allahabad HC have shown more flexibility, particularly on the adequacy-of-reasons question, occasionally remanding for a fresh inquiry rather than quashing outright. If you are before the Allahabad HC, you should plead every defect you have identified, not just the strongest one — you want at least one ground that the court treats as jurisdictionally fatal rather than merely remandable.

## CA Action Checklist: Building a Procedural Challenge to Section 148A Notices

When a client walks in with a Section 148A notice — whether for AY 2024-25 or an earlier year — here is the workflow to follow from day one through to filing a writ petition.

1. Record the exact date the SCN under Section 148A(b) was issued and the date your client actually received it on the e-filing portal. Map both dates against the Section 149 limitation calendar for the relevant AY and confirm the AO was within time when the SCN itself was issued.

2. Download the SCN, the Section 148A(a) approval, the Section 148A(d) order, and the Section 148 notice as separate PDFs from the portal. Check each document individually for a valid DIN in the header. Screenshot the portal at the time of download and preserve the timestamp.

3. Verify the reply window: count the days between the SCN date and the deadline the AO has given. If fewer than seven days are available, document this immediately and include an explicit objection on this ground in the reply itself — do not wait for the writ.

4. File the reply under Section 148A(b) with a dedicated opening section that expressly reserves all jurisdictional and procedural objections. State clearly that engaging on the merits does not constitute a waiver of any procedural defect. Include your limitation argument, your DIN argument, and your opportunity argument as numbered paragraphs before the substantive response.

5. After the Section 148A(d) order is issued, place the order text side by side with your reply and prepare a point-by-point comparison document. Mark every objection you raised and identify whether the order addressed it, dismissed it with reasons, or ignored it entirely. This document becomes Exhibit A in your writ petition.

6. If you decide to file a writ in the High Court, ensure your petition pleads all fatal defects independently — time-bar, DIN absence, inadequate opportunity, and non-speaking order as alternative and cumulative grounds. Courts have shown willingness to quash on any one of them; giving the court four to choose from significantly improves your odds.

7. Preserve all portal timestamps, login records, email notifications from the income tax department, and ITBA portal screenshots as primary evidence. High Courts increasingly scrutinise the exact timing of notice uploads versus reply deadlines, and your digital evidence trail must be unambiguous.

---

**Your concrete next step:** Pull every active Section 148A or Section 148 notice file in your office right now — particularly any matter relating to AY 2024-25 or AY 2025-26 where proceedings were initiated in the last twelve months. For each file, run the four-document DIN check and the date-sequencing audit described above before you do anything else. If you find a defect, reserve it in writing immediately — even if the reply deadline has passed, a Section 148A(d) order can still be challenged in a writ on grounds that appear on the face of the record. Do not let a winnable procedural ground go unpleaded because it was discovered late.
