A Section 148 notice landing in your client's inbox is not a routine compliance matter. It is the beginning of a high-stakes legal process where every decision in the first few days can determine the outcome. This guide walks you through each stage — from the moment the notice arrives to the reassessment proceedings — with the precision a practising CA needs in the field.

---

## What Is a Section 148 Notice and Why Did You Receive It?

Section 148 empowers an Assessing Officer to reopen a completed assessment when they have reason to believe that income chargeable to tax has escaped assessment under Section 147. The notice is not a random event — it is triggered by specific information the AO has received.

**Common triggers include:**
- AIR/SFT mismatches (e.g., high-value property purchase not matching declared income)
- Third-party information from banks, registrars, or other departments
- Survey data collected during a field survey under Section 133A
- Unexplained cash deposits, especially post-demonetisation periods
- Foreign asset disclosures or FEMA-linked information

The Finance Act 2021 significantly restructured this process. Section 148 no longer operates in isolation — it is now preceded by a mandatory inquiry procedure under Section 148A. Understanding this distinction is the first line of defence.

**Time limits under the current law are strictly enforced:**

| Escaped Income Amount | Maximum Reopening Period | Approval Required |
|---|---|---|
| Below ₹50 lakh | 3 years from end of relevant AY | PCIT/CIT |
| ₹50 lakh and above | Up to 10 years from end of relevant AY | PCIT/CIT with CBDT approval |
| Search/survey cases | Special provisions apply | As per Section 153A/153C |

The assessee has a legal right to inspect the reasons recorded by the AO before issuing the notice. Exercising this right early is non-negotiable.

## Section 148 vs Section 148A: Understanding the New Procedure (Post-2021)

Finance Act 2021 inserted Section 148A as a mandatory gateway before any Section 148 notice can be issued. This is the most significant procedural change in reassessment law in decades, and your reply strategy must account for it.

**The sequence now works as follows:**

1. **Section 148A(a):** AO conducts an inquiry, with prior approval of specified authority
2. **Section 148A(b):** AO issues a show-cause notice to the assessee with the material in possession
3. **Assessee responds:** Minimum 7 days given, extendable up to 30 days on request
4. **Section 148A(d):** AO passes a speaking order deciding whether to proceed
5. **Section 148 notice:** Issued only if AO concludes a case exists, with prior approval

If the Section 148A procedure was bypassed, the subsequent Section 148 notice is legally vulnerable. This is not a minor technicality — courts have consistently held it as going to the root of jurisdiction.

The Supreme Court's ruling in **Union of India v. Ashish Agarwal [(2022) 138 taxmann.com 64 (SC)]** settled the position for notices issued between April 1, 2021 and June 30, 2021 under the old Section 148. Those notices were treated as Section 148A(b) show-cause notices, giving assessees a fresh opportunity to respond. If your client's notice falls in this window, the procedural history needs careful review.

## Immediate Actions to Take Within the First 48 Hours of Receiving the Notice

Speed and precision matter here. The decisions made in the first 48 hours shape the entire defence strategy.

**Your 48-hour checklist:**

1. **Verify authenticity:** Log in to the income tax e-filing portal and confirm the notice DIN (Document Identification Number). Every valid notice issued post-October 2019 carries a DIN. A notice without a valid DIN is void.
2. **Record the exact date of service:** This is critical. The limitation clock for your response and any writ petition starts here. Take a screenshot of the portal notification with the timestamp.
3. **Pull the original records:** Retrieve the ITR, computation sheet, Form 26AS, AIS, and TIS for the assessment year mentioned in the notice.
4. **Check for Section 148A(b) compliance:** Confirm whether a prior show-cause notice under Section 148A(b) was issued. If not, flag it as a procedural defect before taking any other step.
5. **Do not file a fresh return immediately:** Premature filing without legal review can be construed as acceptance of the AO's jurisdiction and the reasons for reopening.
6. **Preserve all records:** Do not delete, modify, or archive any digital or physical records related to the flagged transactions. Treat them as potential evidence.

> **Practical note:** In many cases, clients first learn of a Section 148 notice through an automated SMS or email. Always insist they check the portal directly and share the actual notice document, not just the alert.

## How to Access and Analyse the Reasons for Reopening

You cannot build a defence against an allegation you have not read in full. The recorded reasons are the foundation of the AO's case — and often, they are also their weakest point.

**Step 1: File a written application to the AO** requesting supply of the reasons recorded under Section 148 before issuing the notice. This is a settled legal right under the GKN Driveshafts line of jurisprudence. The AO is bound to provide these reasons within a reasonable time.

**Step 2: Analyse the reasons critically.** Ask yourself:
- Is there tangible material, or are the reasons based on mere suspicion or information from an anonymous source?
- Is there a live link between the material cited and the alleged escaped income?
- Are the allegations specific, or are they vague and generalised?
- Is this a change of opinion on facts already examined in the original assessment?

**Step 3: Examine the sanction.** The sanctioning authority — PCIT or CIT — must apply an independent mind to the AO's request. If the approval is mechanical or rubber-stamped without application of mind, it is a valid ground to challenge the entire proceeding.

Document every deficiency you find. Each one is a potential ground for objection, a ground to file a writ petition, or a point to raise during the reassessment itself.

## Filing Objections to the Reasons for Reopening: The GKN Driveshafts Process

The Supreme Court in **GKN Driveshafts (India) Ltd. v. ITO [(2003) 259 ITR 19 (SC)]** laid down a specific procedure that every assessee must follow. Skipping it weakens your legal position significantly.

**The mandatory sequence:**

1. File the return of income in response to the Section 148 notice (see next section)
2. File written objections to the reasons for reopening — separate from the return
3. The AO must pass a speaking order disposing of those objections
4. Only after the objection order is received should you participate further in assessment proceedings

**Common objection grounds to raise:**

| Objection Ground | Factual Basis Required |
|---|---|
| No tangible material | Show the reasons contain only suspicion or borrowed information |
| Change of opinion | Demonstrate the same issue was examined in original assessment |
| Time-barred notice | Prove the escaped income is below ₹50 lakh and 3-year limit has passed |
| Improper sanction | Show the approving authority did not apply independent mind |
| Lack of jurisdiction | AO is not the jurisdictional AO for this assessee |
| Borrowed satisfaction | AO has mechanically adopted another authority's conclusion |

Attach supporting documents, relevant judgments, and applicable CBDT circulars to your objection letter. The AO's speaking order on objections, if adverse, becomes the basis for a writ petition before the High Court.

## Drafting the Reply to Section 148 Notice: Structure and Content

A well-structured reply does two things: it answers the AO's specific allegations, and it creates a clear record for any future litigation. Here is the structure that works in practice.

**Para 1 — Acknowledgment and Identification**
State the assessee's name, PAN, the AY in question, and the notice DIN and date. Confirm receipt of the notice. Example: *"This reply is filed on behalf of Mr. Rajesh Kumar Sharma, PAN: ABCRS1234D, in response to the notice under Section 148 dated 15 May 2025, bearing DIN 2025051500123456, pertaining to Assessment Year 2021-22."*

**Para 2 — Jurisdictional and Procedural Objections**
If the notice is time-barred, if Section 148A procedure was not followed, or if the sanction is defective, state it here clearly. Do not bury this in the middle of the reply.

**Para 3 — Factual Rebuttal**
Address each allegation in the reasons point by point. Use numbered sub-paragraphs that mirror the AO's own paragraph numbering. Attach supporting documents as numbered annexures. Be specific — vague responses invite follow-up notices.

**Para 4 — Legal Grounds**
Cite relevant case laws, CBDT circulars, and statutory provisions. Keep citations precise: court name, year, and citation reference.

**Para 5 — Prayer**
Conclude with a formal prayer: *"In light of the above, it is respectfully submitted that the reasons recorded do not justify reopening of the assessment for AY 2021-22 and the proceedings be dropped."*

**Annexure checklist for the reply:**
- Copy of the original ITR and ITR-V acknowledgment
- Computation of income for the relevant AY
- Form 26AS, AIS, TIS
- Bank statements, investment proofs, transaction documents
- CA certificate if valuation or source of funds is disputed
- Copies of cited case laws and CBDT circulars

Use numbered paragraphs throughout. When the AO writes their reassessment order, they are required to address your points — numbered paragraphs make it impossible for them to ignore specific contentions.

## Filing the Return in Response to Section 148: What You Must Know

Under the GKN Driveshafts procedure, filing a return is the starting point. But how and what you file carries significant implications.

**Key decisions to make:**

- **File as-is or revise?** If the original return correctly reflected all income, file the same figures and clearly state you are filing in response to the Section 148 notice, not because income was missed. If there are genuine omissions the client wants to disclose, weigh the risk carefully — additional income disclosed may trigger penalty proceedings under Section 270A.
- **Claim all missed deductions:** Reopening the year opens the entire assessment. Use this opportunity to claim any deductions, exemptions, or set-offs that were missed in the original ITR. Courts have consistently held that the assessee is entitled to this benefit.
- **File under protest if challenging jurisdiction:** Add a clear statement: *"This return is being filed without prejudice to the assessee's objections to the jurisdiction of the AO and the validity of the notice under Section 148."*

**On the e-filing portal:**
- Log in, go to e-File > Income Tax Returns > File Income Tax Return
- Select the relevant AY and ITR form
- Under "Filing Type," select "In response to notice under Section 148"
- The deadline is typically 30 days from the date of service of the notice, but always verify the specific deadline mentioned in the notice itself

## Documents and Evidence You Must Compile Before the Reassessment Proceedings

Once objections are filed and the AO proceeds with reassessment, the evidentiary stage begins. Preparation here determines the outcome.

**Build your document file in this order:**

1. **Original assessment records:** ITR, ITR-V, computation of income, tax audit report (Form 3CA/3CB and 3CD), and any previous assessment orders for the same AY
2. **Portal records:** Form 26AS, AIS, and TIS for the relevant AY — download and annotate to cross-verify against the AO's stated grounds
3. **Transaction-specific documents:** Bank statements, FD receipts, investment proofs, property sale/purchase agreements, share transfer records — whichever is relevant to the flagged income
4. **Business records:** Contracts, invoices, ledger extracts, and board resolutions if the flagged transaction involves business income or related-party dealings
5. **Prior examination evidence:** If the same transaction or issue was examined during the original scrutiny assessment and accepted, pull those assessment orders and any written submissions made at that time — this is your strongest change-of-opinion defence
6. **Expert evidence:** CA certificates, registered valuer reports, or third-party confirmations if the dispute involves valuation, source of funds, or fair market value
7. **Departmental correspondence:** Print and preserve every portal submission, email, and physical communication. Courts have repeatedly emphasised the importance of a complete correspondence record in writ petitions

> **Named scenario for illustration:** Suppose your client, Ms. Priya Mehta (PAN: AAEPM9876F), received a Section 148 notice for AY 2020-21 citing a high-value property purchase recorded in the SFT data. The original ITR for AY 2020-21 was a scrutiny case that was closed with an acceptance order. Your document file should prominently include that acceptance order — it is direct evidence that the AO is changing their opinion on a matter already examined, which is a ground to quash the reopening entirely.

---

## Your Next Step as the CA

The moment a Section 148 notice arrives, the clock starts on multiple legal deadlines running simultaneously. Do not let routine practice management delay the first steps.

**This week, do the following:**

1. Authenticate the notice on the portal and record the service date
2. Confirm whether Section 148A(b) procedure was followed
3. Apply in writing to the AO for supply of recorded reasons
4. Pull the original AY records and build the document file

The difference between a reassessment that is dropped at the objection stage and one that results in a large addition often comes down to how meticulously the procedural and factual record was built in the first two weeks. Start early, document everything, and use every statutory right available to your client.
