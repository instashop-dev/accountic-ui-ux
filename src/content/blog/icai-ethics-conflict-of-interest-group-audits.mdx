# ICAI Code of Ethics: Conflict of Interest in Group Audits

Group audits have quietly become one of the highest-risk areas for ethics compliance in Indian practice. Between NFRA stepping up enforcement actions and the formal requirements under SA 600, the days of treating conflict-of-interest checks as a box-ticking exercise are over. If your firm is acting as group auditor or component auditor on any multi-entity engagement, this is the article you need to read before your next engagement acceptance meeting.

---

## Why Conflict of Interest in Group Audits Demands Urgent Attention

NFRA's enforcement actions over the last three years have repeatedly called out one specific gap: firms were completing SA 600 checklists but had no documented independence analysis for component auditors. The penalty orders make clear that NFRA expects the same rigour applied to the group auditor to cascade down to every material component. That expectation now has teeth.

SA 600 formalises a layered relationship between the group auditor and each component auditor, and every layer introduces its own conflict exposure. The moment your firm engages a component auditor — or agrees to act as one — you have a relationship that needs to be stress-tested against the ICAI Code, not just the audit standards.

The ICAI Code of Ethics (2020 edition, aligned with IESBA) replaced the older "Rules of Professional Conduct" framework with a structured threat-and-safeguard model. This is a materially stricter standard. The old approach asked "is there a conflict?" The new approach asks "what is the nature of the threat, how significant is it, and what safeguard eliminates or reduces it to an acceptable level?" That analytical shift changes how your engagement files must be built.

---

## What Constitutes Conflict of Interest Under the ICAI Code

Section 220 of the ICAI Code identifies three categories of conflict: financial interest conflicts, employment relationship conflicts, and business relationship conflicts. In a group audit context, each of these can arise at the group auditor level, the component auditor level, or both simultaneously — which is what makes group engagements uniquely difficult to manage.

The network firm rule amplifies this significantly. If your firm is part of a network, a conflict with any entity within that network — including a sister firm in another city that shares your brand or quality standards — triggers disclosure obligations for the group auditor. CAs often overlook this because the conflicted relationship sits in a different office, but the Code does not permit that compartmentalisation.

One of the most common hidden triggers is related-party transactions between group entities and audit firm clients. If your firm audits Company A, and Company A's subsidiary (your audit component) has significant transactions with another firm client, that business relationship needs to be evaluated under Section 220 before you sign anything. These situations rarely announce themselves — they surface when you map the group structure in detail.

## Conflict Scenarios Specific to Group Audit Engagements

**Case Scenario: Three Situations That Catch Firms Off Guard**

**Scenario A — Advisory Services to a Material Component**

Your firm's consulting division has been providing transfer pricing advisory to a wholly owned subsidiary that the audit division is now asked to audit as a material component. The self-review threat here is direct and significant. The advisory work involved forming a view on an outcome — now the audit team is expected to independently evaluate that same outcome. The ICAI Code does not allow you to simply erect an "information barrier" and call it a safeguard. You need to either decline the component audit or discontinue the advisory engagement before accepting. Document which option you chose and why.

**Scenario B — Former Partner as Component Auditor**

The component auditor appointed by the parent's management is a former partner of your firm who completed the cooling-off period just two years ago. Familiarity threat does not evaporate the moment the cooling-off period ends if the former partner worked directly on group entities during their tenure. You need to review which entities they were involved with, assess residual familiarity risk, and communicate that assessment to the group audit committee in writing — even if you ultimately conclude the risk is acceptable.

**Scenario C — Equity Holding in the Parent**

A network member firm holds equity in the parent company. Your firm's office in a different city is auditing a subsidiary of that parent as a component auditor. Under the ICAI Code's network firm provisions, the financial interest conflict at the network level reaches your component engagement. This is not a theoretical risk — it is a reportable conflict requiring written disclosure to the audit committee of the subsidiary and the group auditor, with documented evidence of consent or withdrawal.

## Threat Classification and Safeguard Analysis: A Step-by-Step Workflow

Work through this sequence at engagement acceptance stage. Do not compress or reorder the steps — the documentation logic depends on completing them in this order.

1. Prepare a complete list of all group entities — parent, material subsidiaries, significant associates, joint ventures, and any SPVs consolidating into the group financial statements.
2. Run each entity against your firm's client relationship register and check for existing audit, non-audit, and advisory engagements at both the firm level and the network level.
3. Identify personal relationships: check whether any engagement partner, key audit partner, or quality reviewer has family ties, prior employment, or financial interests connected to any group entity.
4. Classify each identified relationship as a self-interest threat, self-review threat, familiarity threat, advocacy threat, or intimidation threat using the ICAI Code's threat categories.
5. Rate the significance of each threat — significant, moderate, or negligible — based on the nature of the relationship, the materiality of the component, and how directly the threat affects audit judgment.
6. Select safeguards proportionate to the threat rating: for significant threats, the only acceptable outcomes are elimination of the conflict or withdrawal from the engagement; for moderate threats, document the safeguard applied (rotation, additional review, disclosure); for negligible threats, record the basis for your conclusion.
7. Document the entire analysis in the engagement acceptance file with dates, names, and the specific Code sections relied upon — an undated memo is treated by NFRA as equivalent to no memo at all.
8. Obtain written sign-off from the managing partner or ethics partner confirming the analysis was reviewed and the conclusion was approved at the firm level.
9. Prepare the conflict disclosure letter to the group audit committee — or, where applicable, to the audit committee of the relevant component — describing the conflict, the safeguard applied, and inviting formal objection within a specified window (typically 10–15 working days).

This nine-step process should be completed before the engagement letter is issued. If a new conflict surfaces mid-engagement, restart from Step 4 for that specific relationship.

## Disclosure and Documentation Requirements: ICAI Code vs. SA 600 vs. NFRA Expectations

These three frameworks are not alternatives — they are cumulative. Satisfying one does not discharge your obligations under the others. The table below maps the key requirements so you can identify where your current practice may be leaving gaps.

| Framework | Disclosure Requirement | Documentation Required | Common Gap Identified |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| ICAI Code Section 220.7–220.15 | Written disclosure to all affected parties; written consent required where conflict is not eliminated | Conflict identification memo, disclosure letter, consent or objection record | Consent-and-consent-refusal trail absent; disclosures made verbally or informally |
| SA 600 (Paragraphs 17–19) | Group auditor must document basis of reliance on each component auditor, including independence confirmation | Component auditor independence confirmation letter, instructions letter, review of component work papers | Independence confirmations obtained as standard templates without firm-specific conflict analysis |
| NFRA Inspection Framework | Expects evidence that both SA 600 and ICAI Code obligations were met, not just SA 600 checklists | Complete engagement file with dated threat assessments, managing partner sign-off, committee communications | SA 600 checklist present but ICAI Code threat-and-safeguard analysis and consent records missing |

The practical implication: your engagement file needs three distinct sections for conflict management — the SA 600 independence confirmation from the component auditor, the ICAI Code threat-and-safeguard memo, and the audit committee disclosure with the consent record. NFRA inspectors look for all three. Firms that satisfy SA 600 documentation but skip the Code's consent trail are the ones receiving adverse inspection comments.

## Practical Templates: Conflict Disclosure Letter and Engagement File Checklist

**Practitioner Note: What Your Disclosure Letter and File Checklist Must Actually Contain**

The conflict disclosure letter to the audit committee is not a standard independence declaration. It must do three specific things: name the conflicting relationship with enough specificity that the audit committee can make an informed judgment, describe the safeguard you have applied and why you consider it sufficient to reduce the threat to an acceptable level, and invite the committee to raise objections within a defined window. A letter that says only "we confirm our independence" does not meet Section 220's requirements and will not survive NFRA scrutiny.

Draft the operative paragraph of your disclosure letter along these lines:

*"We wish to draw to the Audit Committee's attention that [name of firm/network member] currently provides [nature of service] to [name of entity], which is a [material/significant] component of the group financial statements. This creates a [self-review/familiarity/self-interest] threat to our independence in respect of [component name]. The safeguard we have applied is [describe safeguard — e.g., ring-fencing of the advisory engagement, appointment of a separate engagement partner with no involvement in the advisory work]. We are satisfied that this safeguard reduces the threat to an acceptable level. The Audit Committee is invited to communicate any objection to this arrangement within 15 working days of the date of this letter. In the absence of an objection, we will proceed on the basis that the Audit Committee has noted and accepted this disclosure."*

For the engagement file, the eight mandatory documents are:

1. Group entity mapping worksheet with date of preparation
2. Firm relationship register extract for all group entities, dated and signed
3. Threat identification and classification memo
4. Safeguard selection and rationale document with managing partner sign-off
5. Conflict disclosure letter to audit committee (dated, addressed to named members)
6. Audit committee acknowledgment or consent record (or documented follow-up if no response received)
7. Component auditor independence confirmation (SA 600-compliant, not a generic template)
8. Mid-engagement conflict update note (even if the conclusion is "no new conflicts identified")

Version control matters. Each document must carry a preparation date, review date, and the name of the partner who reviewed it. NFRA has treated undated documents as non-compliant in multiple inspection reports. Build this dating discipline into your file review process, not as an afterthought when the file is being assembled for closing.

## Key Takeaways and Action Points for CAs Handling Group Audits

The single most important habit change you can make is to conduct a conflict-of-interest review at three defined points — engagement acceptance, planning stage, and immediately before signing the group audit report. Conflicts that did not exist in April can materialise by November: a new advisory assignment, a partner joining from a group entity, a client acquisition that brings a network firm into the picture. A one-time pre-engagement check is no longer sufficient.

At the firm level, the most effective control is a centralised relationship register updated quarterly. This should capture all audit and non-audit engagements across the firm and its network, with fields that allow you to cross-reference against group audit entities. Firms that rely on individual partners to self-identify conflicts mid-engagement are consistently the ones that end up with NFRA findings.

Two regulatory developments deserve your close attention right now. NFRA is conducting an ongoing consultation on revisions to SA 600 that are expected to introduce more explicit requirements around component auditor independence documentation. Simultaneously, the [ICAI Ethics](/blog/icai-peer-review-stage-2-audit-preparation/) Board is reviewing whether additional disclosure thresholds should apply specifically to listed group entities. Both developments point in the same direction: the documentation bar will rise, not fall.
