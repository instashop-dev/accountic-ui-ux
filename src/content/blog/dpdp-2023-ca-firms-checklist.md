The Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 came into force in stages through 2024 and 2025, and the parts that matter to a practicing CA firm are now live. If your firm is still operating on a pre-2023 engagement letter, you're exposed — and a 2025-era listed-company client will, almost certainly, ask you about it.

Here's the seven-item checklist we've worked through at DPS &amp; Co. Adapt to your firm.

## 1. You are a Data Fiduciary. Act like one.

Under DPDP, when you process a client's personal data — and a tax notice containing a PAN, a GSTIN, a bank account number, a Form 26AS print is personal data — you're the Data Fiduciary. Your client is the Data Principal. This changes what you owe them:

- **Notice** under s. 5, at the time of collection.
- **Purpose limitation** — you can only use the data for the engagement scope.
- **Retention limits** — once the purpose is fulfilled, the data either goes back to the client or is deleted.
- **Rights** under Chapter II — access, correction, erasure, grievance.

Your engagement letter should spell every one of these out. A 2019-vintage engagement letter doesn't.

## 2. Update the engagement letter. Specifically.

The new engagement letter should include, at minimum:

- Purpose of processing (tax advisory, audit, notice reply, whatever the scope is).
- Categories of data collected (PAN, GSTIN, books of account, bank statements, notices).
- Retention period (e.g., seven years from engagement close, or until express deletion request).
- Third-party processors (your accounting software, your filing tool, your AI workbench if any).
- A "no cross-client use" commitment.
- Grievance officer contact.

If you use Accountic — or any AI tool — name it, name its India-residency posture, and name the DPA you've executed with it.

## 3. Map your subprocessors.

Every CA firm has subprocessors even if they don't call them that. Tally. WhatsApp (yes, if you move PDFs through it — stop). Gmail for firm comms. Your return-filing utility. The CAPTCHA-solver script an article used once. Document them. Terminate anything that doesn't meet the standard. Keep a list.

## 4. The "no training" commitment.

If your firm uses any AI tool — for notice drafting, for ledger classification, for document summarisation — your client is going to ask whether their data trains the underlying model. The answer should be no, and it should be **in writing from the vendor.** A tool that cannot commit to that in its DPA is a tool that a compliance-aware firm cannot use on listed-company work.

## 5. Data residency: India-resident or India-residents'-data-resident.

If any of your clients are listed companies, regulated entities, or government-adjacent, their legal teams will insist on India-resident processing. "Encrypted in transit to a US region" does not meet that bar. Know where every subprocessor stores the data, and be ready to hand over that answer on a one-page memo.

## 6. Breach notification workflow.

Under DPDP, a breach of personal data triggers notification obligations within a defined window. Most firms do not have a breach workflow at all. Build one now — even a one-page internal SOP is better than zero — and make sure the firm's engagement letter references it.

## 7. Retention clean-up.

Most CA firms have a decade of client data on shared drives, email archives, and partner laptops. DPDP's purpose limitation and erasure rights mean that data you don't need is a liability. Do one pass, once a year. Delete what you don't need. Document what you keep and why.
