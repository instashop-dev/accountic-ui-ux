The faceless assessment regime has a narrow but stubborn line of case law around one phrase: **application of mind**. The ITAT has, in repeated orders through 2024 and into 2025, set aside assessment orders where the AO's reasoning looks like a copy-paste from the show-cause. The same judicial discipline applies on the other side of the table — to the CA's reply.

## What the phrase actually means

"Application of mind" is not a slogan borrowed from a training module. It's a substantive test: did the decision-maker engage with the specific facts, specific law, and specific objections in front of them, or did they return a pro-forma output?

The test has two practical signals:

1. **The order answers the objection that was raised.** Not a similar-sounding objection. The one in front of it.
2. **The reasoning is specific to the facts.** Not "in view of the above submissions, the addition is justified," but a sentence a reader could not have written without reading the reply.

## What this means for a reply

The reply you file should be able to *force* application of mind. A boilerplate reply makes a boilerplate order possible. A specific reply makes a boilerplate order look suspicious on appeal.

Three concrete tests we apply at DPS &amp; Co. before a s. 143(3) or s. 147 reply leaves the firm:

- **No generic sentences until the prayer.** If a paragraph could appear in any reply, it doesn't belong here.
- **Every assertion has a source.** A bank statement page, a return acknowledgement, a confirmation letter, or a case citation. No floating claims.
- **Ground-by-ground numbering matches the notice.** If the notice raises four grounds, the reply has four numbered responses. Not three, not five.

## Why it matters for AI-drafted replies specifically

The judiciary has started noticing — not yet in named orders, but clearly in oral remarks — that generic AI-drafted pleadings are landing in assessment proceedings. A draft that has been passed through ChatGPT and filed verbatim, with fabricated citations or "application-of-mind-style" filler, is the fastest path to an adverse inference.

This is exactly why Accountic ships citation verification as a first-class feature. The reply you file has to look like you read the notice, thought about it, and answered the specific grounds. It can be drafted with AI. It cannot *read* as if drafted by AI.

## One self-check before filing

Read the reply aloud once. At any point, does a sentence feel like it could be lifted into a reply for a different client, a different AY, a different section? Rewrite it.

That single habit is what separates replies that survive appeal from replies that don't.
