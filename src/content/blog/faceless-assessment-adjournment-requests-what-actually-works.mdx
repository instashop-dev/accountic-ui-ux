After fifteen years of handling assessments, I can tell you that the shift to faceless proceedings has changed exactly one thing about adjournments: you can no longer walk into a room and explain yourself to a human being. Everything else — the legal standards, the burden on you as the representative, the consequences of silence — remains just as unforgiving. What has changed is that your written request now does all the work your personality used to do.

This guide covers what actually secures adjournments at NFAC, what gets you an ex-parte order instead, and how to protect your client's record when the system says no.

---

## Why Adjournment Requests Fail in [Faceless Assessment](/blog/faceless-assessment-under-section-144b-how-the-nfac-process/)

NFAC runs on automated timelines. The standard response window is 15 days from the date of notice, and the system does not pause that clock because a human being has not yet read your request. There is no adjudicating officer sitting across from you who can exercise discretion based on your explanation — the request either fits within acceptable parameters or it does not.

Generic requests citing "additional time is required to compile documents" are the single most common reason for rejection. The system has no mechanism to weigh the reasonableness of that statement. Without a specific, documentable ground backed by evidence, the request is treated as a non-response.

You also need to understand the distinction between two types of requests. A **Show Cause Notice (SCN) adjournment** — where NFAC has proposed an addition and asked you to respond — carries higher stakes and marginally more flexibility because an addition cannot technically be made without adequate opportunity. A **questionnaire response extension** is more mechanical and more likely to be rejected outright if the ground is weak.

Ex-parte orders in faceless proceedings are triggered at a lower threshold than in conventional assessment. Under Section 144B(1)(xii), if no response is filed and the deadline passes, the AU can proceed without further notice. The system is not waiting for you to catch up.

## The Legal Basis You Must Cite in Every Request

Filing an adjournment request without a statutory anchor is the equivalent of submitting a nil return with no computation — technically done, practically worthless.

The primary provision you should cite is **Section 144B(7) read with Section 144B(1)(xii)** of the Income Tax Act, 1961. Section 144B(7) explicitly requires that the assessee be given a reasonable opportunity of being heard before any variation is made. This is your procedural fairness anchor, and NFAC cannot override it.

**CBDT Circular No. 23/2019** and the subsequent directions issued when the faceless scheme was operationalised both affirm that the principles of natural justice apply to faceless proceedings. Citing these alongside Section 144B(7) signals that you know the regulatory framework and are not simply asking for a favour.

Where the timeline granted is genuinely unreasonable — say, a 7-day window to respond to a questionnaire requiring third-party bank documents — invoke **Article 14 of the Constitution** on the ground that unequal treatment without reasonable opportunity violates the right to equality. This is not hyperbolic; Bombay and Gujarat High Courts have upheld this argument in faceless contexts.

The Supreme Court's affirmation in **Pr. CIT v. Saurashtra Kutch Stock Exchange** on the principle of adequate opportunity remains good law and directly applicable. Citing it in your request places NFAC on notice that a denial may not survive scrutiny. You may also add **Section 282A** (authentication of notices) as an ancillary ground where there is any ambiguity about the DIN on the original notice — it rarely defeats the request on its own, but it strengthens the procedural record.

## Accepted Grounds vs. Rejected Grounds: A Field-Tested Breakdown

Based on portal experience across multiple NFAC cycles, here is how grounds actually perform:

| Ground | Outcome | What Makes It Work (or Fail) |
|---|---|---|
| Medical emergency of assessee | **Accepted** | Hospital letterhead with admission dates, doctor's certificate on clinic letterhead |
| Medical emergency of authorised representative | **Accepted with caveats** | Certificate required; must explain why no other CA can respond |
| Third-party document procurement delay (bank, foreign entity) | **Accepted** | Email trail showing request was made *before* the notice date |
| Portal upload technical failure | **Accepted** | ITBA error screenshot with timestamp + IT helpdesk ticket number |
| CA travelling / attending another hearing | **Rejected** | Professional unavailability is not the assessee's ground |
| "Awaiting information from client" (bare statement) | **Rejected** | No specificity, no third-party, no evidence of effort |
| Audit season workload | **Borderline** | Only works when combined with a specific alternate date and partial response filed simultaneously |
| Documents voluminous beyond portal limits | **Accepted** | Screenshot of upload error and a list of documents pending upload as an annexure |
| "Insufficient time to prepare" (no further detail) | **Rejected** | Indistinguishable from delay tactic — always rejected |

The pattern here is consistent: accepted grounds are *specific*, *documentable*, and *outside the control of the assessee or representative*. Rejected grounds are vague, within professional control, or unverifiable.

## Drafting the Request: Language That Clears NFAC Scrutiny

The opening paragraph of your request carries disproportionate weight. State the **PAN, Assessment Year, DIN of the notice, and the exact relief sought** within the first three lines. Do not build to your point — lead with it.

> *"In connection with the notice dated [DATE] bearing DIN [XXXX], issued under Section [XXX] of the Income Tax Act, 1961, for Assessment Year [XXXX-XX], the assessee [NAME, PAN: XXXXXXXXXXX] respectfully submits that the response presently due on [DATE] cannot be filed for the following specific and documentable reasons, and requests an extension to [SPECIFIC DATE]."*

Use formal, passive construction throughout. "The assessee is unable to furnish the documents" reads better to an automated system than "I cannot provide the information." Avoid emotional language — phrases like "great hardship" or "serious prejudice" are invisible to a system that is scanning for keywords and structure.

Identify the specific document you are waiting for and name the institution. "A certificate confirming the nature of deposits held in the NRE account is awaited from HDFC Bank, Fort Branch, Mumbai, as requested vide email dated [DATE]" is actionable and verifiable. "Bank documents are pending" is not.

Always propose a **specific date** for your extended response — not "reasonable time" or "sufficient opportunity." If you are asking for an extension because of a bank certificate, look at the bank's actual processing time (typically 7–10 working days for certified statements) and work backward to a date you can commit to.

Close with an explicit undertaking:

> *"The assessee undertakes that no further adjournment shall be sought in respect of this notice if the extension to [DATE] is granted, and that a complete response will be filed on or before that date."*

This undertaking matters. It signals finality and good faith — both of which improve the request's odds.

## Supporting Documents: What to Attach and How to Format Them

An adjournment request without supporting documents is an assertion. NFAC does not act on assertions.

**For medical grounds**, attach a hospital letterhead certificate with admission and discharge dates. A WhatsApp image of a prescription does not meet the threshold. The certificate should be on clinic or hospital letterhead, signed and stamped. If the medical emergency relates to the assessee personally, also attach a brief note explaining why no authorised representative could have responded on behalf of the assessee.

**For third-party document delays**, the strongest evidence is an email chain showing that the request was made *before* the original notice date, or at least within 48 hours of receiving it. A request made on the day the adjournment is filed proves nothing. Forward the bank's or institution's acknowledgement of receipt with your submission.

**For portal technical issues**, an ITBA error screenshot with a visible timestamp is the minimum. If you have an IT helpdesk ticket number from the income tax portal's support system, include it — this is corroborating, system-generated evidence that NFAC cannot dismiss.

On formatting:

- File naming: `PAN_AY_ADJ_REQ_DDMMYYYY` — this matters for tracking and avoids rejection on administrative grounds
- PDF size: **5MB per attachment** is the NFAC portal limit — compress files using Adobe Acrobat or a PDF compressor, but check legibility after compression
- Consolidate everything into a **single PDF** rather than multiple attachments where possible — it reduces the chance of one document being overlooked and makes the submission cleaner

## The Submission Mechanics: Timing and Portal Steps That Matter

**File at least 5 working days before the response due date.** Same-day adjournment requests are almost never granted because there is no processing time for the system to act and communicate a decision before the deadline. If you file on Day 14 of a 15-day window, you are effectively filing no adjournment at all.

The portal workflow is:

1. Log in to the Income Tax e-Filing portal with the assessee's credentials (or as authorised representative)
2. Navigate to **e-Proceedings → Response to Outstanding Demand / Notice**
3. Select the relevant notice and look for **Submit Adjournment Request** — this is a separate option from filing the substantive response
4. If the adjournment option is not visible (this happens intermittently), use **'Any Other'** submission type and clearly label the subject as "Adjournment Request — DIN [XXXX] — AY [XXXX-XX]"
5. Upload your consolidated PDF (named per convention above)
6. Submit and **immediately download the acknowledgement PDF with the submission number**

A critical step that many practitioners skip: **file a partial substantive response at the same time as the adjournment request.** If you are waiting for one bank certificate but can respond to eight of the twelve questions in the questionnaire, respond to those eight now. A partial response demonstrates good faith and, more importantly, prevents an ex-parte order even if the adjournment is denied. Silence is the only thing that guarantees an ex-parte.

If no decision on the adjournment is received within 5 working days, raise a **Grievance on the Income Tax portal** referencing the submission acknowledgement number. This creates a paper trail and sometimes prompts a manual review.

## When the Adjournment Is Rejected: Immediate Next Steps

A rejected adjournment is not the end of the matter — but only if you act before the deadline passes.

**File the best available partial response before the deadline.** This is non-negotiable. A partial response with a covering letter preserves your right to contest the assessment. Silence converts a procedural problem into a substantive liability.

Your covering letter within the partial response should state, explicitly, that the adjournment was wrongly denied — cite Section 144B(7) again — that the assessee is unable to file a complete response within the time available for the specific documented reasons, and that all rights to challenge the adequacy of the opportunity are reserved. Keep this to half a page. It is a record-preservation document, not an argument.

If NFAC proceeds to pass an assessment order, invoke the **appellate mechanism under Section 250** before CIT(A). The denied adjournment is now a procedural ground of appeal. Attach the original adjournment request, the rejection, and your covering letter as part of the appeal record.

For cases where the addition is significant and the denial of opportunity is clear-cut — short timelines, specific documented grounds that were rejected without reasons — a **writ petition under Article 226 before the High Court** is available. The Bombay High Court's ruling in **Sesa Goa** on the principle of *audi alteram partem* applies directly. The Gujarat and Delhi High Courts have similarly intervened where faceless proceedings proceeded without adequate opportunity.

The one thing you must never do: allow the deadline to pass without filing anything while waiting to see what happens. That is the only scenario with no recovery path.

## Practical Checklist Before You Submit Any Adjournment Request

Run through this before hitting submit:

- [ ] **DIN of original notice** correctly quoted in the request body
- [ ] **Specific alternate response date** proposed — not "reasonable time" or "sufficient opportunity"
- [ ] **At least one statutory provision** cited (minimum: Section 144B(7))
- [ ] **Supporting document** attached in PDF format, under 5MB, named `PAN_AY_ADJ_REQ_DDMMYYYY`
- [ ] **Partial substantive response** prepared and ready to file simultaneously
- [ ] **Prior communications referenced** by acknowledgement number if this is a repeat or follow-up request
- [ ] **Undertaking of no further adjournment** included in the closing paragraph of the request
- [ ] Request filed at least **5 working days before** the due date
- [ ] Submission acknowledgement PDF **downloaded and saved** immediately after filing

---

## Your Next Step

Pull up the last adjournment request you filed — or the template you currently use — and check it against this checklist. If it does not have a DIN reference, a specific alternate date, a statutory citation, and a no-further-adjournment undertaking, it is underperforming.

The gap between adjournment requests that work and those that do not is almost entirely in the drafting and timing. The law is on your side under Section 144B(7) — but you have to invoke it correctly and early enough for it to matter.
