A Section 148 notice landing on your client's desk three, five, or seven years after the relevant assessment year is not an automatic death sentence for the taxpayer's position. The Finance Act 2021 completely rewired the reassessment machinery, and if you know exactly where to look, most extended-period notices have at least one — often more than one — fatal defect baked right in.

This guide is for practising CAs who need a working playbook, not a textbook summary of the law. We cover the full arc: from the moment the notice arrives to the post-assessment appeal stage, with templates, timelines, and the counter-arguments you will face from AOs.

---

## The New Reassessment Framework: What Changed in 2021

The Finance Act 2021 replaced the old Sections 147 to 151 entirely with effect from 1 April 2021. The single most important shift is the replacement of the subjective **"reason to believe"** standard with an objective **"information with the AO"** standard as defined in Explanation 1 to Section 148. This is not a cosmetic change — it fundamentally alters the AO's burden.

Under the old regime, an AO could initiate reopening based on his own reasoning on facts already on record, as long as he could articulate a belief that income had escaped assessment. Post-2021, reopening must be triggered by **specific information from defined external sources** — not the AO's re-reading of the original file.

### The Two Time-Limit Tiers

| Window | Condition | Approval Required |
|---|---|---|
| Up to 3 years from end of AY | Any amount of escaped income with valid information | JCIT / Additional CIT |
| 3 to 10 years from end of AY | Escaped income must be **₹50 lakh or more** (per the 148A(d) order) | PCIT / CIT |
| Beyond 10 years | Not permissible under the new framework | N/A |

The ₹50 lakh threshold for the extended window is not a guideline — it is a jurisdictional condition precedent. If the AO cannot demonstrate at the 148A(d) stage itself that escaped income is at least ₹50 lakh, the notice beyond three years is void.

### Section 148A: The Mandatory Pre-Notice Inquiry

Parliament introduced Section 148A as a hard procedural gate. Before issuing a Section 148 notice, the AO must now:
1. Conduct an inquiry under 148A(a) (if required, with prior approval)
2. Serve a show-cause notice under 148A(b) giving the assessee **at least 7 days** to respond
3. Consider the reply and pass a **speaking order** under 148A(d) with prior approval from the specified authority

Only after completing all three steps can a valid Section 148 notice be issued. Skipping or abbreviating any step is not a curable irregularity — it is a jurisdictional defect.

### The Ashish Agarwal Judgment and the Transition Period

The Supreme Court's decision in **Union of India v. Ashish Agarwal (2022) 444 ITR 1 (SC)** addressed the chaos that arose when the Department issued Section 148 notices under the old regime even after the Finance Act 2021 came into force. The Court validated those notices but directed that they be treated as show-cause notices under Section 148A(b), requiring the AO to follow the fresh procedure before proceeding. If your client received a notice in the 2021–22 transition window, verify whether the AO actually completed the 148A procedure post-Ashish Agarwal — many did not.

## Decoding the Notice: Key Red Flags to Check Before Responding

The moment a Section 148 notice arrives, do not draft a substantive reply first. Run this checklist. In my experience, at least 40% of extended-period notices fail on one or more of these grounds.

### Pre-Response Checklist

**1. Date of Issue vs. Assessment Year**

Calculate the exact number of years between the end of the relevant AY and the date of notice issue. For a notice issued on 15 March 2024 for AY 2019-20, the AY ended on 31 March 2020 — that is just under 4 years. This falls in the 3–10 year window, meaning the ₹50 lakh threshold must be met. If the notice is for AY 2017-18, you are looking at nearly 7 years — still within the extended window, but the ₹50 lakh condition becomes even more critical to scrutinise.

**2. Prior 148A(b) Show-Cause Notice**

Verify that a show-cause notice under 148A(b) was issued before the Section 148 notice. Check the date gap — the assessee must have received **at least 7 days** to reply. A show-cause notice issued on Day 1 and the Section 148 notice issued on Day 5 is defective on its face.

**3. The 148A(d) Speaking Order**

Obtain the 148A(d) order (the AO is obligated to provide it, and you should demand it immediately). This order must:
- Specifically identify the information that triggered reopening
- Quantify the escaped income and establish it meets the ₹50 lakh threshold (for extended cases)
- Bear the approval of the specified authority under Section 151
- Be reasoned — a one-line order saying "information received from DUVVADA indicates escaped income" does not qualify as a speaking order

**4. The Source of "Information"**

The specific information cited must fall within the exhaustive categories in Explanation 1 to Section 148. Vague references to "information received from the department" or "analysis of returns" are not adequate. Demand the underlying document.

**5. ITBA-Generated DIN**

Every notice issued by the Department must carry a valid Document Identification Number generated through the ITBA portal, per **CBDT Circular No. 19/2019**. A notice without a DIN, or with a manually assigned reference number, is invalid. This is a quick, often overlooked check that can immediately void a notice.

## Understanding the 'Information' Requirement for Extended Reopening

The word "information" in Section 148 is not open-ended. Explanation 1 provides an exhaustive list of qualifying sources. As a CA advising a client on a 3–10 year reopening, your first job is to determine whether the AO's claimed information actually fits within this closed list.

### Qualifying Sources of Information

- **DUVVADA (Data Upload and Verification):** Flags generated through the Department's risk profiling system
- **Form 26AS / AIS mismatches:** Discrepancies between amounts reflected in AIS and the return filed — but see the caveat below
- **SFT (Statement of Financial Transactions):** Filings by banks, registrars, mutual funds under Section 285BA
- **Survey material:** Documents seized or statements recorded during a survey under Section 133A
- **Audit objections:** Revenue audit or internal audit objections that meet the prescribed format
- **Information from other investigations:** Material gathered during search and seizure operations, even of third parties, provided the linkage to the assessee's income is specific and direct

### What Courts Have Held as Insufficient

The Bombay and Delhi High Courts have consistently quashed notices where the AO's "information" was really just a **change of opinion** — a fresh view on facts already examined during the original scrutiny assessment. If the AO accepted a particular deduction in the original assessment under Section 143(3), he cannot reopen merely because he now disagrees with that decision. There must be genuinely new information, not a re-examination of old material.

AIS mismatches are a particular grey area. Courts have held that an internal data discrepancy, standing alone, is not "information" that justifies reopening — especially if the mismatch can be explained by timing differences or double-reporting by multiple deductors. The AO must corroborate the AIS discrepancy with external, tangible information before using it to trigger extended-period reopening.

The ₹50 lakh quantification is also non-negotiable at the 148A(d) stage. The AO cannot say "escaped income is expected to be substantial" — he must identify specific income, from the specific information, and arrive at a figure of ₹50 lakh or more. Estimates based on "potential" or extrapolation from unrelated third-party data will not survive challenge.

## Step-by-Step Response Workflow for 148 Notices Beyond 3 Years

Here is the workflow I use for every extended-period 148 notice. Do not skip steps, and do not reorder them. Sequence matters both legally and strategically.

### The 6-Step Response Workflow

**Step 1 — Acknowledge and Preserve (Day 0)**

The moment the notice arrives (whether by post, e-mail, or on the e-filing portal), note the exact date of receipt in writing. Log into the ITBA/e-filing portal and download the notice, the 148A(d) order, and any prior 148A(b) notice. Create a timestamped file with all documents. Check whether the notice carries a valid DIN.

**Step 2 — File Return Under Protest (Within 30 Days)**

File the return of income required under Section 148. This is mandatory — non-filing exposes your client to ex-parte assessment. However, the return must be filed with an explicit covering letter stating:

> *"This return is filed without prejudice to and while reserving all rights and objections as to the validity, jurisdiction, and legality of the notice issued under Section 148 dated [date]. The filing of this return shall not be construed as acceptance of the jurisdiction of this notice or as a waiver of any preliminary objections."*

**Step 3 — Draft and File Preliminary Objections (Within 7–10 Days)**

Before you do anything else substantively, file written preliminary objections before the AO. This is the GKN Driveshafts paper trail. The objections must specifically challenge jurisdiction and limitation — I cover the drafting template in the next section.

**Step 4 — Demand Disclosure of Information and Approval Order**

Within the preliminary objections (or separately via a written application), formally request the AO to disclose:
- The exact "information" relied upon under Section 148A(a)
- A copy of the 148A(d) order if not already provided
- The approval granted by the specified authority under Section 151, including the designation of the approving authority

The AO is obligated to provide this. If he refuses, that refusal itself becomes a ground for writ.

**Step 5 — Await Speaking Order on Preliminary Objections**

Do not participate substantively in assessment proceedings until the AO has disposed of your preliminary objections by a speaking order. If the AO calls for substantive documents before disposing of objections, respond in writing that you await disposal of preliminary objections first, while reiterating your reservation clause.

**Step 6 — Evaluate Writ vs. Continue Assessment**

If the AO rejects your preliminary objections, you face a fork in the road. You can:
- File a writ petition in the High Court immediately, before the assessment order is passed
- Continue participating in assessment proceedings on merits (under protest) while preserving the jurisdictional challenge for CIT(A)/ITAT/court

The writ route is preferable when the jurisdictional defect is clear and the facts are largely undisputed. If the facts are complex or the ₹50 lakh escaped income may actually be provable, the assessment route preserves more options.

## Drafting the Preliminary Objection Letter: Template and Key Arguments

Here is a structured template you can adapt. Every word in the opening paragraph is deliberate — do not paraphrase it loosely.

---

**[Client's Name / PAN]**
**Assessment Year: [AY]**
**Notice under Section 148 dated: [Date]**

**To:**
The Assessing Officer,
[Ward / Circle]

**Sub: Preliminary Objections to Jurisdiction — Notice under Section 148 dated [Date] — Without Prejudice**

Sir/Madam,

The assessee hereby formally and specifically objects to the jurisdiction exercised through the above notice, prior to and without submitting to the merits of the reassessment proceedings proposed. This letter constitutes the preliminary objection as required by the Hon'ble Supreme Court in *GKN Driveshafts (India) Ltd. v. ITO (2002) 259 ITR 19 (SC)*. Any response filed by the assessee in these proceedings is and shall remain without prejudice to these preliminary objections and shall not constitute submission to jurisdiction or waiver of any ground raised herein.

**Argument 1 — Limitation**

The relevant assessment year is AY [XX]. The assessment year ended on 31 March [YY]. The notice under Section 148 was issued on [Date], which is [X] years and [Y] months after the end of the assessment year. The 3-year normal limitation period expired on [Date]. Reopening beyond 3 years requires, as a jurisdictional condition precedent under the proviso to Section 149(1), that the escaped income is ₹50 lakh or more. The 148A(d) order dated [Date] does not establish this threshold based on identified, specific income — it relies on [describe: estimated/projected/circumstantial basis]. This does not satisfy the statutory requirement.

**Argument 2 — Defective 148A Procedure**

[Choose applicable sub-arguments:]
- No show-cause notice under 148A(b) was served prior to the 148 notice *(if applicable)*
- The show-cause notice was dated [Date] and the Section 148 notice was dated [Date] — a gap of [X] days, which is less than the mandatory 7-day period
- The 148A(d) order is not a speaking order — it does not identify the specific information, does not quantify escaped income, and does not record the reasoning of the AO
- The approval under Section 151 was not obtained from the specified authority

**Argument 3 — Stale or Vague Information / Change of Opinion**

The "information" cited in the 148A(b) notice / 148A(d) order is [describe: AIS mismatch / DUVVADA flag / other]. This information was [available during original scrutiny / does not pertain to specific income of the assessee / amounts to a change of opinion on material already examined under Section 143(3) assessment completed on [Date]]. The Hon'ble [Bombay / Delhi] High Court has held in [cite case] that change of opinion on the same facts does not constitute fresh "information" under Explanation 1 to Section 148.

**Argument 4 — Invalid Approval Under Section 151**

For notices beyond 3 years, Section 151(1) requires sanction from the **Principal Commissioner or Commissioner of Income Tax**. The approval in the present case has been granted by [designation as stated in the 148A(d) order]. This designation does not satisfy the statutory requirement. [If an Additional CIT or JCIT has granted approval for an extended-period notice, this is a clear ground for quashing.]

**Prayer:**

In light of the above, the assessee respectfully prays that:
1. These preliminary objections be disposed of by a reasoned speaking order before any further proceedings are conducted.
2. The notice under Section 148 dated [Date] be dropped and the proposed reassessment proceedings be closed as without jurisdiction.

## Writ Petition Route: When and How to Approach the High Court

A writ under Article 226 of the Constitution is not the first resort — but it is often the most powerful one when the jurisdictional defect is clear on the face of the record. Here is how to decide when to file.

### File a Writ When:

- The AO rejects preliminary objections without a reasoned order
- The AO proceeds to substantive assessment without disposing of preliminary objections
- The 148A procedure was completely bypassed or clearly violated
- The approving authority was demonstrably of the wrong designation
- The notice is clearly time-barred on a mathematical calculation of dates

Do not file a writ simply because you disagree with the AO's factual findings on escaped income. Writs under Article 226 are reserved for jurisdictional and procedural illegality, not factual disputes.

### Building the Pre-Writ Paper Trail

The GKN Driveshafts doctrine requires that the assessee exhaust the internal remedy of filing preliminary objections before approaching the writ court. Without this paper trail, the High Court may dismiss the writ as premature. Ensure you have:
- A copy of the preliminary objections filed with proof of acknowledgment
- The AO's rejection order (or evidence of non-disposal)
- The return filed under protest with the covering letter

### Grounds for Writ (Summary)

1. Violation of the mandatory 148A procedure
2. Approval by a wrong-level authority under Section 151
3. Notice issued beyond the limitation period
4. Complete absence of qualifying "information" as defined under Explanation 1

### Interim Relief

Always pray for a **stay of assessment proceedings** as interim relief in the writ petition. An AO who has received a writ notice will typically pause proceedings, but without an express stay order, he is not legally obligated to do so. An ex-parte assessment order passed after the writ is filed creates unnecessary complications.

### Territorial Jurisdiction

File in the High Court having territorial jurisdiction over the AO's location. In centralised processing or faceless assessment cases, this question is nuanced — the Bombay HC has held that the jurisdictional HC is determined by the AO's base location, not the taxpayer's address. Get this right before filing.

### Key Judgments to Cite

| Court | Case | Point of Law |
|---|---|---|
| Bombay HC | Siemens Financial Services Pvt. Ltd. | Validity of 148A procedure; non-speaking 148A(d) order quashed |
| Delhi HC | Suman Jeet Shali v. ITO | Mandatory nature of 148A; PCIT approval requirement |
| Allahabad HC | Rajeev Bansal v. UOI | Transition period notices; Ashish Agarwal compliance |
| Supreme Court | GKN Driveshafts (India) Ltd. v. ITO | Preliminary objection procedure before participation |
| Supreme Court | Union of India v. Ashish Agarwal | Validation and restriction of transition-period 148 notices |

## Common AO Counter-Arguments and How to Rebut Them

AOs defending extended-period notices tend to recycle the same five or six arguments. Here is each one, and the precise rebuttal to deploy.

### Counter-Argument 1: "AIS Mismatch Constitutes Information"

**AO's position:** The AIS shows credit of ₹X lakh not reflected in the return — this itself is qualifying "information."

**Your rebuttal:** AIS data is internally generated by the Department from TDS and SFT filings. Multiple courts, including the Bombay HC, have held that an AIS mismatch standing alone is not "information" within Explanation 1 to Section 148. The mismatch must be corroborated by tangible, external information — a specific SFT filing, a third-party statement, or survey material — establishing that the credit represents undisclosed income of the assessee. The AO has not produced any such corroborating material. Further, the mismatch is fully explainable by [TDS from multiple deductors for the same income / timing difference / misclassification in AIS].

### Counter-Argument 2: "Escaped Income Estimated at ₹50 Lakh Based on Potential"

**AO's position:** Based on the information available, it is estimated that escaped income could exceed ₹50 lakh.

**Your rebuttal:** The ₹50 lakh threshold is a jurisdictional condition, not an estimate. The Supreme Court and multiple High Courts have held that the AO must identify specific income, from specific information, and arrive at a concrete figure of ₹50 lakh or more at the 148A(d) stage itself. Hypothetical extrapolation or "potential" escapement does not satisfy this requirement. The 148A(d) order in the present case uses the language "[quote the estimate language]" — which is precisely the kind of speculative quantification that courts have repeatedly struck down.

### Counter-Argument 3: "Approval Was Obtained from PCIT"

**AO's position:** The sanction required under Section 151 has been duly obtained from the Principal Commissioner.

**Your rebuttal:** Verify the designation carefully. The approval document should be obtained and scrutinised. In several instances, approval has been granted by an **Additional Commissioner of Income Tax holding in-charge charge of PCIT** — this does not satisfy the requirement of Section 151(1), which mandates approval from the substantive PCIT or CIT. The Bombay HC has quashed notices where the approving authority held only an in-charge designation. If the designation in the approval order does not unambiguously show a substantive PCIT/CIT, the approval is invalid.

### Counter-Argument 4: "Assessee Participated in Proceedings, Hence Objection Waived"

**AO's position:** By filing a return and responding to notices, the assessee has submitted to jurisdiction and cannot now challenge the same.

**Your rebuttal:** This argument is settled against the Department. The Supreme Court in *GKN Driveshafts* specifically held that filing a return and participating in assessment proceedings, after filing preliminary objections on record, does not constitute waiver of jurisdictional challenges. The preliminary objection letter filed on [date] and acknowledged by this office on [date] expressly reserves all jurisdictional rights. Every subsequent submission in these proceedings has been made "without prejudice" to those objections, as stated in each covering letter. There has been no waiver.

### Counter-Argument 5: "Notice Was Issued Within Time — Computation Runs from End of AY"

**AO's position:** The three-year period runs from the end of the assessment year, and the notice was issued within that window.

**Your rebuttal:** Confirm this independently. The Finance Act 2021, as it stands, computes the limitation period from the **end of the relevant assessment year** — not from the date of the original assessment order, not from the date of filing of the return. For AY 2019-20, the AY ended on 31 March 2020. The three-year normal limit expired on 31 March 2023. If the notice was issued even one day after this date, it falls in the extended-period window and the ₹50 lakh condition must be met. Cross-check the date computation arithmetically, and do not rely on the AO's own calculation.

## Assessment Stage: Conducting Proceedings Without Conceding Jurisdiction

If the preliminary objections are rejected and you decide not to file a writ immediately, you must participate in the assessment proceedings — but you must do so in a way that keeps your jurisdictional challenge alive and on record.

### The Reservation Clause

Every single submission you make — whether in response to a Section 142(1) notice, a questionnaire, or a hearing — must begin with this paragraph:

> *"Without prejudice to the preliminary objections to jurisdiction filed on [date] and acknowledged by this office, and reserving all rights thereunder, the assessee provides the following information/documents…"*

This is not boilerplate. It is the legal mechanism that prevents the AO — or a higher forum — from later arguing that your substantive participation constituted acceptance of jurisdiction.

### Responding to 142(1) and 143(2) Notices

Do not ignore Section 142(1) or 143(2) notices on the ground that you have pending jurisdictional objections. Non-compliance entitles the AO to pass a best-judgment assessment under Section 144, which creates a far more complicated record for writ or appeal purposes. Respond fully on the merits, with the reservation clause at the top of every response.

### Maintaining a Parallel Paper Trail

Keep a contemporaneous log of every document filed, every notice received, and every hearing attended. Note the dates, DIN numbers, and the substance of any oral observations made by the AO. This record becomes your appellate and writ record. If the AO makes verbal assurances that he will reconsider the jurisdictional objections, follow up in writing requesting a formal order.
