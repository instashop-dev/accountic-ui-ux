The Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 received Presidential assent on 11 August 2023. The rules are still being finalised, but the core obligations — consent, purpose limitation, data principal rights — are already enacted law. If your firm is waiting for the gazette notification of rules before acting, you are already behind.

This is not a law school overview. This is a working checklist for CA firms that file ITRs, respond to income tax notices, and hold years of client financial data. Let us go through exactly what needs to change.

---

## Why DPDP Act 2023 Changes Everything for CA Firms

Most CAs assume data protection law applies to tech companies and banks. That assumption needs to go.

The moment your firm collects a client's PAN card, Aadhaar copy, bank statement, or salary slip — you are processing **personal data** under the DPDP Act. That makes your firm a **Data Fiduciary**, legally responsible for how that data is collected, stored, used, and eventually deleted.

The financial exposure is real. Penalties for non-compliance can reach **₹250 crore for Significant Data Fiduciaries**. Even for smaller firms, the Data Protection Board can levy penalties for failure to implement reasonable security safeguards or ignoring client data rights requests.

What makes this particularly relevant for CA practice is the nature of the data you handle. ITR filing involves Aadhaar-PAN linking data, bank account details, employer information, and asset schedules. Notice responses involve even more sensitive material — transaction details, foreign asset disclosures, property valuations. Every individual client you work with is a **Data Principal** whose rights you are now legally obligated to respect.

---

## Which Client Data Types Fall Under DPDP Scope

Not all data types carry the same compliance weight, but a surprisingly large portion of what a typical CA firm handles falls squarely within scope.

| Data Type | Collected During | DPDP Status |
|---|---|---|
| PAN and Aadhaar numbers | KYC, ITR onboarding | Directly identifiable — consent required |
| Bank account numbers and IFSC | ITR refund details, GST registration | Personal data — consent required |
| Salary slips and Form 16 | ITR filing (salaried clients) | Personal financial data — consent required |
| Capital gains statements | ITR filing, notice response | Financial data — consent required |
| Passport and visa details | Foreign income, FEMA matters | Directly identifiable — consent required |
| Income tax notice documents | Notice response workflow | Sensitive financial data — consent required |
| WhatsApp/email document threads | Ad hoc document collection | Consent required if stored or processed |
| Data of deceased clients | Estate and succession matters | Special handling — legal heir authorisation needed |
| Data of minor clients | Returns filed for minors | Guardian consent mandatory |

The practical takeaway here: if you are collecting it for a client engagement, it is almost certainly in scope. The question is whether you have the right consent framework in place before collection begins.

Data shared informally — a WhatsApp forward of a Form 26AS, a salary slip sent to a personal email — still counts as data you have received and are now processing. Informal collection does not create an informal compliance obligation.

## Consent Framework Requirements: What CA Firms Must Build

Under the DPDP Act, consent must satisfy five conditions simultaneously: it must be **free, specific, informed, unconditional, and unambiguous**. Your existing signed engagement letter almost certainly does not meet all five.

A standard engagement letter says something like "the client agrees to provide all documents necessary for the engagement." That is not specific enough. The DPDP Act requires you to tell the client, before collection, exactly what data you will collect, for what purpose, with whom it may be shared, and for how long it will be retained.

**The purpose limitation rule is the one most firms will violate without realising it.** If a client gives consent for you to file their ITR, you cannot use that same data set to pitch them for a loan advisory service, send them marketing material, or onboard them onto a new GST compliance retainer without fresh consent. Each new purpose needs its own consent.

Key consent framework requirements to build into your firm's processes:

1. **Consent notice in plain language** — no legal jargon, and available in the client's preferred Indian language if requested.
2. **Timestamped consent log** — record when consent was given, through what channel (email, DocuSign, wet signature), and for which specific purpose.
3. **Separate consent for third-party sharing** — if client data goes to any software platform (ClearTax, Tally, TRACES), an outsourced article clerk team, or a co-partner firm, you need documented consent for that disclosure.
4. **Consent withdrawal mechanism** — clients must be able to withdraw consent, and your firm must have a process to act on that withdrawal.

The consent log is not bureaucratic overhead — it is your first line of defence if a client later disputes what they authorised.

## Pre-Engagement Checklist: Before Onboarding a New Client

This is the phase where most firms currently have the biggest gap. Documents are collected before any formal consent process takes place. Fix this first.

**Step 1: Send a DPDP-compliant privacy notice before requesting any documents.**
This notice should state what data you will collect, why, who will access it, where it will be stored, and how long it will be retained. Keep it to one page. It is not a contract — it is an information document.

**Step 2: Obtain explicit, purpose-specific consent.**
Use a consent form that lists the specific services: ITR filing for AY 2025-26, GST return filing, income tax notice response, etc. Each service should have a separate checkbox or acknowledgement. A blanket "I agree to share all documents" is not sufficient.

**Step 3: Verify the client's age.**
If the client is below 18, you need verifiable parental or guardian consent before processing any data. This applies to ITRs filed for minors, UGAM investments held in minor names, and any engagement where a minor is the data principal.

**Step 4: Define and communicate the data retention period.**
Tell the client upfront: "We retain your documents for [X] years as required under the Income Tax Act. After that period, your data will be securely deleted." This sets expectations and reduces friction when erasure requests come in later.

**Step 5: Assign an internal data handler.**
Every client engagement should have one named person in the firm who is responsible for that client's data security. This is not a DPDP Act requirement per se, but it is essential for accountability when something goes wrong.

## Active Engagement Checklist: ITR Filing and Notice Response Workflows

Once the engagement begins, the data handling risks multiply. Here is what to enforce at the workflow level.

**Storage and access controls:**
- All client documents must go onto firm-level encrypted storage — not personal laptops, not personal Google Drive accounts.
- Access to client files should be role-based: the article clerk handling data entry should not have access to the partner-level notice response files.
- Maintain an access log showing who opened which client file and when.

**Communication protocols:**
- Do not share PAN, Aadhaar, or bank details over regular WhatsApp or personal email. Use firm email with TLS encryption, or a dedicated client portal.
- If a client sends documents over WhatsApp informally, acknowledge receipt and request re-submission through the proper channel. Delete the WhatsApp copy after transfer.

**Third-party disclosure logging:**
When you upload a client's data anywhere — the income tax e-filing portal, TRACES, GST portal, MCA — log it. Record the date, the platform, what data was uploaded, and under which engagement. This becomes your disclosure register.

**Scenario — Notice Response for Mr. Aditya Sharma, PAN ABCDE1234F, AY 2022-23:**
His case was selected under Section 148A. In responding to the notice, your team accessed his capital gains statements, bank statements for FY 2021-22, and property sale documents. Under [DPDP compliance](/blog/dpdp-act-2023-client-data-handling-checklist-for-ca-firms/), your file should show: (a) consent was obtained at onboarding for notice response work, (b) only documents relevant to the Section 148A query were compiled and shared, (c) the physical documents were stored in locked filing, and (d) the digital response package was sent via firm email, not personal Gmail.

The principle of **data minimisation** applies here. Share only what the notice specifically asks for. Do not attach additional financial documents "for context" if they were not requested.

## Third-Party and Cloud Software: Compliance Obligations

This is the section most CA firms will find uncomfortable, because the current situation is almost universally non-compliant.

Under the DPDP Act, your firm's liability as a Data Fiduciary does not end when data leaves your server. If you share client data with a third-party software vendor, outsourced team, or cloud platform, **you remain responsible** for how that data is handled.

| Vendor Type | Risk Level | Required Action |
|---|---|---|
| Tax software (ClearTax, TaxCloud, Winman) | High — processes full ITR data | Verify their DPDP compliance; sign Data Processing Agreement (DPA) |
| Cloud storage (firm-level Google Workspace, Microsoft 365) | Medium — stores all client documents | Ensure enterprise plan with DPA; disable personal account sync |
| Free tools (personal Dropbox, Google Sheets) | Very High — no DPA, no data residency guarantee | Stop immediately; migrate to firm-controlled storage |
| Outsourced article clerks or data entry teams | High — direct access to raw client data | Sign confidentiality and data handling agreement before access |
| Co-sourcing arrangements with other CA firms | High — cross-firm data sharing | Formal DPA required; client consent for cross-firm sharing |

A **Data Processing Agreement (DPA)** is a contract between your firm and the vendor specifying what data they can process, for what purpose, with what security standards, and what happens to the data after the engagement ends. If your tax software vendor cannot produce a DPA, that is a significant red flag.

Do not wait for the software companies to approach you. Send them a written request asking for their DPDP compliance documentation and DPA template. Log the response (or non-response).

## Client Rights Under DPDP Act: What Your Firm Must Be Ready to Handle

The DPDP Act gives individual clients — your Data Principals — a set of rights they can exercise against your firm. You need internal processes to handle these before a client invokes them.

**Right to Access:** A client can ask your firm to tell them what personal data you currently hold about them. Your firm must be able to produce this information. This means your document storage must be searchable and organised by client.

**Right to Correction:** If a client believes data in your records is inaccurate — say, an old bank account number or a misspelled name on a draft return — they can demand correction. Your firm must have a process to verify and update records.

**Right to Erasure:** After an engagement ends, clients can request deletion of their personal data. This right is not absolute — it is overridden by your legal retention obligations under the Income Tax Act and other statutes. But once the statutory retention period expires, the data must go.

**Right to Grievance Redressal:** Your firm must designate a contact person or a documented process that clients can use to raise data complaints. This does not need to be a dedicated Data Protection Officer for smaller firms — it can be a named partner — but it must be documented and communicated to clients.

**Response timelines:** While the rules are yet to be notified, the DPDP Act framework and international practice suggest a 30-day response window for data rights requests. Build this into your client service protocols now.

## Data Retention and Deletion Policy for CA Firms

The tension between DPDP erasure rights and statutory record-keeping requirements is real, but it is manageable if you plan it properly.

**Statutory retention requirements override DPDP erasure requests during the retention period.** The Income Tax Act requires retention of books of accounts and supporting documents for 6 years from the end of the relevant AY in ordinary cases, and up to 8 years in cases involving specific claims. SEBI, FEMA, and GST have their own retention requirements that may apply depending on the client.

Once the statutory period expires, however, you cannot simply leave old data sitting in an archive folder indefinitely. The DPDP Act requires that data be **securely deleted** when it is no longer needed for the purpose it was collected.

**Build a document retention schedule like this:**

| Document Type | Statutory Retention Period | Action After Period |
|---|---|---|
| ITR acknowledgements and computation sheets | 6–8 years from AY | Secure digital deletion + physical shredding |
| Form 16, salary slips | 6 years from AY | Secure deletion |
| Capital gains supporting documents | 6–8 years (longer if appeal pending) | Secure deletion after appeal resolution |
| Notice correspondence and replies | Duration of proceedings + 2 years | Secure deletion |
| Bank statements and financial statements | 6 years from AY | Secure deletion |
| KYC documents (PAN, Aadhaar copies) | Duration of engagement + 2 years | Secure deletion |

Communicate this schedule to clients during onboarding. When a client later asks for their data to be deleted, you can refer to this schedule, explain what you are legally required to retain, and confirm what you will delete immediately.

For digital files, "deletion" means overwriting or cryptographic erasure — not moving to the Recycle Bin. For physical documents, it means cross-cut shredding.

## Practical DPDP Compliance Checklist: One-Page Summary for CA Firms

Use this as an internal audit tool. Go through each item and mark your firm's current status honestly.

**Pre-Engagement**
- [ ] Privacy notice drafted and shared with client before any document collection
- [ ] Explicit, purpose-specific consent obtained and logged with timestamp
- [ ] Minor client check completed; guardian consent obtained if applicable
- [ ] Data retention period communicated to client in writing
- [ ] Internal data handler assigned for this client

**During Engagement**
- [ ] All documents stored on firm-level encrypted storage only
- [ ] No client PAN, Aadhaar, or bank data shared over personal email or regular WhatsApp
- [ ] Access log maintained showing who accessed client files and when
- [ ] Third-party disclosures (e-filing portal, TRACES, GST portal) logged with date and purpose
- [ ] Data minimisation applied — only necessary data shared in notice responses

**Software and Vendors**
- [ ] Data Processing Agreements signed with all third-party software vendors
- [ ] Vendor DPDP compliance verified in writing
- [ ] Free/personal cloud tools removed from client data workflows
- [ ] Article clerks and outsourced staff bound by data handling agreements

**Client Rights**
- [ ] Grievance contact person designated and communicated to clients
- [ ] Internal process documented for handling access, correction, and erasure requests
- [ ] Response timeline (target: 30 days) built into client service protocols

**Post-Engagement**
- [ ] Retention schedule applied to all documents from the completed engagement
- [ ] Secure deletion process in place for expired data
- [ ] Client notified when their data has been deleted upon request

## Immediate Action Steps for CA Firms to Start DPDP Compliance

You do not need to wait for the final rules to be notified. The consent obligations, purpose limitation rules, and data principal rights are already in the enacted law. Here is where to start this week.

**Step 1 — Audit your current onboarding process.**
Pull out your standard engagement letter, your client onboarding email, and any document request templates you use. Check each one against the five consent requirements: free, specific, informed, unconditional, unambiguous. Identify every gap. Most firms will find that their engagement letter authorises data collection but does not specify purpose, retention period, or third-party sharing.

**Step 2 — Update your engagement letter and add a consent annexure.**
Revise the standard engagement letter to include a DPDP-compliant consent clause. Attach a one-page privacy notice as a standard annexure. Have a legal professional review the final version, particularly the consent language and the third-party disclosure section. This is a one-time investment that protects the firm for every future client onboarding.

**Step 3 — Designate a data compliance lead and run a staff briefing.**
Pick one person — a partner or a senior manager — to own DPDP compliance internally. Their job is not to build a compliance department. It is to maintain the consent log, respond to client data rights requests, and ensure the data handling protocols are followed day to day. Run a one-hour briefing with all staff and article clerks covering the three non-negotiables: no personal device storage, no unencrypted sharing, and every document collection needs prior consent.

The firms that get ahead of this now — before the enforcement machinery is fully operational — will avoid the scramble when the Data Protection Board begins processing complaints. More importantly, demonstrating structured data handling practices to clients is increasingly becoming a differentiator in a competitive advisory market.

Start with Step 1 today. The audit takes two hours and tells you everything you need to prioritise.
