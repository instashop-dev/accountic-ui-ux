Section 148 of the Income Tax Act, 1961 is probably the most high-stakes notice a CA handles in a year. The assessing officer claims reason to believe that income has escaped assessment. Your client wants a position by Friday. The demand could run into crores. And under the faceless regime, there is no sit-down meeting where you can talk the AO through your reasoning — the reply *is* the argument.

Every s. 148 reply has the same spine. The art is in the specificity.

## The five things every s. 148 reply must establish

1. **Jurisdiction is good (or it isn't).** Was the sanction under s. 151 obtained from the correct authority at the correct rank? Was the AO's recorded reason served with the notice? If either is missing, say so at paragraph 1 — not paragraph 12.
2. **The "reason to believe" is not a "reason to suspect".** The post-*Lakhmani Mewal Das* standard is still the one the ITAT applies. Reopening on borrowed satisfaction, or on information that doesn't rationally connect to escapement, fails.
3. **The amount alleged to have escaped is reconciled.** If the AO says ₹14.7 lakh, your reply should show, by document, where every rupee of that came from — sale of listed securities, already-filed return, reconciled bank entries.
4. **The limitation and procedural conditions are met on the Department's side.** Under the s. 148 / 148A post-Finance Act 2021 framework, timelines matter. Call them out.
5. **The prior position of the assessee is disclosed honestly.** Faceless assessment rewards consistency. Hiding a previously-taken position because it's now inconvenient is a worse outcome than addressing it head-on.

## Order of the arguments

In our own drafts at DPS &amp; Co., we've converged on a five-part structure that the AO can read in under six minutes:

> **1. Captioned-notice reference.** DIN, date, section, AY.
> **2. Preliminary objections without prejudice.** Jurisdiction, sanction, limitation.
> **3. Facts as they actually are.** Not "submitted that", but ledger references, bank statements, and reconciliations.
> **4. The law applied to those facts.** One IT Act section cited, one binding precedent, one circular. No more.
> **5. Prayer and a concrete evidence list.** What you want the AO to do, and what you've enclosed.

Point 4 is where most generic drafts collapse. A reply that cites five cases reads like a CA who is trying to impress. A reply that cites *one* case — the right one — reads like a CA who knows what they're doing.

## The citation that most replies get wrong

The most common failure mode we see, reviewing archived replies from the pre-Accountic workflow, is citation drift. A draft cites *"section 147A"* (doesn't exist), or *CIT v. Kelvinator of India* for a proposition Kelvinator didn't stand for, or a circular that's been superseded.

If you're going to cite one Supreme Court judgment on s. 148, it's still **[ITO v. Lakhmani Mewal Das (1976) 103 ITR 437 (SC)](/blog/lakhmani-mewal-das-s148)** — the "rational nexus" standard. It has survived every legislative overhaul since, including the s. 148A framework. If you're going to cite one circular, check the CBDT repository *today* — not what you cited six months ago.

## What to enclose

- Return of income filed u/s 139(1), with acknowledgement.
- Computation of total income.
- Bank statements for the relevant period, with the escapement-alleged entries tick-marked.
- Confirmations from parties, where applicable.
- Prior replies on the same matter, if any.
- A short index referencing the above by page number.

A well-indexed enclosure list makes the AO's job easier. An AO whose job has been made easier is more likely to accept the reply on merits.

## One last thing

The faceless regime's standard — *"application of mind"* — is not a slogan. It's a directive, and the ITAT has been willing to set aside assessment orders on it. The same standard applies to the *reply*. A boilerplate reply is a red flag to a decent AO. A reply that reads like you read the notice, thought about it, and answered the specific grounds is a reply that gets accepted.
