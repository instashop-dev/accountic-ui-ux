## Section 142(1) Notice: Respond in 7 Steps

A Section 142(1) notice lands in your client's inbox and the clock starts ticking. Miss the deadline or file a sloppy response and you are looking at a Best Judgement Assessment, a ₹10,000 penalty, or worse. This article gives you a clean, repeatable workflow so every 142(1) notice your practice handles is dealt with efficiently and without loose ends.

---

### What Is a Section 142(1) Notice?

The Assessing Officer issues a Section 142(1) notice under the Income Tax Act, 1961 either before or during assessment proceedings to compel a taxpayer to produce specific information. It is a tool the AO uses to build the factual record before passing an assessment order, and it demands a precise, documented response.

There are three distinct triggers for this notice. First, the AO can direct a taxpayer who has not filed a return to do so. Second, the AO can call for specific books of account, documents, or records. Third, the AO can require a written explanation on any matter that appears necessary for the assessment.

Do not confuse this with a scrutiny notice under Section 143(2) or a reopening notice under Section 148. Each carries its own compliance timeline, penalty regime, and procedural obligations — treating them interchangeably is a common and costly mistake.

---

### 142(1) vs 143(2) vs 148 Notices: Key Differences

Before you draft a single line of response, confirm which notice you are actually dealing with. The table below captures the distinctions that matter most in practice.

| Feature | Section 142(1) | Section 143(2) | Section 148 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Primary purpose | Demand return, documents, or written explanation | Initiate scrutiny assessment | Reopen a completed assessment |
| Stage of proceedings | Before or during regular assessment | After return is filed; within 3 months of end of FY in which return filed | After assessment is finalised; strict time limits apply |
| Who can be targeted | Any taxpayer, including non-filers | Only taxpayers who have filed a return | Taxpayers where income has escaped assessment |
| Response obligation | Produce documents or explanation within the stated deadline | Appear and / or file detailed submissions | File fresh return for the relevant AY |
| Penalty for non-compliance | ₹10,000 per default under Section 271(1)(b) | Adverse inference; possible ex-parte assessment | Return non-filing consequences plus reassessment |
| Prosecution risk | Section 276D for wilful non-compliance | Generally not triggered at this stage | Section 276C if concealment is involved |
| Can precede 143(3) order? | Yes — frequently a stepping stone to a scrutiny order | No — 143(2) itself opens scrutiny | No — 148 opens a separate reassessment proceeding |

One nuance worth flagging: in AY 2024-25 and AY 2025-26 assessments running under the Faceless Assessment Scheme, 142(1) notices are issued through the National Faceless Assessment Centre (NaFAC). The DIN on the notice is your first verification checkpoint — no DIN, no valid notice.

### 7-Step Workflow to Respond to a Section 142(1) Notice

This is the workflow I use in my own practice. Follow it in sequence and you will not miss anything material.

1. Log in to the income tax e-filing portal and download the notice from the **e-Proceedings** tab. Verify the Document Identification Number (DIN), the Assessment Year, the section cited (confirm it reads 142(1) and not 143(2) or 148), and the response deadline. A notice without a valid DIN is legally infirm — note this before doing anything else.

2. Read the notice carefully to identify the specific demand. The AO will typically fall into one of three categories: asking for a return to be filed, demanding specified books or documents, or seeking a written explanation on a particular issue. Your entire response strategy depends on which of these three it is.

3. Collate and reconcile the documents requested against the filed return. Cross-check figures — turnover, income heads, deductions — between the ITR, books of account, GST returns, Form 26AS, and AIS. Any variance you spot now is a variance the AO has also spotted; address it proactively in your covering letter rather than leaving it unexplained.

4. Prepare a structured written response. Start with a brief covering letter that acknowledges the notice, states the DIN and AY, and lists every document being submitted in a numbered index. If the notice asks for a written explanation, answer each point paragraph by paragraph, referencing the supporting document by its index number. Keep the language factual and professional — do not over-explain or raise issues the notice has not raised.

5. Upload the covering letter and all attachments through the **e-Proceedings** tab on the income tax portal. Confirm that each file is under the portal's size limit before you begin. Submit and do not close the browser until the portal generates a submission acknowledgement with a timestamp.

6. If you genuinely need more time — because documents are held up with a bank, registrar, or overseas counterparty — file a written request for extension before the original deadline lapses, not after. Upload the extension request through the same e-Proceedings workflow. An extension granted on record is infinitely better than a missed deadline with no communication.

7. Download and save the acknowledgement receipt immediately. Update your client's compliance register with the notice reference number, the AY, the date of response, and the documents submitted. Set a follow-up calendar reminder for 30 days to check whether the AO has raised any further queries or passed an order.

### Documents Commonly Demanded Under 142(1)

The AO's document list can look intimidating, but in practice most 142(1) notices draw from a predictable pool of records. Your primary financial documents will almost always include audited financial statements, the trial balance, bank statements for all accounts, account ledgers for key heads, and Form 16 / TDS certificates.

For transaction-specific demands — especially where the AO has spotted a specific mismatch — expect requests for sale deeds and registered agreements, capital gain computation worksheets, foreign remittance proofs (Form 15CA/15CB, FIRC), loan agreements, and related-party transaction schedules.

The most practical thing you can do for your practice is build a client-specific document checklist mapped to the most common 142(1) demand categories. When a notice arrives, you run the checklist and know within an hour what you have, what is missing, and what will take time to gather. That preparation collapses a three-day scramble into a morning's work.

---

### Penalty and Consequences for Non-Compliance

Ignoring a 142(1) notice is not a low-risk option. Section 271(1)(b) empowers the AO to levy a penalty of ₹10,000 for each distinct direction that is not complied with. If the notice contains multiple specific document demands and you ignore all of them, the penalties compound.

The more serious consequence is a Best Judgement Assessment under Section 144. When a taxpayer does not respond, the AO is permitted to assess income on the basis of whatever information is available — including third-party data from the Annual Information Statement and Form 26AS — without the benefit of any explanation from the taxpayer's side.

Wilful non-compliance can also attract prosecution under Section 276D, which can result in rigorous imprisonment of up to one year with a fine. This provision is not invoked lightly, but it exists and it applies — which is reason enough to ensure every 142(1) notice in your practice gets a timely, documented response.

**Client Scenario:**

A trading client in the textile business received a 142(1) notice for AY 2023-24 asking for reconciliation between gross turnover declared in the ITR (₹4.2 crore) and the aggregate turnover reported across GSTR-1 and GSTR-3B filings (₹4.6 crore). The gap was approximately ₹40 lakh and the AO wanted a written explanation with supporting data.

On reviewing the records, the difference was entirely attributable to two items: advance receipts that had been reported in GST returns in the prior financial year but recognised as revenue in the books only in AY 2023-24, and a credit note reversal that appeared in GSTR-3B but not in the ITR turnover figure. Both were legitimate timing differences with clear documentary backup.

The CA prepared a one-page reconciliation statement in tabular format, attached the relevant GSTR-1 and GSTR-3B summaries, a copy of the relevant credit note, and an advance receipt ledger. The covering letter explained each item by reference to the supporting document index. The response was uploaded through the e-Proceedings tab within the notice deadline. The AO accepted the explanation, no penalty was levied under 271(1)(b), and the assessment was completed under Section 143(3) without a Best Judgement order.

This outcome was not luck — it was the direct result of a structured, well-documented response filed on time.

Pull up your practice's pending matters list right now and identify every open 142(1) notice. For each one, confirm the response deadline, check whether the DIN has been verified, and assign a team member to begin the document collation using the checklist approach described above. If any deadline is within the next seven days, treat it as urgent and move it to the top of the queue today.
