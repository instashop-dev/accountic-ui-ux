## DPDP Act 2023: How CAs Should Handle Law Enforcement Data Disclosure Requests

The Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 is no longer on the horizon — it is here, and it changes the risk calculus every time a tax authority or GST officer turns up demanding your client's records. If you practice as a CA partner, you are simultaneously a data fiduciary under DPDP and a person legally bound to comply with summons under the Income Tax Act and CGST Act. Getting either side wrong carries serious consequences. This article gives you a practical, defensible workflow to navigate both obligations at once.

---

### Why CAs Are Caught Between Two Laws

CAs hold some of the most sensitive personal and financial data in the economy — bank statements, ITR filings, balance sheets, beneficial ownership details, transaction trails. Under the DPDP Act 2023, a firm that processes personal data of a large number of data principals across multiple clients is likely to qualify as a **Significant Data Fiduciary** once the central government notifies the threshold criteria. That status carries heightened obligations around data governance, security safeguards, and accountability.

At the same time, **Section 131 of the Income Tax Act** gives Assessing Officers and higher authorities the powers of a civil court to summon and examine any person — including your firm — and to compel production of books, documents, and records. **Section 91 of the CGST Act** gives GST officers similarly broad powers to demand records from any person. Neither provision is new; CAs have been navigating these summons for years.

What is new is the DPDP overlay. **Section 17(1)(b) of the DPDP Act 2023** carves out an exemption for processing personal data "in the interest of sovereignty and integrity of India or security of the State or maintenance of public order or prevention, detection, investigation or prosecution of any offence." This exemption is real, but it is not a blank cheque. The disclosure still needs to arise from a lawful state function, follow due process, and be proportionate. As the data fiduciary, it is your firm's job to verify that each of those boxes is actually ticked before you hand over anything.

---

### Anatomy of a Lawful Disclosure Request: What Qualifies

Not every communication from a government officer is a valid disclosure request under DPDP. Before your firm produces a single document, run the incoming request against the criteria below.

| Criterion | Compliant Request | Non-Compliant / Risky Request |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Issuing authority | Identified officer of a statutory body (CBDT, GSTIN, ED, CBI) with designation and office seal | Anonymous email, WhatsApp message, or informal phone call with no letterhead |
| Statutory basis cited | Specific section quoted (e.g., "u/s 131(1A) of the Income Tax Act, 1961") | Vague reference to "departmental inquiry" or "routine verification" |
| Data principal identified | Named taxpayer, PAN, GSTIN, or otherwise clearly identified individual or entity | Broad sweep such as "all clients from sector X" or "all files for FY 2019-24" |
| Purpose stated | Stated purpose links to a specific investigation or proceeding | No purpose stated, or purpose appears to be a fishing expedition |
| Proportionality | Requests only the data reasonably necessary for the stated purpose | Requests entire client database or years of records beyond the scope of the proceeding |
| Mode of service | Served in writing via post, process server, or authorised electronic mode | Verbal instruction or unofficial email from a personal address |

A broad or unfocused summons — for example, demanding "all client records maintained for FY 2019-24" without identifying a specific data principal — may well engage the proportionality standard implicit in DPDP. You are not obliged to comply blindly; you have both the right and the responsibility to seek clarification or challenge the scope through appropriate channels before disclosure.

### DPDP-Compliant SOP: Responding to a Disclosure Summons

The moment a summons or data request lands at your firm, a defined protocol must kick in. Ad hoc decisions made under time pressure are where DPDP liability is born.

1. **Log the request within two hours of receipt.** Record the date and time of receipt, the issuing authority's name and designation, the statutory provision cited, the data principal(s) named, and the exact scope of data demanded. Use your Disclosure Request Intake Form (see the documentation section below) and assign a unique reference number.

2. **Verify the authority's identity and jurisdiction before doing anything else.** Confirm the officer's posting via the CBDT/GSTN officer lookup or an official phone number sourced independently — not the number on the summons itself. A forged or spoofed summons is not a lawful request, and responding to one creates its own liability.

3. **Escalate to the senior partner and, where the request is unusually broad or the client is high-profile, to external counsel within 24 hours.** This is your internal legal review gate. No data should be pulled from client files until this gate is cleared. Document who was consulted and what decision was reached.

4. **Map the requested data against your client files and identify every personal data field in scope.** Then separate out fields that are not within the stated scope of the summons. Tax returns for an AY 2024-25 scrutiny matter do not automatically justify handing over bank account details for earlier years unless those are specifically demanded and justified.

5. **Redact or anonymise all personal data fields that fall outside the scope of the lawful request** before compiling the disclosure package. For example, if the summons names one director, the personal details of co-directors not named in the proceeding should be masked.

6. **Prepare a covering letter that references the statutory basis of your disclosure** and expressly states that it is made under compulsion of law. This letter protects you under DPDP by documenting that the disclosure was not voluntary and was limited to what was legally demanded.

7. **Hand over the disclosure package in a controlled manner** — ideally in person against acknowledgement, or via the designated portal if the authority provides one. Retain a complete copy of everything disclosed, including the covering letter and any acknowledgement received.

8. **Update your DPDP Incident Register immediately after disclosure.** Record the date of handover, the identity of the receiving officer, a description of the data disclosed, and the legal basis relied upon. Assess within 48 hours whether client notification is permissible or prohibited (see the next section).

### Notify or Stay Silent? Data Principal Rights Under DPDP

**Client Scenario:**

Your firm receives a Section 131(1A) summons from the Investigation Wing of the Income Tax Department, naming a high-net-worth individual client and demanding all investment-related records and bank statements for AY 2024-25 and AY 2025-26. The summons carries a confidentiality caveat that the inquiry is ongoing. You comply after internal review and controlled disclosure. Now the client — your data principal — calls asking why certain documents have gone missing from the shared folder.

Do you tell them what happened?

The DPDP Act 2023 does not impose an explicit obligation to notify the data principal when disclosure occurs under the Section 17(1)(b) lawful-state-function exemption. The Act is silent on this point, which means your fiduciary duty as a CA under the ICAI Code of Ethics becomes the governing standard. That duty of loyalty and transparency would ordinarily push you toward disclosure to the client.

However, **Section 138 of the Income Tax Act** restricts the disclosure of information obtained during tax proceedings, and active investigations under the PMLA and the Income Tax Act carry implicit (and sometimes explicit) tipping-off restrictions. If you notify the client and the client destroys evidence or flees the jurisdiction, your firm could face obstruction allegations. The legal risk runs both ways.

The defensible position is this: **document your decision and the reasoning behind it in writing, retain that note for at least three years, and if in any doubt, seek legal advice before notifying the client.** Your engagement letter's privacy notice clause (see below) should already have alerted the client to the possibility of statutory disclosures — which reduces the ethical imperative to provide case-specific notification and also insulates you from a client complaint that you acted without authority.

---

### Template Documentation Pack Every CA Firm Needs

Documentation is not a post-incident luxury — it is your first line of defence in both a DPDP audit and a departmental inquiry. Your firm needs the following instruments in place before the next summons arrives.

**1. Disclosure Request Intake Form.** A structured one-page form that captures: the name and designation of the issuing authority, the statutory provision cited, the date and mode of receipt, the data principal(s) identified, the exact scope of data demanded, and the name of the partner who received and logged the request. This form triggers the escalation clock.

**2. Data Disclosure Decision Log.** A running register, maintained per engagement or per client, that links each disclosure event to the legal basis relied upon, the name and designation of the approver, a description of the data disclosed versus data withheld, and the redaction actions taken. This log is what you produce if the Data Protection Board ever asks how your firm processed personal data under a state-function exemption.

**3. Client Privacy Notice clause in your engagement letter.** Every new engagement letter — and renewed letters for existing clients — must include a clause that discloses, in plain language, that the firm may be legally required to produce client records to statutory authorities and that such disclosure may occur without prior notice to the client where the law prohibits tipping-off. This pre-disclosure does not waive client rights; it simply ensures that your basis for compelled disclosure is transparent and documented from day one. Firms still using legacy engagement letters without DPDP-aligned privacy clauses are exposed.

A basic data processing agreement or data handling appendix, covering your firm's role as data fiduciary and the client's rights under the DPDP Act, is also worth considering for larger retainers and corporate clients who are themselves regulated entities.

### Penalties for Getting It Wrong on Either Side

The penalty exposure on both sides of this tension is significant. Optimising for one law while ignoring the other is not a viable strategy.

| Violation | Applicable Law | Maximum Penalty / Consequence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Unlawful or excessive disclosure of personal data without valid legal basis | DPDP Act 2023, Section 33 | Monetary penalty up to ₹250 crore; ICAI disciplinary proceedings for professional misconduct |
| Failure to maintain adequate security safeguards for personal data | DPDP Act 2023, Section 33 | Penalty up to ₹200 crore |
| Wilful non-compliance with a Section 131 summons | Income Tax Act, Section 276D | Prosecution carrying rigorous imprisonment up to one year, with or without fine |
| Contempt or obstruction in response to a Section 131 order | Income Tax Act, Section 131 read with contempt provisions | Civil contempt proceedings before the authority or superior court |
| Obstruction of a GST officer acting under Section 91 | CGST Act, Section 125 and Section 69 | Penalty up to ₹25,000; in cases of wilful evasion, arrest without bail under Section 69 |
| Tipping off a subject under investigation | PMLA 2002 / IT Act implied provisions | Prosecution for obstruction of justice; regulatory action by ICAI |

The ₹250 crore DPDP penalty is not a theoretical number. The Data Protection Board, once fully constituted and operational, will have adjudicatory powers and the authority to conduct inquiries on its own motion. Firms that cannot produce a disclosure decision log or demonstrate that they followed a documented protocol will find that "we always comply with government requests" is not a defence.

### Your Concrete Next Step

By the end of this week, pull out your standard engagement letter and check whether it contains a DPDP-compliant privacy notice clause covering statutory disclosures. If it does not, redraft that clause and have it reviewed by a lawyer familiar with both the DPDP Act and the ICAI Code of Ethics. Simultaneously, create your Disclosure Request Intake Form and designate the partner responsible for receiving and logging all future summons. These two actions cost you a few hours and position your firm to respond to the next Section 131 or Section 91 summons without the panic of working out the protocol in real time.
