A Section 143(2) notice lands in the portal and, within seconds, the clock starts running. In my experience across hundreds of scrutiny matters, the difference between a clean closure and a botched assessment almost always comes down to what the CA does in the first 48 hours — not the last 48. This workflow gives you a repeatable, day-by-day system to handle every 143(2) notice without dropping the ball on deadlines, documents, or client communication.

---

## What Section 143(2) Actually Triggers and Why Speed Matters

Section 143(2) is not a request for information — it is the Income Tax Department formally placing the assessee's return under scrutiny. Without a valid 143(2) notice, the AO has no jurisdiction to frame a scrutiny assessment under Section 143(3). That single legal fact makes the notice's validity, and your response to it, jurisdictionally critical.

The AO must issue the 143(2) notice **within 3 months from the end of the financial year in which the return was filed**. For a return filed in AY 2023-24 (FY 2022-23), the last date for issuing the notice is 30 June 2024. If the notice was issued even one day late, you have a jurisdictional defect — flag it immediately and preserve it in writing.

A common misconception: if you do not respond, the notice does not lapse. The AO can proceed **ex-parte under Section 144** and make best judgement additions. Those additions are far harder to dislodge at CIT(A) than a properly contested 143(3) order.

The most common CASS selection triggers you will encounter are:

- **High-value cash deposits or withdrawals** flagged in the AIS
- **Mismatch between income declared and Form 26AS/AIS credits**
- **Large property or share transactions** not reconciling with returned income
- **Foreign remittances** appearing in the AIS without corresponding income disclosure

Finally, determine immediately whether the notice is **limited scrutiny** (restricted to one or two specific issues as indicated in the notice text on ITBA) or **complete scrutiny** (entire return is open). Misreading this distinction wastes days of document preparation on issues the AO has not actually raised.

## Day 1 — Notice Triage: Read, Classify, and Brief the Client

The moment you receive the 143(2) notice — whether by portal intimation, email, or client forwarding — start the clock and do these steps in order.

**Step 1: Log the notice into your tracking system.** Record the DIN, the AO's name and ward/circle, the assessment year, the return's original filing date, the notice date, and the response deadline. Do this before reading the body of the notice. If it is not logged, it does not exist in your practice management system.

**Step 2: Classify the notice type.** Open the ITBA portal under the assessee's login, navigate to e-Proceedings, and read the notice text carefully. The department's standard language will specify whether it is limited scrutiny under CASS or a complete scrutiny case. Write this classification prominently in your matter file.

**Step 3: Extract every query as a numbered list.** Do not summarise. Reproduce the AO's exact language for each issue raised. Then share this numbered list with the client in writing — email or WhatsApp message, same day — along with a structured information request checklist. Do not wait for a meeting to send the checklist; meetings cause delays.

**Step 4: Flag any jurisdictional defects.** If the notice date appears to fall outside the statutory 3-month window, or the notice lacks a valid DIN (Document Identification Number as mandated by CBDT Circular No. 19/2019), document this fact in a contemporaneous note. You will need this preserved argument if the matter escalates.

## Day 2–3 — Document Excavation: The Master Checklist by Notice Type

Every scrutiny notice clusters around a handful of recurring triggers. Rather than building a document list from scratch each time, use this categorised checklist and adapt it to the specific notice in hand.

### Master Document Checklist by Scrutiny Trigger

| Scrutiny Trigger | Core Documents Required | Common Gaps to Watch |
|---|---|---|
| **Cash deposits / large withdrawals** | All bank statements (every account), source explanation letters, gift deeds, loan agreements with ITRs of lenders | Statements from dormant or minor accounts the client forgot to mention |
| **High-value property purchase** | Registered sale deed, stamp duty valuation certificate, source of funds trail, capital gains computation (if sale involved) | SRO valuation vs. returned consideration mismatch |
| **Business income mismatch** | Books of accounts, audited P&L and Balance Sheet, GSTR-1, GSTR-3B, TDS certificates, Form 26AS reconciliation | GST turnover vs. ITR turnover difference unexplained |
| **Foreign income or assets (Schedule FA/FSI)** | Form 67 (filed within due date), NRE/NRO bank statements, DTAA claim workings, foreign tax credit certificates | Form 67 filed late — this disqualifies the credit claim entirely |
| **Deduction claims (80C/80D/80G)** | Investment proof with UDIN where applicable, premium receipts, donation receipts with 80G registration validity | 80G registration lapsed; ELSS statement not matching deduction claimed |
| **Share transactions / capital gains** | Contract notes, demat account statement, cost of acquisition workings, grandfathering calculation (for pre-Jan 2018 listed shares) | ISIN-level reconciliation with AIS — AIS shows gross proceeds, not net gains |

On Day 2, before you touch any other document, **pull the AIS and Form 26AS for the relevant AY under the assessee's PAN**. Reconcile every line item against the filed return. Any unexplained credit in the AIS that does not appear in the return is the first place the AO will make an addition — you need to address it proactively, not reactively.

## Day 3–4 — Gap Analysis: Spotting What You Cannot Produce

By the end of Day 3, you should have a clear picture of what documents exist and what does not. Day 4 is decision day — any gap identified after Day 4 puts you in reactive mode.

Build a two-column tracker in a simple spreadsheet:

| Document Required | Status | Action / Owner | Due Date |
|---|---|---|---|
| SBI Bank Statement (FY 2022-23) | In hand | — | — |
| HDFC Bank Statement (FY 2022-23) | Missing | Client to request certified copy | Day 5 |
| Gift deed from father (₹15 lakh) | In hand | Scan and label as Exhibit-C | Day 4 |
| Father's ITR for AY 2023-24 | Missing | CA office to obtain from father's CA | Day 5 |

For missing bank statements, **initiate the request to the bank immediately** — most banks take 3–5 working days for certified copies, and that timeline does not compress simply because you asked politely. If original documents are genuinely unavailable (lost in a fire, business closure), prepare a **sworn affidavit** explaining the circumstances and offer secondary evidence such as passbook entries, ledger confirmations, or third-party receipts.

By Day 4, assess whether the gaps are significant enough to warrant an **adjournment application** (covered in detail later in this article). One well-reasoned adjournment is categorically better than a first reply that is factually incomplete — incomplete replies invite additions that are then much harder to reverse at the appellate stage.

If any issue in the notice involves a legal position that is genuinely contested — say, applicability of a DTAA provision, or the character of a receipt — flag it for a senior opinion before that position is committed to writing in your submission. Written submissions are admissions in the AO's hands.

## Day 4–5 — Drafting the Written Submission: Structure That AOs Accept

The written submission is the record. Everything the AO, CIT(A), and ITAT will read later flows from what you write now. Eloquence is irrelevant — structure and precision are everything.

### Proven Submission Structure

**Para 1 — Factual Background:** State the PAN, the assessment year, the date of original filing, the nature of income (salary, business, capital gains, etc.), and the fact that the return was filed voluntarily within the due date. This orients the AO and establishes that the return is not a belated or defective filing. Keep this to three or four sentences.

**Para 2 onwards — Query-by-Query Response:** Address each query raised in the notice in a separately numbered paragraph that **mirrors the AO's own numbering**. Never consolidate unrelated queries into a single answer. The structure within each query-paragraph should follow this sequence: legal position first → factual explanation second → supporting document referenced by exhibit number third.

For computation disputes, attach a **side-by-side reconciliation table** rather than a prose explanation. For example:

| Item | As per AIS / AO's Apparent Computation | As per Filed Return | Difference | Explanation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Gross rental receipts | ₹8,40,000 | ₹8,40,000 | Nil | — |
| Standard deduction (30%) | — | ₹2,52,000 | ₹2,52,000 | Section 24(a) — correctly claimed |
| Net income from house property | ₹8,40,000 | ₹5,88,000 | ₹2,52,000 | Standard deduction not reflected in AIS |

**Closing Prayer:** End with a formal prayer requesting acceptance of the return as filed. In the alternative, request a personal hearing under Section 142(1) read with Rule 14 if any oral clarification would assist the AO. This preserves your right to appear before the order is passed.

**Critical drafting caution:** Avoid phrases like "inadvertent error," "oversight," or "clerical mistake." These admissions are routinely used by AOs to justify penalty proceedings under Section 270A for under-reporting or misreporting of income. If an error was made, address it factually and quantify its correct treatment — do not characterise it.

## Day 5–6 — Compliance Filing on ITBA/e-Proceedings Portal

The response must be filed under the **assessee's login** on the Income Tax portal — not the CA's login, and not through the CA's portal access. This is non-negotiable. Responses filed through the wrong login are technically not on record.

### Step-by-Step Portal Submission

1. Log in at **incometax.gov.in** using the assessee's PAN and password.
2. Navigate to: **e-Proceedings → View Notices → Select the relevant DIN-linked notice → Respond.**
3. Verify the DIN on screen matches the DIN on the physical/downloaded notice. If there is a corrigendum or revised notice, respond to the latest one — but note both in your submission.
4. Prepare your PDF bundles in advance. Each file must be **below 50 MB**. For large document sets, compress using Smallpdf or Adobe Acrobat and split into logical bundles (e.g., Bundle A — Bank Statements, Bundle B — Books of Accounts).
5. Label every exhibit file clearly before uploading: `Exhibit-A_BankStatement_SBI_AY2324.pdf`, `Exhibit-B_GiftDeed_DatedDDMMYYYY.pdf`. AOs work with multiple files; a clearly named file is a courteous one.
6. Upload the main written submission PDF first, followed by the exhibits in numbered order.
7. Submit and **immediately download the system-generated acknowledgement** including the submission timestamp. Save this in both the client's digital folder and your firm's matter management system.

**If the portal is unresponsive near the deadline** (which happens with maddening regularity during peak assessment season), do two things simultaneously: send a physical copy by speed post to the AO's office address, and send an email to the jurisdictional AO with all documents attached. Print both the postal receipt and the email delivery confirmation. These constitute contemporaneous evidence of your intent to comply within the deadline — critical if the matter ever goes to CIT(A) on a preliminary objection.

## Day 7 — Post-Submission Checklist and Client Communication

Submitting the response is not the end of your work — it is the end of the first phase. Day 7 is about closing the immediate cycle cleanly and setting up for what comes next.

**Internal closure steps:**

- Update your notice register: submission date, portal acknowledgement number, exhibits submitted, next expected action (show-cause notice, hearing date, or 143(3) order).
- Save a complete **offline copy** of the submission package — written reply plus all exhibits — in a client-specific folder on your server. The Income Tax portal does not guarantee indefinite document access, and portal outages at the worst possible moment are an occupational hazard.
- Note the Section 143(3) order timeline: the AO generally has **9–12 months from the end of the assessment year** to pass the final order. For AY 2023-24, this typically means the order must be passed by 31 December 2024 or 31 March 2025 depending on whether the case was picked up under CASS or otherwise. Brief the client on this.

**Client communication:**

Send the client a structured email on Day 7 covering: what was submitted, which position was taken on each issue, what the likely next steps are, and what the client should and should not do. Specifically, advise the client in writing **not to make direct contact with the AO**. Uncoordinated client communications — phone calls, unscheduled visits, informal emails — frequently contradict carefully constructed written positions and give AOs additional material to work with.

If any position in the reply carries genuine litigation risk (for example, a valuation claim that the AO is likely to contest, or a foreign tax credit that depends on timely filing of Form 67), **document your professional advice in writing and obtain the client's sign-off**. This is not about protecting yourself against the client — it is a professional conduct requirement under the ICAI Code of Ethics, and it protects both of you if the matter escalates.

## Handling Adjournments: When and How to Ask Without Weakening Your Case

An adjournment is a legitimate procedural tool, not a sign of weakness — but only if used correctly and sparingly. Here is the tactical playbook.

**When to seek an adjournment:**
- Critical documents (certified bank statements, foreign tax certificates) are genuinely outstanding and will be available within a defined window.
- The client or a key witness is hospitalised and personal hearing attendance is required.
- A senior legal opinion on a contested issue is pending and cannot responsibly be skipped.

**How to file the adjournment request:**

1. File the request **at least 2–3 days before the response deadline** — last-minute requests, whether on the portal or by email, are routinely ignored or refused.
2. State a **specific, documentable reason** — not "documents are being collected" but "certified bank statement from HDFC Bank has been requested on [date] and the bank has confirmed a 5-working-day turnaround."
3. Ask for a **specific alternative date**, not an open-ended extension. AOs respond far better to: "We request that the response date be extended to 25 June 2025" than to "We request adequate time."
4. Get the acknowledgement of the adjournment request in writing — portal submission timestamp or email delivery receipt.

**What adjournments do not do:** They do not reset or extend the AO's scrutiny period. The 143(3) order deadline runs on its own clock regardless of how many adjournments are granted. Use the adjournment window productively — if documents are already in hand, begin drafting immediately rather than waiting for the new deadline.

## Building a Reusable 143(2) Response System for Assessment Season

If your firm handles more than 10–15 scrutiny notices per assessment season, an ad hoc approach will fail. You need a system.

### The Five Pillars of a Scalable 143(2) Practice

**1. Live Notice Register**

Maintain a shared spreadsheet (or notice management platform) updated every working day during assessment season. Minimum columns:

| Client Name | PAN | Notice Date | AY | Type (L/C) | Response Deadline | Status | Owner |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Mehta Textiles Pvt Ltd | AAACM1234F | 15-May-25 | 2023-24 | Complete | 14-Jun-25 | Drafting | Partner A |
| Rajan K (Individual) | BBBPR5678G | 20-May-25 | 2023-24 | Limited | 19-Jun-25 | Doc Collection | Article Clerk |

**2. Para-Level Response Template Library**

Build a library of draft paragraphs for the 15 most common scrutiny triggers — cash deposits, property purchases, ESOP taxation, rental income, capital gains, etc. Personalise these for each case; do not draft from scratch. A well-structured template cuts drafting time by 60–70% and maintains consistency across your team.

**3. Delegation Protocol**

Document collection (Day 2–3 checklist) is articled assistant work. Gap analysis (Day 3–4) is a senior associate responsibility. Drafting and review is CA partner work. Submission filing (Day 5–6) can be delegated to a trained senior article under partner supervision. Do not let partners spend time chasing bank statements — that is not a productive use of professional time.

**4. Automated Deadline Reminders**

Set calendar alerts at Day 1 (notice logged), Day 3 (document checklist returned by client), and Day 6 (submission due) for every active notice. Never rely on memory during peak assessment season when you may have 20 live notices running simultaneously.

**5. Post-Matter Debrief**

After each matter is closed, spend 15 minutes reviewing: Was any document needed that was not on your standard checklist? Did a new type of query appear that requires a template paragraph? Was any portal step confusing or time-consuming? Update your template library after every matter. Over two or three seasons, this builds an institutional knowledge base that makes your firm significantly faster and more accurate than competitors working from scratch each time.

## Common Mistakes CAs Make in 143(2) Responses (and How to Avoid Them)

After reviewing responses across dozens of scrutiny matters at the CIT(A) stage — where problems in the original reply become visible — these are the most consequential errors I see repeatedly.

### Mistake Catalogue and Prevention

| Mistake | Why It Damages You | Prevention |
|---|---|---|
| **Responding to the wrong DIN or AY** | Creates a jurisdictional gap — the AO's notice is technically unanswered | Cross-verify PAN, AY, and DIN against the portal before drafting a single line |
| **Submitting uncertified copies** | AOs increasingly reject these; leads to show-cause for non-compliance | All copies must be self-attested by the assessee; where UDIN is relevant (audit documents), attach the CA's UDIN |
| **Volunteering information not asked for** | Opens new issues the AO had not flagged — the equivalent of handing the AO a new map | Answer only what is asked; if context is essential, keep it minimal and directly relevant |
| **Mixing legal and factual arguments without structure** | The AO cannot follow your reply; both arguments lose credibility | Use the lead-legal, follow-factual, cite-document structure for every query paragraph |
| **Not saving the submission acknowledgement** | You cannot prove timely compliance if the matter escalates to CIT(A) or ITAT | Download and save the acknowledgement the moment you submit; back it up offline |
| **Client making unsupervised contact with the AO** | Uncoordinated verbal statements contradict written submissions and create new concessions | Advise this in writing on Day 1 and again on Day 7; make it a standard client engagement protocol |

The first and last mistakes on this list are the most common in practice and the most avoidable. Both require a process fix, not additional knowledge — a checklist prevents wrong DINs, and a client briefing protocol prevents uncoordinated communications.

## Your Next Step: Build the System Before the Next Notice Arrives

A 143(2) notice is not an emergency if you have a workflow. It becomes one only when you are building the system while the clock is already running.

This week, before the next notice arrives, do three things:

1. **Create your notice register** — even a simple spreadsheet — and commit to updating it every working day during assessment season.
2. **Draft template paragraphs** for the five scrutiny triggers most common in your client base and store them in a shared folder your team can access.
3. **Set up a Day 1 / Day 3 / Day 6 reminder template** in your calendar system so every new notice gets three automatic alerts the moment it is logged.
