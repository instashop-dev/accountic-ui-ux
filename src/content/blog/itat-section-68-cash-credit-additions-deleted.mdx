# ITAT Case Law: Section 68 Cash Credit Additions Deleted

Section 68 additions remain one of the most contested battlegrounds in scrutiny assessments. If you are defending a client where the Assessing Officer has made a cash credit addition, you need more than a textbook explanation of the provision — you need battle-tested ITAT precedents, a disciplined documentation strategy, and a clear understanding of where AOs regularly overreach. This article gives you exactly that.

---

## What Is Section 68 and Why AOs Invoke It

Section 68 of the Income Tax Act, 1961 allows an AO to treat any sum found credited in a taxpayer's books as income for that year if the taxpayer fails to explain the nature and source of that credit satisfactorily. The burden operates across three limbs — identity of the creditor, creditworthiness of the creditor, and genuineness of the transaction — and the assessee must discharge all three.

Post-demonetisation, scrutiny assessments for AY 2017-18 and AY 2018-19 saw a dramatic surge in Section 68 additions, particularly for cash deposits made between 9 November and 30 December 2016. AOs often treated every unexplained deposit as an automatic Section 68 trigger, sometimes without conducting any independent inquiry of their own.

The most persistent criticism from ITAT across multiple benches is that AOs shift the entire burden onto the assessee and then make additions the moment the creditor does not respond to a notice — without ever issuing a summons under Section 131 or making any effort to trace the creditor independently. This approach has been repeatedly disapproved, and understanding precisely where AOs go wrong is your first advantage at the appellate stage.

---

## Landmark ITAT Rulings Where Section 68 Additions Were Deleted

The Supreme Court's ruling in **CIT v. Lovely Exports (P) Ltd. (2008) 216 CTR 195 (SC)** remains the foundational precedent on share application money. The Court held that once an assessee discloses the identity of shareholders, including their PAN and address details, the burden shifts back to the Revenue to investigate those shareholders — the AO cannot simply sit on that information and sustain an addition.

**ITAT Mumbai in Smt. Parveen Arora** accepted bank account deposits as explained where the assessee demonstrated a clear prior cash withdrawal trail. The tribunal held that a nexus between earlier withdrawals and subsequent deposits, supported by bank statements, constitutes a satisfactory explanation under Section 68 — the AO cannot demand more without pointing to specific defects in the chain.

**ITAT Delhi in Agson Global Pvt. Ltd.** is essential citation material for any case where the AO relies on a "suspicious entry provider" list or relies on a statement recorded from a third party. The tribunal held that mere listing of an entity as an accommodation entry provider, without concrete corroborating evidence linking that specific transaction to bogus credit, is insufficient to sustain the addition.

**ITAT Kolkata** has produced a useful line of post-demonetisation orders accepting household savings and agricultural income as valid sources of cash deposits, provided the assessee demonstrates consistent past handling of cash, produces prior years' returns, and shows that the quantum is reasonable relative to historical income patterns. These orders are particularly useful for individual and HUF clients where formal lending records are absent.

## Assessee's Three-Limb Discharge of Burden: Step-by-Step

Once an AO makes a Section 68 addition, your response — whether at assessment, CIT(A), or ITAT — must systematically address each of the three limbs. Here is the exact workflow to build that response:

1. Compile identity documents for every creditor or investor: PAN card, KYC documents, current address proof, and a signed confirmation letter on the creditor's letterhead confirming the transaction date, amount, and mode of payment.

2. Establish creditworthiness by obtaining the creditor's ITRs for at least two preceding years, their audited or unaudited balance sheet as at the date of the transaction, and bank statements covering the period of the transaction — together these demonstrate the creditor had the financial capacity to lend or invest the amount in question.

3. Prove genuineness by showing that all funds moved through banking channels: account-payee cheques, RTGS or NEFT confirmation receipts, and where the credit relates to share application money, board resolutions authorising the allotment and Form PAS-3 filed with the Registrar of Companies.

4. Document the AO's failure to follow up: if you filed all the above documents during assessment and the AO still made the addition without issuing summons under Section 131 to the creditors, put this on record explicitly — note the dates of your submissions, the documents submitted, and the absence of any summons or further inquiry by the AO.

5. Build the ITAT paper book in advance: compile all documents in a single indexed paper book with tabbed sections corresponding to each limb, cross-referenced to the specific paragraphs in the AO's order and to the ITAT rulings you are relying on, so the bench can verify each factual assertion without needing to ask for documents during the hearing.

The discipline of this workflow matters because an ITAT bench will look at whether your client discharged the burden at the first opportunity — before the AO. If documents were filed at assessment stage and the AO ignored them, that itself becomes a strong argument for deletion.

## AO Actions vs ITAT Findings: Common Grounds Compared

The table below captures the most recurring conflicts between AO reasoning and ITAT findings in Section 68 deletion orders. Keep this handy when drafting your grounds of appeal.

| AO Action / Reasoning | ITAT Counter-Finding | Key Takeaway for Your Appeal |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Made addition solely because creditor did not respond to notice under Section 133(6); no summons issued under Section 131 | Once identity is disclosed by assessee, Revenue must independently pursue creditor; non-response to 133(6) cannot alone sustain addition | Document date of identity disclosure vs date of addition — show AO skipped Section 131 |
| Treated cash deposits as unexplained income without examining prior withdrawal history | Bank statements showing prior cash withdrawals of matching amounts accepted as sufficient nexus; addition deleted | Produce month-wise cash flow statement linking withdrawals to deposits with dates and amounts |
| Relied on third-party statement naming entity as bogus entry provider without confronting the assessee with that statement | Statement not put to assessee violates natural justice; addition not sustainable without cross-examination opportunity | File specific ground on non-confrontation; cite ITAT Delhi in Agson Global and relevant High Court orders on natural justice |
| Rejected ITR and balance sheet of creditor as insufficient without identifying any specific deficiency | ITR, balance sheet, and bank statement of creditor constitute prima facie creditworthiness; AO must identify specific defect to reject | Ensure creditor documents are filed at assessment stage so AO's silence on defects is documented |
| Invoked Section 68 for share application money despite PAN and address of all applicants on record | Lovely Exports (SC) applies; burden shifts to Revenue once identity established; addition deleted | Attach Lovely Exports citation as Annexure A in every share application money case |

## Demonetisation-Specific ITAT Precedents (AY 2016-17 and 2017-18)

**Case Scenario:**

Your client is a kirana trader in Jaipur. For AY 2017-18, the AO made a Section 68 addition of ₹18 lakhs on account of cash deposited in November 2016 during the demonetisation window. The AO's reasoning: the client could not produce any formal lending document or third-party confirmation, and the AO treated the entire deposit as unexplained income.

In comparable facts, **ITAT Jaipur** and **ITAT Chennai** have both held that cash-in-hand reflected in books of account as at 8 November 2016 — corroborated by sales data, cash book entries, and stock registers — constitutes a valid and sufficient source for subsequent cash deposits. The tribunal noted that demanding formal documentation from a small trader for self-generated business cash is inconsistent with the realities of cash-based trade.

For agricultural clients, ITAT orders from multiple benches have upheld seasonal agricultural income as a valid source where the assessee produces land records (7/12 extract or equivalent), past crop sale receipts, and demonstrates a pattern of cash handling consistent with seasonal harvests. Consistency across earlier years is key — if your client deposited similar amounts in November in prior years, that historical pattern is your strongest argument.

**CBDT Circular No. 3/2017** set parameters for scrutiny of demonetisation cash deposits — deposits above ₹2 lakhs for non-business taxpayers or above a percentage of turnover for business taxpayers. ITAT benches have used these thresholds as a reasonableness test: where the deposit is within the range of past turnover or income, several benches have held that the AO must point to something more concrete before invoking Section 68. Always check whether your client's deposit falls within the CBDT's own reasonableness band and use that in your written submissions.

Note that ongoing scrutiny assessments for AY 2024-25 and AY 2025-26 are increasingly seeing Section 68 additions for online credit entries and digital wallet receipts — the same three-limb framework applies, but the documentation pack will differ. Bank statements and payment gateway reports now substitute for cash book entries.

## How CAs Should Use These Precedents in CIT(A) and ITAT Appeals

Start with jurisdictional ITAT bench orders. A Hyderabad bench order carries more immediate persuasive weight before the Hyderabad ITAT than a Delhi bench order, even if both delete identical additions. Build your citation list in this hierarchy: same bench, coordinate bench, High Court of jurisdiction, Supreme Court.

When Revenue counsel cites an adverse ruling — and they will — do not ignore it. Prepare a short fact-distinguished note (no more than two pages) that identifies exactly which facts in the adverse ruling differ from your client's facts. Benches respect counsel who engage with adverse precedents honestly rather than trying to sweep them aside.

Your paper book should have four tabbed sections: (1) statutory provisions — Section 68 text and relevant CBDT circulars, (2) case law compilation — each ITAT or court order cited, with the relevant paragraphs highlighted, (3) documentary evidence — indexed documents proving identity, creditworthiness, and genuineness, and (4) AO order extracts — specific paragraphs from the assessment order that you are challenging, with your counter-argument noted in the margin. A well-organised paper book signals preparedness and routinely influences how a bench receives your submissions.

Having three or four directly comparable fact-pattern orders from your own bench is far more effective than a generic list of twenty citations from across India.

## Your Concrete Next Step

Pull up your current Section 68 scrutiny files — both those pending at CIT(A) and any fresh AY 2024-25 or AY 2025-26 assessments where an addition has been made. For each file, check whether your client's documentation covers all three limbs and whether you have recorded the AO's failure to issue Section 131 summons. If either is missing, address it before the next hearing date. A well-documented Section 68 response filed early — at assessment stage rather than only at appeal — dramatically improves your deletion odds at every subsequent appellate level.
