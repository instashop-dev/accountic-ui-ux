Unlike a s. 148 notice, a s. 143(2) notice doesn't come with the presumption that the Department has already formed a view. It's an invitation to a scrutiny proceeding. That changes the tone of the reply — less defensive, more expository. More "here is what happened," less "here is why you are wrong."

That shift in tone is what most drafts get wrong.

## What a 143(2) notice actually wants

The CASS-selected 143(2) notice is usually triggered by a mismatch: Form 26AS vs. return, GSTR-3B vs. books, or a deposit vs. disclosed income. The AO, at this stage, is asking you to reconcile.

So the first job of the reply is to *reconcile*, visibly. Not to argue.

A good s. 143(2) reply looks like this:

> **Reconciliation Statement — AY 2024–25**
> Turnover as per audited financial statements: ₹9,86,47,120
> Turnover as per GSTR-3B for the year: ₹10,05,11,200
> Difference: ₹18,64,080
> Comprising:
>  — Inter-state stock transfers (Annexure 3): ₹12,00,000
>  — Credit notes issued post year-end, booked in next FY: ₹6,64,080

Three lines of arithmetic beat three pages of prose. Every single time.

## Structure that has worked for us

1. **Acknowledgement of notice.** DIN, date, AY. Reference the specific issue flagged, not "your notice."
2. **Short factual narrative.** Two paragraphs max. What the assessee does, revenue base, relevant year.
3. **Point-by-point reconciliation.** Every flagged mismatch, with the number, the source, and the reconciliation.
4. **Supporting documents list.** Numbered, cross-referenced in the reconciliation.
5. **Prayer.** Request that the proceedings be dropped / the return be accepted / a specific modification be made.

Skip the "Whereas" language unless the notice has specifically framed a legal question. Scrutiny is conversational. Write conversationally.

## The citations that *shouldn't* be in a 143(2) reply

A reply to a routine 143(2) should not cite *CIT v. Kelvinator*. It should not cite Supreme Court judgments. It should not refer to "binding precedents." Doing so signals that you think this is a s. 148 fight when it isn't — and signals to the AO that you're already preparing for appeal.

Save your case law for the grounds of appeal. Use your reply for the reconciliation.

## When it *does* escalate

If the AO's next notice, u/s 142(1) or u/s 143(3) show-cause, rejects the reconciliation or adds a new ground, *then* the reply tone shifts. That second reply is where the legal argument belongs. Clear separation between the two registers is what the ITAT rewards on appeal.

## One habit worth copying

Before filing, open the notice and the reply side-by-side. Read ground 1 of the notice. Read your response to ground 1. Does it answer the actual question?

Then ground 2. Then ground 3. Until every ground has a direct, specific response.

That's the only discipline required. Most drafts that fail on appeal fail because one ground was addressed in a paragraph that discussed three others.
