After fifteen years of dealing with jurisdictional Assessing Officers, the shift to faceless assessment felt seismic for most practitioners. The informal back-channel, the personal hearing in the AO's chamber, the ability to gauge the officer's mood — all of it gone. What replaced it is a structured, digitally auditable process under Section 144B that demands a different kind of discipline from CAs. If you are still treating NFAC notices the way you treated old-style scrutiny letters, you are already behind.

This guide walks you through the entire Section 144B lifecycle — how the machinery works, what each notice means, where the deadlines sit, and exactly what you should do the moment a faceless assessment notice hits your client's portal.

---

## What Is Faceless Assessment and Why Section 144B Matters

Section 144B was inserted by the Finance Act 2021 as the statutory backbone of the Faceless Assessment Scheme, replacing the earlier administrative scheme with legislative teeth. Before this, the scheme existed through executive notifications — legally vulnerable to challenge. Section 144B made it statute.

The core idea is jurisdictional anonymity. The case is no longer owned by the AO in your client's city. It is routed through the National Faceless Assessment Centre in Delhi, handled by units whose identities are not disclosed to the taxpayer or CA. This eliminates the discretion — and the corruption — that came with face-to-face scrutiny.

The section covers two types of assessments: scrutiny assessments under Section 143(3) and best judgment assessments under Section 144. Both now travel through the NFAC machinery unless specifically exempted (search cases, for instance, remain outside the faceless framework). Every assessment your firm handles for AY 2021-22 onwards is almost certainly a Section 144B matter.

## Architecture of the NFAC: Who Does What

One of the most common mistakes CAs make is treating NFAC as a monolith. It is not. There are five distinct functional units, and understanding which one is acting at any given stage helps you calibrate your response.

| Unit | Role | When You Encounter It |
|---|---|---|
| **NFAC (Delhi)** | Single communication gateway — all notices and responses flow through it | Every stage; your only interface |
| **Assessment Unit (AU)** | Conducts scrutiny, raises queries, drafts assessment order | Throughout scrutiny; author of the draft addition proposal |
| **Verification Unit (VU)** | Independently verifies facts, documents, third-party data, field inquiries | Behind the scenes; triggers additional queries to AU |
| **Technical Unit (TU)** | Specialist input on valuation, transfer pricing, forensic accounting | Cases with complex valuations, international transactions |
| **Review Unit (RU)** | Reviews AU draft order before finalisation — a compulsory internal check | Final stage before the order is issued |

The critical operational point: **you never communicate directly with any unit other than through the NFAC portal**. If someone claims to be an Assessment Unit officer calling you for information, treat it as a red flag. The entire design of Section 144B is to prevent such contact.

## Step-by-Step Walkthrough of the Section 144B Process

Here is the full lifecycle mapped chronologically. Print this out and keep it on your desk.

**Stage 1 — Case Selection**
The case enters the system via CASS (Computer Assisted Scrutiny Selection) or specific criteria flagged by the department. The taxpayer receives an intimation under Section 143(2) — this is the trigger notice that starts the clock on the assessment timeline.

**Stage 2 — Section 142(1) Notice**
The NFAC issues a notice under Section 142(1) with a detailed questionnaire or list of documents. This is the AU's first real communication with you. Read it carefully — every query has a purpose, and your response scope should match it exactly.

**Stage 3 — Response Upload**
You upload the client's response and supporting documents on the Income Tax portal within the prescribed time. This is not email. This is not a physical submission. Portal-only, and the submission timestamp matters.

**Stage 4 — Show Cause Notice (SCN) / Draft Assessment Proposal**
If the AU proposes additions after reviewing your response, it issues a Show Cause Notice with the draft additions and the reasoning. This is the most consequential document in the entire process — it tells you exactly what the department intends to add and on what legal basis.

**Stage 5 — Written Response and Video Conferencing**
You file a written response to the SCN. If you want a personal hearing, you must request a Video Conferencing session within this same response window. The NFAC schedules it. This is your final substantive intervention before the order is drafted.

**Stage 6 — Review Unit Examination**
The Review Unit examines the AU's draft order. It can approve it or send it back for revision. You have no visibility into this stage, but it exists as a quality control layer. Orders that are procedurally defective at this stage are caught internally.

**Stage 7 — Final Order and Demand Notice**
The final assessment order and demand notice are issued electronically by NFAC and served on the registered email and portal account. From this point, the 30-day payment clock starts, and your appeal window opens.

**Typical total timeline:** 6 to 18 months from the Section 143(2) notice to the final order, depending on complexity and the department's workload.

## Key Notices a CA Will Encounter and How to Read Each One

Each notice in the Section 144B process serves a specific legal function. Treat each one as a legal document, not administrative correspondence.

**Section 143(2) Notice**
Check three things immediately: the Document Identification Number (DIN), the date of issue, and whether it was served within three months from the end of the financial year in which the return was filed. A notice served beyond this limitation period is invalid. Also check the PAN and Assessment Year — basic errors occur and must be caught early.

**Section 142(1) Notice**
Read the specific schedule of documents and questions. Assess whether the queries are within the scope of the return filed and the issues flagged. You are not obliged to respond to queries that go beyond the lawful scope of scrutiny. Responding to everything without reading the scope carefully often opens more issues than it closes.

**Show Cause Notice (Draft Additions)**
This is where the real work begins. Scrutinise the proposed additions, the sections under which they are proposed, and the AO's reasoning. Look for factual errors, wrong section application, and missing consideration of documents already submitted. The SCN response is your last real intervention point — an inadequate response here will cost your client at the appellate stage.

**Final Assessment Order**
Verify the demand computation line by line. Check interest levied under Sections 234A, 234B, and 234C — computational errors here are common. Check whether any penalty proceedings have been initiated, as these carry separate timelines under Section 274.

**One non-negotiable rule:** every communication from the department must carry a DIN. Under CBDT Circular 19/2019, any notice or order without a DIN is void. Check this on every document before doing anything else.

## Statutory Timelines and Limitation Periods CAs Must Track

Missing a deadline in a faceless assessment is far more damaging than in the old system. There is no AO you can call to informally extend time. Build these dates into your practice management system the moment a notice arrives.

| Event | Statutory Deadline | Consequence of Breach |
|---|---|---|
| Section 143(2) notice issuance | Within 3 months from end of FY in which return is filed | Notice (and assessment) invalid |
| Assessment completion | 9 months from end of FY in which 143(2) notice is issued (AY 2022-23 onwards) | Assessment barred by limitation |
| Response to SCN on draft additions | Typically 15–30 days as specified in notice | No further opportunity; additions confirmed |
| VC hearing request | Within the SCN response window | No post-deadline requests accepted |
| Extension request | At least 5 days before deadline | Portal extensions not guaranteed if filed late |
| Penalty notice under Section 274 | Separate timeline — do not conflate with assessment | Missed penalty response creates separate liability |

**Section 144B(9) is your procedural weapon.** Any assessment made in violation of the procedure prescribed under Section 144B is void ab initio. Track every procedural step the department takes. If the draft order was not reviewed by a Review Unit, if the SCN was not served, if the VC hearing was denied after being requested — all of these are grounds to challenge the order entirely, not just the quantum.

## Mandatory Personal Hearing via Video Conferencing: Rights and Procedure

Section 144B(6)(viii) provides an explicit statutory right to a video conferencing hearing if the taxpayer requests one. This is not discretionary. If the NFAC denies a properly made request for a VC hearing, the resulting order is legally challengeable on that ground alone.

**How to exercise the right correctly:**
Make the request in writing through the portal at the time of responding to the SCN or Section 142(1) notice. Do not make it verbally, do not send an email outside the portal, and do not make it after the response deadline has passed.

**How to prepare for the session:**
VC hearings are brief — typically 20 to 30 minutes. The assessor on the other end is handling multiple cases. Your verbal presentation must be crisp, focused on the two or three core issues that will move the needle, and backed by documents already uploaded. Prepare a structured written submission and upload it to the portal before the VC date. The written record matters more than what you say on the call.

**After the session:**
Upload a written summary of the arguments made during the VC within 24 to 48 hours. This creates an auditable paper trail. Record the VC session reference number, the date, and the approximate duration — you will need these details if you challenge the assessment order at the CIT(A) or ITAT level.

## Practical CA Checklist: Responding to a Faceless Assessment Notice

Use this checklist from the moment a client forwards a faceless assessment notice to your team.

### First 24 Hours

- [ ] Log into the IT portal under the client's credentials (or your authorised representative login)
- [ ] Download the notice and check the DIN — if absent, the notice may be void
- [ ] Verify the service date, assessment year, and PAN on the notice
- [ ] Check whether the Section 143(2) notice was issued within the statutory 3-month window
- [ ] Note the response deadline and immediately calendar it with a 5-day buffer reminder

### First 48 Hours

- [ ] Brief the client on the nature of the notice and likely timeline
- [ ] Obtain or refresh the Power of Attorney / authorisation if not current
- [ ] Register CA credentials as authorised representative on the portal if not already done
- [ ] Pull the original ITR, computation, and all schedules relevant to the queries raised

### Preparing the Response

- [ ] Map each query to the specific document or explanation that addresses it — nothing more, nothing less
- [ ] Prepare an indexed, bookmarked PDF submission; label each exhibit clearly (e.g., "Exhibit A — Bank Statement for FY 2022-23")
- [ ] Draft a covering letter that cross-references each document to each query point
- [ ] Avoid volunteering information not asked for — unnecessary disclosures expand the scope of scrutiny

### Submission and Follow-Up

- [ ] Upload documents in permitted formats (PDF/Excel); test the upload function at least 24 hours before deadline
- [ ] If seeking an extension, file the portal request at least 5 days before the deadline with a clear documented reason
- [ ] Download and retain the submission acknowledgement number immediately after upload
- [ ] If the notice is an SCN, decide whether to request a VC hearing and include that request in the same response filing

## A Quick Example: How This Plays Out in Practice

**Scenario:** Kanika Exports Pvt. Ltd. (PAN: AABCK1234P) files its ITR for AY 2023-24 in October 2023. In January 2024, it receives a Section 143(2) notice via NFAC — well within the 3-month window. A Section 142(1) notice follows in March 2024, asking for reconciliation of turnover between the ITR and Form 26AS, and explanation of a Rs. 45 lakh credit entry.

The CA downloads both notices, verifies DINs, and maps the queries to bank statements, sale invoices, and GSTR-1 data. The response is uploaded in indexed PDF format within the 30-day window. In June 2024, the AU issues an SCN proposing a Rs. 45 lakh addition under Section 68 (unexplained cash credit), stating the explanation was insufficient.

The CA immediately requests a VC hearing in the SCN response, uploads a detailed written submission with confirmation of the party's identity, ITR copy, and banking trail, and appears for the VC in July 2024. The written arguments are re-uploaded the same day. The Review Unit approves a revised draft removing the addition. Final order is issued with no addition — timeline: approximately 9 months from the first notice.

This outcome was possible because every deadline was met, every document was labelled, and the VC hearing right was exercised correctly. None of it required a phone call to any officer.

## Your Next Step

If your firm does not already have a standardised Notice Management Register for tracking NFAC notices across all clients — with DIN verification, response deadlines, extension logs, and submission acknowledgement numbers — build one this week. A shared spreadsheet is the minimum viable tool; a proper practice management system is better.

The faceless system does not forgive procedural sloppiness. But it also cannot ignore a well-documented, properly submitted response. Your edge as a CA in this environment is not relationships — it is process discipline and documentation quality. Build those habits into your team's workflow now, before the next notice arrives.
