## AI for Income Tax Act Research: Build a Custom GPT for Indian CAs

If you have spent ten minutes asking ChatGPT a question about Section 56(2)(x) and received a confident answer citing the Internal Revenue Code, you already know the problem. Generic AI tools are not built for Indian tax practice. Building a custom GPT trained on authoritative Indian tax documents changes that equation entirely, and it is well within reach for any CA firm that is willing to spend two focused hours on the setup.

---

## Why Generic AI Tools Fail Indian Tax Practitioners

Out-of-the-box large language models — ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot — are trained on the entire internet. That means they have absorbed the US Internal Revenue Code, HMRC guidance, Australian GST rules, and Indian tax law all in the same undifferentiated pile. When you ask about "withholding tax on professional fees," the model may blend Section 194J with IRS Publication 15 logic and return something that sounds plausible but is jurisdictionally contaminated.

The second problem is staleness. Most base training data cuts off before late 2021, which means CBDT circulars issued after the Finance Act 2021, the AIS framework notifications, and amendments introduced by the Finance Acts of 2022, 2023, and 2024 are simply absent from the model's memory. For AY 2024-25 and AY 2025-26 compliance queries, this gap is not a minor inconvenience — it is a liability.

Professional liability is the third problem, and it is the one your PI insurance underwriter cares about. If a CA relies on an AI hallucination and gives a client wrong advice on, say, the Section 43B(h) MSME payment disallowance applicable from AY 2024-25, the professional negligence exposure is real. The CA signed the advice. The model did not.

---

## What a Custom GPT for Indian Tax Research Actually Is

A Custom GPT is not a new AI model that you train from scratch. Think of it as a specialised operating skin placed over an existing large language model. You write instructions that tell the model what it is, what jurisdiction it must stay inside, and what tone and format to use. Then you upload knowledge files — PDFs, text documents — that the model can search against when answering your questions.

The underlying mechanism that makes this work is called Retrieval-Augmented Generation, or RAG. When you ask a question, the system first searches your uploaded documents, retrieves the most relevant passages, and then feeds those passages to the language model as context before it generates an answer. The model is no longer guessing from its training data; it is reading your documents first and then responding.

Fine-tuning, which is the other term you will hear, is a different and more expensive process where you actually retrain the model's weights on new data. For most CA firms, fine-tuning is overkill and impractical. A well-configured Custom GPT with good knowledge files and a tight system prompt delivers 90 percent of the accuracy benefit at a fraction of the effort.

The system prompt — the set of written instructions the model receives before every conversation — is where you lock jurisdiction. A single line like "You are an Indian tax research assistant. You must only cite provisions of the Income Tax Act 1961, CBDT circulars, ITAT and High Court judgments from Indian jurisdictions, and no other tax law" meaningfully reduces contamination from foreign tax law references.

## Source Documents to Include in Your Custom GPT Knowledge Base

The quality of your custom GPT is exactly as good as the documents you feed it. Upload thin or outdated source material and you will get thin, outdated answers. Use this checklist as your minimum viable knowledge base:

| Document Category | Recommended Source | Update Frequency |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Income Tax Act 1961 (consolidated, latest Finance Act amendments) | income-tax.gov.in official PDF | After each Finance Act assent |
| CBDT Circulars and Notifications (last 5 years) | incometaxindia.gov.in Circulars section | Monthly check |
| CBDT Press Releases and FAQs | PIB / CBDT official releases | As issued |
| ITAT landmark judgments (selected) | itatonline.org, indiankanoon.org | Quarterly |
| High Court judgments on common advisory issues | High Court portals / tax law databases | Quarterly |
| ITR form instructions and schema (ITR-1 to ITR-6) | incometaxindia.gov.in Downloads | Annual (post-Budget) |
| AIS / TIS field definitions and FAQs | Compliance Portal / CBDT guidance | As updated |
| Finance Act text (2021 to current) | e-gazette.nic.in | Annual |

A few practical notes. Download the Income Tax Act as a single consolidated PDF rather than chapter-by-chapter files — this preserves cross-reference integrity. When uploading CBDT circulars, prefer the official PDF over third-party summaries, because the model will cite the document you give it, and you want citations that hold up under scrutiny.

## Step-by-Step: Building Your Custom GPT on ChatGPT (OpenAI)

You will need a ChatGPT Plus or ChatGPT Team subscription to access the GPT Builder. The Team plan is preferable for firms because it allows you to share the GPT internally without exposing it to the public ChatGPT store.

1. Log in to chatgpt.com and navigate to the top-left menu. Click "Explore GPTs" and then "Create" to open the GPT Builder interface.
2. Switch from the "Create" tab to the "Configure" tab immediately. The Create tab uses a conversational setup wizard which is convenient but gives you less control over the system prompt.
3. Write your system prompt in the Instructions field. Start with a jurisdiction lock statement, then specify the output format you want (e.g., "Always cite the section number, sub-section, and proviso text verbatim before providing your analysis"), and add a refusal instruction ("If the answer requires information not found in the uploaded knowledge files, say so explicitly rather than speculating").
4. Upload your knowledge files under the Knowledge section. OpenAI currently allows up to 20 files per custom GPT with a total cap of 512 MB. Prioritise the Income Tax Act, Finance Act amendments, and the last three years of CBDT circulars as your first batch. Name your files descriptively — "Income-Tax-Act-1961-Amended-FinanceAct2024.pdf" is far more useful than "IT Act Final v3.pdf" when you are troubleshooting.
5. Enable the "File Search" toggle. Without this, the model will not perform retrieval against your uploaded documents and will fall back to its general training data — exactly what you are trying to avoid.
6. Run your validation test prompts before publishing. Ask the GPT: "What is the disallowance mechanism under Section 43B(h) for payments to MSME suppliers, and from which assessment year does it apply?" A correctly configured GPT should return the section text, confirm AY 2024-25 applicability, and not add any extraneous foreign law commentary.
7. Set visibility to "Only me" or "Only people with a link" depending on whether you want to restrict access to yourself or extend it to designated team members. Never publish a firm's knowledge-base GPT to the public GPT Store — you are handling confidential advisory material.

## Prompt Engineering Techniques for Accurate Tax Statute Queries

The quality of the question determines the quality of the answer. Most CAs who are disappointed with AI research tools are asking weak questions and receiving weak answers. A structured prompt framework changes this.

The framework I recommend is called **SCAR**: **S**tatute, **C**ontext, **A**sk, **R**estrictions. Write your statute reference first ("Under the Income Tax Act 1961"), then set the factual context ("for a resident individual with salary and rental income"), then ask your specific question, and finally add a restriction ("cite only CBDT circulars and ITAT judgments, no foreign authority").

**Example:**

Here is a direct comparison that illustrates the difference between a weak prompt and a strong one for the Section 43B(h) issue.

**Weak prompt:** "What are the rules for MSME payments under income tax?"

This returns a general summary with no section citations, often blending GST compliance obligations into the answer and sometimes referencing generic "small business payment" rules from non-Indian contexts.

**Strong prompt (SCAR framework):** "Under the Income Tax Act 1961, Section 43B(h) inserted by the Finance Act 2023: in the context of a manufacturing company (not itself an MSME) that has made payments to a registered micro-enterprise supplier outstanding as on 31 March 2024, confirm (a) the exact disallowance mechanism, (b) whether the 15-day or 45-day limit applies, and (c) cite the CBDT circular or notification, if any, that clarifies computation. Restrict your answer to Indian law only and quote the proviso text verbatim."

The strong prompt returns the exact statutory text, correctly identifies the 15-day / 45-day distinction based on written agreement status, confirms AY 2024-25 as the first year of applicability, and flags if no clarificatory circular has been loaded in the knowledge base. That is actionable research.

Always follow up a strong prompt with a challenge prompt: "Are there any conflicting High Court views or ITAT decisions in your knowledge base that reach a different conclusion?" This surfaces judicial uncertainty you need to flag to the client.

## Limitations, Guardrails, and Professional Responsibility

No matter how well you build this tool, the output remains a research draft. It is not a legal opinion, it is not advice, and it cannot replace your professional judgment. Make this non-negotiable inside the firm: every AI-generated research note must be reviewed by a qualified CA before it influences any client communication.

ICAI has not yet issued a formal standard specifically governing AI-assisted tax advisory work, but the existing framework on professional competence and due diligence applies in full. Using AI does not reduce your obligation to verify the accuracy of the advice you sign off on. If anything, it raises the bar, because clients and regulators will hold you to the standard of "what would a reasonably diligent CA have checked" — and checking AI output against the primary source is part of that standard.

Build a simple verification checklist into your workflow. Before any AI-sourced section citation leaves the firm, someone must open income-tax.gov.in or the official e-Gazette PDF and confirm the text verbatim. A five-minute cross-check is adequate for most queries. It is not optional.

Data privacy is the issue most firms are ignoring. When you upload client-specific documents — a client's ITR, AIS extract, or legal notice — to an OpenAI Custom GPT, that data leaves your system and is processed on OpenAI's servers. Unless you have a data processing agreement in place and have disclosed this to the client, you may be in breach of your confidentiality obligations. The rule is simple: your custom GPT's knowledge base should contain only public-domain statutory documents, not client data. Use the GPT for statute and circular research; use your own secure system for client file analysis.

## Maintaining and Updating Your Custom GPT as Tax Law Evolves

A custom GPT built today is already partially outdated by next February's Budget. Maintenance is not optional — it is the difference between a useful research tool and a liability trap.

1. Create a quarterly document refresh calendar with four mandatory review dates: post-Budget (February/March), post-Finance Act assent (typically April), post-monsoon CBDT notification cycle (September/October), and year-end compliance period (December/January). Mark these in the firm calendar with a designated responsible person assigned to each cycle.
2. Version-control your knowledge files using a simple naming convention such as "ITA-1961-v7-FinanceAct2024-Assented20Apr2024.pdf." When you replace a file, archive the old version in a separate folder. This gives you a clear audit trail of which version of the law was loaded in the GPT at any given time — which matters if a research output is ever questioned.
3. When you update knowledge files, delete the old file from the GPT Builder and upload the new version. Do not simply add additional files layering on top of old ones — conflicting versions of the same provision will confuse the retrieval mechanism and degrade answer quality.
4. Assign GPT maintenance to a specific person in the firm — typically a junior CA or the firm's designated technology owner. Draft a one-page maintenance SOP that covers what to check, when to check it, and how to log changes. Without formal ownership, the task will drift.
5. Monitor OpenAI's platform release notes for changes to the GPT Builder, file limits, or retrieval behaviour. OpenAI has changed file handling parameters multiple times in 2023 and 2024. A platform update that changes how file search works can silently degrade your GPT's accuracy without any visible error message.

## Your Concrete Next Step

Do not plan to build this tool. Build it this week. Pull the Income Tax Act consolidated PDF from income-tax.gov.in, download the last three years of CBDT circulars, and spend ninety minutes on the GPT Builder configuration with the system prompt and test queries described above. Run the Section 43B(h) test prompt as your baseline accuracy check. If the GPT passes that test, you have a working research assistant. If it does not, the knowledge base or system prompt needs adjustment — and you will know that before any client ever sees the output.

The firms that build this capability now will handle statutory research in minutes instead of hours. The firms that wait will still be waiting when the next Finance Act rewrites three more sections.
