## Section 143(1) vs 143(3): CA Triage Workflow Guide

Every CA has seen it — a client forwards a PDF from the Income Tax portal with a subject line that says "Notice from Income Tax Department" and asks, "What do I do now?" Your first job is not to panic alongside them. Your first job is to classify the notice correctly within five minutes of reading it, because the wrong classification leads to the wrong remedy, and the wrong remedy wastes critical deadline days.

This guide gives you a repeatable triage framework for AY 2024-25 and AY 2025-26 notices. Bookmark it. Share it with your team.

---

### 143(1) Intimation vs 143(3) Assessment: Core Legal Distinctions

A Section 143(1) intimation is issued by the Centralised Processing Centre (CPC), Bengaluru after automated processing of the filed return. It is not a scrutiny order. It reflects adjustments made by the system — arithmetic errors, incorrect claims flagged by the database, or TDS mismatches — and it carries a demand or refund figure based purely on what the CPC computed.

A Section 143(3) order is fundamentally different. It is issued by a human Assessing Officer (AO) after a full scrutiny assessment where the AO examines your client's books, financial statements, and submissions. The AO applies judgment, raises queries, considers your responses, and then passes a speaking order with reasons.

The legal finality gap is critical. If your client does nothing about a 143(1) intimation within 30 days of the date of issue, the demand becomes final and recoverable. Silence is acceptance. A 143(3) order, by contrast, carries formal appellate rights under Section 246A — the client can appeal to the Commissioner of Income Tax (Appeals) within 30 days of the order date, and the dispute continues through a defined statutory process.

### Side-by-Side Comparison: Intimation vs Scrutiny Assessment

Use this table every time you receive a notice. If a single column entry does not match what you see in the document, re-examine your classification.

| Parameter | Section 143(1) Intimation | Section 143(3) Scrutiny Assessment |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Trigger | Automated CPC processing of filed return | AO-initiated selection via CASS or manual criteria |
| Issuing authority | CPC, Bengaluru (system-generated) | Jurisdictional AO with name, ward/circle, and designation |
| Timeline for issue | Within 9 months from end of FY of filing | AO must complete within 12–18 months from end of AY |
| Nature of demand | Adjustment based on prima facie errors in return | Demand based on AO's assessment after full examination |
| Response window | 30 days from date of intimation | Variable — each hearing notice specifies its own date |
| Appellate remedy | Rectification u/s 154 or CPC grievance | Appeal to CIT(A) under Section 246A within 30 days of order |
| DIN requirement | Not applicable (CPC-generated) | Mandatory per CBDT Circular 19/2019 |

### How to Identify Which Notice Your Client Actually Received

**Client Scenario:** Your client sends you a notice received for AY 2024-25. The document header reads "Intimation under Section 143(1) of the Income-tax Act, 1961" and shows a CPC reference number in the format CPC/2425/XXXXXXXXXX. There is no AO name, no ward/circle designation, and no DIN. This is unambiguously a 143(1) intimation. Act within 30 days of the date printed on the document.

Now contrast this: another client sends a document that reads "Assessment Order under Section 143(3)" at the top, lists the AO's name and designation, cites a Ward/Circle/Range, carries a DIN in the format ITBA/AST/F/143(3)/XXXX/XXXXXXXXXX, and has a demand notice under Section 156 attached as an annexure. This is a 143(3) order, and the 30-day appeal clock under Section 246A starts from the date of that order.

For portal verification, log into the Income Tax e-Filing portal and check two separate tabs. Section 143(3) proceedings appear under **e-Proceedings** — you will see the case open with pending compliance dates. Section 143(1) intimations appear under **View Returns/Forms > Intimation u/s 143(1)**. If you cannot find a 143(3) proceeding in the e-Proceedings tab, the client almost certainly has a 143(1) intimation, not a scrutiny matter.

The Section 156 demand notice is your strongest confirmatory signal. Its presence alongside an assessment order means the AO has determined tax payable after scrutiny — this is not a CPC adjustment. Treat it accordingly.

### CA Triage Workflow: Classify, Prioritise, and Act

Run this workflow from the moment your client sends you the notice. Do not skip steps or re-sequence them — the classification in Step 2 determines every downstream action.

1. Receive the notice document from the client and immediately check the date of issue printed on the face of the document. Log this date in your practice management system before you read anything else — the deadline clock starts here, not when you first look at it.

2. Read the document header and identify the section reference. If it says "143(1)" with a CPC reference number, classify as Intimation. If it says "143(3)" with an AO name, ward, and DIN, classify as Scrutiny Assessment. If you are unsure, cross-check using the portal tabs described in the previous section.

3. Calculate the applicable deadline based on classification. For 143(1), add 30 days to the date of issue. For 143(3), check the hearing notice or order date and note both the compliance date on the notice and the 30-day appeal window under Section 246A. Set calendar reminders at Day 7, Day 15, and Day 25.

4. Generate a client document checklist tailored to the notice type. For 143(1), you typically need the ITR acknowledgement, Form 26AS, AIS/TIS, and any supporting documents for the adjustment made. For 143(3), you need books of accounts, bank statements, financial statements, and all documents relevant to the queries raised by the AO.

5. Draft the response. For a 143(1) intimation with a demand you dispute, prepare a rectification application under Section 154 with supporting evidence. For a 143(3) matter, prepare a detailed written submission addressing each query or ground raised in the order or notice.

6. Submit the response through the correct portal pathway, download and preserve the acknowledgement, and update your practice management system with the submission date and acknowledgement number. Never rely on browser history as proof of submission.

7. If the 143(1) demand persists after your rectification application and is not corrected within the expected processing timeline, escalate to a CPC grievance on the portal. If a 143(3) order remains adverse after all submissions, initiate the appeal under Section 246A without waiting further — time does not pause for negotiations.

### Deadline Map and Penalty Exposure for Missed Timelines

The 30-day window on a 143(1) intimation is tighter than most CAs treat it. Once it lapses without a response or rectification application, the demand becomes final and recoverable under Section 220. The department can then initiate recovery proceedings, attach bank accounts, or adjust against pending refunds — all without further notice.

On the 143(3) side, the AO must complete the assessment within 12 months from the end of the AY (extendable to 18 months in cases involving international transactions or certain specified domestic transactions). But within that window, each hearing notice sets its own compliance date. Failing to respond to a hearing notice without cause gives the AO grounds to proceed ex-parte — that means the AO passes the order without your client's submissions, almost always resulting in a higher demand.

Interest under Section 220(2) runs at 1% per month on the unpaid demand amount from the date it becomes due. For a demand of ₹10 lakhs, that is ₹10,000 every month the client delays. Put this number in front of your client in the first conversation — it converts abstract compliance urgency into a concrete rupee cost that drives action.

### Common CA Errors and How to Avoid Them

The most expensive mistake is treating a 143(1) demand as low priority because it looks automated. Some CAs tell clients, "It is just a CPC notice, we will sort it out." Thirty days later, the demand is final. The fix is procedural discipline: every 143(1) intimation with a demand goes into your workflow tracker on the day it is received, with a hard deadline 25 days from issue.

Filing a rectification under Section 154 against a 143(3) order is the wrong remedy and a waste of your client's time. Section 154 applies to rectification of apparent mistakes from the record — it is not designed to contest an AO's substantive assessment findings. When a 143(3) order is wrong, the remedy is an appeal under Section 246A to CIT(A). Using 154 in this context does not stop the appeal clock from running, so you could find yourself out of time for the correct remedy while waiting for a 154 response that was never going to resolve the dispute.

Always verify the DIN on any 143(3) document before treating it as a valid notice. CBDT Circular 19/2019 mandates that all income tax orders, notices, summons, and letters must carry a computer-generated DIN. A 143(3) notice or order without a DIN is invalid and must be treated as if it was never issued — the Supreme Court and multiple High Courts have confirmed this position. Check the DIN on the ITBA portal before drafting any response.

### Using Practice Automation Tools to Manage Notice Triage at Scale

Manual tracking across a portfolio of 200 or 500 clients is where deadline risk concentrates. A single missed entry in a spreadsheet can turn a correctable 143(1) error into a final demand — and the client will rightly hold the CA responsible.

Practice automation platforms like Accountic can ingest notices directly from the Income Tax portal, classify them as 143(1) or 143(3) based on document metadata, and trigger separate countdown timers with the correct response windows. The classification happens at ingestion — your staff do not make the call manually. This eliminates the single largest source of error in notice management.

Pre-configured client communication templates for intimation responses versus scrutiny proceedings save meaningful time. Drafting a covering letter, a document request email to the client, and a submission cover sheet for a 143(1) matter takes 15–20 minutes from scratch. With a configured template, it takes under five minutes. Across 50 notices in a busy March-to-September season, that is hours returned to chargeable work.

Dashboard-level triage views are the operational game-changer for CA principals. Instead of asking each team member for a status update, you open one screen, filter by days-to-deadline, and immediately see which 143(1) or 143(3) matters need your personal attention today. Nothing falls through because it was "with the junior" and nobody followed up.
