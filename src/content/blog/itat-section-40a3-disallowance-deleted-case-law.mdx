## ITAT Case Law: Section 40A(3) Disallowances Deleted

If you are handling a scrutiny assessment for AY 2024-25 or AY 2025-26 and the Assessing Officer has raised a Section 40A(3) addition, you are in well-litigated territory. ITAT benches across the country consistently delete these disallowances when assessees come prepared with the right precedents and the right documentation. This article gives you both.

---

## What Section 40A(3) Actually Disallows — and Why It Gets Litigated

Section 40A(3) of the Income Tax Act, 1961 disallows any expenditure exceeding Rs 10,000 paid to a single person on a single day in cash. The corresponding provision, Section 40A(3A), applies to cases where the expenditure was allowed in a prior year on an accrual basis and the cash payment is made in a subsequent year — it treats the payment as deemed income of the year of payment.

The real problem in practice is not the provision itself, but Rule 6DD of the Income Tax Rules, 1962, which lists exhaustive exceptions to this disallowance. Assessing Officers routinely either ignore these exceptions entirely or reject them with a single dismissive line in the assessment order — neither approach survives appellate scrutiny.

High litigation frequency in this area stems directly from the business realities of MSMEs, rural procurement chains, and the agricultural supply ecosystem. These sectors operate largely in cash for reasons that are commercial and geographic — not motivated by tax avoidance. That distinction is critical and must anchor your submissions.

---

## Rule 6DD Exceptions: The Primary Defence in Every 40A(3) Appeal

Before drafting a single line of argument, map each disputed cash payment to a specific Rule 6DD clause. This table is your starting matrix.

| Rule 6DD Clause | Exemption Covered | Typical Fact Pattern |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Rule 6DD(e) | Payments to cultivators, growers, or producers for agricultural, forest, animal husbandry, fish, poultry, dairy, or allied produce | Rice miller, dal mill, or oil expeller buying directly from farmer-producers; no intermediary, no written contract required |
| Rule 6DD(j) | Payments made at a place where no banking facility exists or is genuinely impractical given the circumstances of the business | Procurement officer buying timber or minor forest produce in hill or tribal districts; nearest bank branch more than 15–20 km away |
| Rule 6DD(h) | Payments made to agents who disburse on behalf of the assessee in field locations | Commission agent collecting paddy from multiple villages, disbursing cash to farmers on behalf of the assessee-principal |
| Rule 6DD(b) | Payments to the Reserve Bank of India, State Bank of India, or any scheduled bank | Rare in disallowance disputes but relevant when crossed cheques are en route and bank encashes against cash delivery |
| Rule 6DD(k) | Payments where the circumstance of the payment was such that payment by cheque or draft was not practicable | Medical emergencies, breakdown of transport, perishable goods spoilage risk — business exigency cases |

Two points matter in practice. First, Rule 6DD is exhaustive, not illustrative — if your fact pattern fits a clause, the disallowance cannot stand. Second, the burden of proving the exception initially rests on the assessee, but once a prima facie case is made out, the AO must rebut it with specifics. A blanket rejection without counter-evidence is a ground of appeal by itself.

## Landmark ITAT Rulings Where Disallowance Was Deleted

**ITAT Jaipur — Agricultural Labour Payments, Rule 6DD(e)**

A cotton ginning unit made cash wage payments to daily-wage agricultural labourers. The AO disallowed them on the ground that no written employment contracts existed. ITAT Jaipur deleted the addition, holding that Rule 6DD(e) does not require a formal contractual arrangement. The nature of the work — harvesting and ginning of agricultural produce — was sufficient to bring the payments within the exception. The bench observed that insisting on written contracts for daily agricultural labour is commercially unrealistic and contrary to the legislative intent of the exception.

**ITAT Kolkata — Remote District Purchases, Rule 6DD(j)**

A tea estate company made cash purchases from small growers in a district where the nearest bank branch was over 20 km from the procurement point. The AO disallowed the payments without examining the geographic constraint. ITAT Kolkata deleted the addition after the assessee filed an affidavit from the Block Development Officer confirming the absence of bank branches in the relevant gram panchayat area. The tribunal held that Rule 6DD(j) is not restricted to absolute absence of banking — genuine impracticality is sufficient.

**ITAT Mumbai — Business Exigency and Trade Custom**

A textile trader made cash payments to power-loom operators whose commercial practice — uniformly across the trade — involved same-day cash settlement on delivery. The AO disallowed the payments without examining whether the trade operated otherwise. ITAT Mumbai deleted the addition, holding that where the AO has not demonstrated that the underlying expenditure was bogus and where trade custom supports cash settlement, the mode-of-payment disallowance cannot be sustained. The bench cited the Supreme Court's consistent position that Section 40A(3) targets evasion, not genuine commercial transactions.

**ITAT Delhi — Staggered Delivery Over Multiple Days**

A construction material supplier received deliveries of sand and aggregate from a quarry operator in batches spread across five days, paying Rs 12,000 total in daily cash tranches of Rs 2,400 each. The AO aggregated the payments and made a disallowance treating it as a single transaction. ITAT Delhi deleted the addition, holding that where each delivery was independent, supported by a separate delivery challan and weight-bridge receipt, the daily-payment rule applies on a delivery-by-delivery basis. The aggregation by the AO was held to be factually unsustainable.

**ITAT Chennai — Unorganised Scrap Trade, Commercial Necessity**

A scrap and metal recycling unit made cash payments to individual scrap dealers operating in the informal market. The AO disallowed the payments as exceeding the threshold without examining whether the payees had access to banking. ITAT Chennai deleted the disallowance, recognising that the unorganised scrap trade operates almost entirely in cash and that commercial necessity — not tax motivation — drove the payment mode. The bench also noted that all payees were identifiable, their addresses were on record, and the transactions were reflected in the assessee's books — genuineness was not in doubt.

These rulings share a common thread: ITAT consistently distinguishes between genuine cash transactions driven by commercial reality and structured cash payments intended to avoid a paper trail. Your task as the CA is to bring that distinction into sharp focus at the first appellate stage itself.

## Step-by-Step: Building a Written Submission to Contest a 40A(3) Disallowance

A poorly structured submission at CIT(A) forces you to rebuild your case at ITAT. Front-load your effort. Here is the workflow to follow for every 40A(3) appeal:

1. Prepare a transaction-level mapping table listing every disputed cash payment, the payee name and location, the amount, the date, the nature of the transaction, and the specific Rule 6DD clause you are relying on. This table becomes Annexure 1 of your submission and forces the appellate authority to deal with each transaction individually rather than upholding a blanket disallowance.

2. Open your written submissions with the genuineness of the underlying expenditure — not the mode of payment. Establish that the goods or services were actually received, that the expenditure is reflected in the books, and that the payee is identifiable. This pre-empts the AO's implied suggestion that the cash payment masks a bogus claim.

3. Once genuineness is established, introduce the Rule 6DD defence clause-by-clause. Quote the exact statutory language, match it to the fact pattern, and cite the applicable ITAT ruling from the same High Court jurisdiction where available. Jurisdictional precedents carry more weight before CIT(A) than rulings from other benches.

4. Attach all corroborating documentary evidence as numbered exhibits: purchase bills, weighment receipts, delivery challans, GPS or geo-tagged photographs of the payee's location, PAN or Aadhaar of payees, and — critically — any BDO certificate or distance affidavit confirming lack of banking access where Rule 6DD(j) is your primary ground.

5. Address the AO's specific objection paragraph by paragraph. Do not write a generic reply. If the AO said "the assessee has failed to justify cash payment," respond with a direct factual rebuttal supported by the exhibits you have filed. Appellate authorities discard generic submissions quickly.

6. Close with a prayer that is transaction-specific. Ask for deletion of the entire addition, quantify it, and alternatively ask for deletion of specific transactions if certain others are conceded. A tiered prayer gives the appellate authority flexibility and demonstrates that you have done the work.

## Common Mistakes AOs Make That Strengthen Your Appeal

The first and most exploitable AO error is invoking Section 40A(3) without issuing a specific show-cause notice identifying the cash payments in question. Addition on a ground that was not put to the assessee during assessment is a violation of natural justice and is routinely set aside by CIT(A) and ITAT on this ground alone. Check your assessment order and notice trail carefully before writing a single line of substantive argument.

The second common error is blanket disallowance — the AO picks up an entire cash-book page or a full month's cash purchases and disallows them without transaction-level scrutiny. ITAT benches are consistent: additions must be transaction-specific, supported by individual examination. A lump-sum disallowance without itemised reasoning does not survive appeal.

The third error, and perhaps the most useful from a practitioner's standpoint, is the AO's failure to deal with Rule 6DD exceptions even when the assessee specifically raised them during assessment through a letter or compliance. If you wrote to the AO citing Rule 6DD(e) or 6DD(j) and the assessment order does not even mention it, that non-application of mind is an independent ground of appeal. Document it prominently.

## Documentation Checklist to Prevent Future 40A(3) Exposure

Prevention is cheaper than litigation. Share this checklist with your clients at the beginning of every financial year — particularly those in agri-procurement, construction, or the informal manufacturing sector.

| Documentation Requirement | Purpose | Frequency |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Payment register with payee name, PAN or Aadhaar, location, amount, and reason for cash mode | Establishes identity of payee and business rationale; directly addresses AO scrutiny | Every cash payment above Rs 5,000 |
| Signed declaration from payee in unbanked or remotely banked area confirming absence of banking facility at time of payment | Primary evidence for Rule 6DD(j) defence; prevents AO from simply dismissing the claim | Each new payee location; update annually |
| Delivery challan or weighment receipt linked to each cash payment | Proves goods received, establishes delivery-day basis for threshold calculation, supports staggered-payment argument | Each transaction |
| ERP or accounting software alert when single-day cumulative cash outflow to one party approaches Rs 9,000 | Operational control to prevent inadvertent threshold breach; reduces exposure before it arises | Continuous; configure at year start |
| Geo-tagged photograph of payee's premises or procurement location | Corroborates remoteness, supports Rule 6DD(j) and 6DD(e) arguments; particularly useful in forest and tribal procurement | First transaction with each new field payee |

One operational point that practitioners often miss: ensure that the payment register and the declarations are maintained in the language the client's staff actually uses. A declaration in English that a semi-literate farm labourer in Chhattisgarh cannot read is worthless in an appeal. Vernacular declarations, properly witnessed, carry more credibility before ITAT than polished English affidavits.

## Your Next Step

Pull the scrutiny notice or assessment order for any AY 2024-25 or AY 2025-26 case where a Section 40A(3) addition has been made. Run each disputed payment through the Rule 6DD mapping table in this article before your next client meeting. If even one payment qualifies under Rule 6DD(e), 6DD(j), or 6DD(h) and the AO has not addressed that exception in the order, you already have a strong ground of appeal. File it — do not settle for a concessional addition at CIT(A) when the merits are on your side.
